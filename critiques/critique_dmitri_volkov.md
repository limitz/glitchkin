# CRITIQUE — "Luma & the Glitchkin" Character Design
## Reviewer: Dmitri Volkov, Character Design Lead
## Date: 2026-03-29
## Round: Cycle 01

---

> *I have read the style guide (character design sections), luma.md, cosmo.md, byte.md, and character_lineup.md in full. My critique addresses the documented design intent and construction specifications. I am evaluating these as design documents intended to guide production — not finished frames. That said, the problems I identify here will propagate directly into production if not addressed.*

---

## Overall Assessment

This is a first pass that shows genuine thinking about character design fundamentals. The shape language framework is coherent, the trio's color temperature distribution is strategically sound, and the ensemble interdependency has been worked out with care. There are clear influences (the style guide correctly names *Hilda*, *The Owl House*, *Kipo*) and the team has absorbed those influences rather than merely copying them.

None of that is enough.

What I see are three documents that have been written with the confidence of finished designs when what they actually contain is well-organized intent. The silhouette claims are overconfident. Byte's design has a fundamental legibility crisis at production scale that has been documented but not solved. Cosmo's shape language is the weakest in the trio — not because rectangles are wrong, but because this document has not earned the rectangle claim with enough geometric specificity. And Luma, who is the strongest of the three, has a pixel hoodie pattern that will become a production liability the moment the show runs at broadcast resolution.

There is a good show underneath this first pass. It is not visible yet.

## Luma — Critique

### Silhouette Clarity
The silhouette claim is largely justified. A near-circular head topped by a large asymmetric hair cloud above a wide rounded torso with short stubby legs and oversized feet — this reads. At thumbnail size, the hair mass is the dominant signature, and that mass is distinctive enough to survive reduction. The document correctly notes that orange hoodie against near-black hair creates maximum contrast, and this is the right structural decision.

**However:** The document claims the silhouette should read as "energetic young girl" before color is applied, and I would argue it reads closer to "round thing with large hair." The body below the neck contributes almost nothing to the silhouette's personality. The rounded rectangle torso at only 1.1x head width is so close to the head width that in silhouette, the head and body may merge into a single blob. This is a proportional risk that needs to be tested, not assumed. The spec does not mention the width of the kangaroo pocket hem — if that line is too faint or too integrated into the torso silhouette, the mid-body loses all articulation. No field test of the actual silhouette has been documented.

### Shape Language Consistency
Circle language is correctly applied and thoroughly committed to. Every element curves, every corner softens. This is disciplined work and it is right for the character concept. The tension between the "safe" rounded edges and the "kinetic" proportions is well-conceived and well-executed in the document spec.

The one inconsistency: the pants are described as "warm dark indigo" — a fine color choice — but the pants silhouette contribution is barely discussed. Short, non-tapering legs that end in oversized oval feet. In silhouette, the leg region below the hoodie hem is thin indigo columns into white-ish shoes. This may read as nothing. The feet read as large, but is large-feet a silhouette signature when her head already has the giant hair cloud dominating? Hierarchy problem — two attention anchors competing, and only one (the hair) is likely to win.

### Appeal
High. The character concept is genuinely appealing — a kid who cannot hurt you even at full speed. The color choices are emotionally correct: orange is warm, forward, optimistic. The design rationale calling her "a bowling ball that talks" is the clearest articulation in any of the three documents of what the design is actually trying to do. That specificity is good.

The emotional hair reactivity system is an excellent production idea, well-specified. Excited hair lifts and multiplies flyaways. Sad hair droops and compresses. This is the kind of secondary animation design that makes a character feel alive without requiring expensive additional animation. Credit where it's due: this is thoughtful.

**Problem area:** The pixel hoodie pattern. I understand its narrative purpose — digital world intruding into warm world, evidence of Luma's entanglement, visual hook. The concept is correct. The execution as documented is a production nightmare. A multi-color grid of small squares described at approximately 3-4px at standard rig size, on a character who is going to appear in wide shots, medium shots, and tiny thumbnail sizes across multiple media. The document acknowledges this with a fallback: "the pattern becomes a simple abstract texture that hints at the grid — but never disappears entirely." This fallback is not designed. It is described. There is no specification for what the simplified texture looks like, at what scale the transition occurs, how animators know which version to use, or how consistency is maintained across the production. This is a significant oversight. If the pattern can't be specified in a simplified form, it will be inconsistent across the production. Inconsistency at a character's signature element is a fundamental production failure.

### Expressiveness
The six-expression sheet is well-executed in document form. The breakdown of facial muscle behavior is specific and correct — this designer knows how expressions work mechanically. The "Reckless Excitement" expression is the strongest: eyes at maximum aperture, floating eyebrows, ear-to-ear grin, elevated hair. This reads. The "Guilty Sheepishness" expression is the most original — specifically, the detail that the grin is "too wide and too symmetrical to be genuine" is the kind of insight that separates a character designer from a shape decorator.

Minor: the forced grin of guilt reads identically to certain versions of Cosmo's forced-grin expression. Two different characters having overlapping expression shapes at the same emotional beat (guilt, embarrassment) is a continuity problem in ensemble storytelling. The audience should be able to read emotional register from character alone. If Luma's guilty grin and Cosmo's mortified grin look similar, that distinction collapses.

### Distinctiveness
Very strong. At any thumbnail size, Luma would not be confused for any other character in this or most other shows currently in production. The combination of circular head, massive asymmetric hair cloud, and orange hoodie is distinctive. The cyan laces as the one intrusive glitch detail in her analog wardrobe is a quiet design touch that rewards close attention.

### Production Durability
The main risk is the pixel pattern, already noted. Secondary risk: the document specifies "4-6 large spiral-curl indicators" within the hair silhouette in standard rendering. In production animation, this number needs to be locked — not a range. Animators working at speed will either over-render or under-render the hair consistently if the spec says 4 to 6. Pick a number. 5. Lock it. Document the exceptions.

## Cosmo — Critique

### Silhouette Clarity
This is the weakest silhouette of the three. The claim that "the silhouette should read as 'careful, organized, slightly bookish young person'" is aspirational, not descriptive. Let me apply the squint test to what is actually specified.

In silhouette: a tall rounded rectangle head (only marginally taller than wide — 1.15:1 ratio), a flat hair cap that adds 0.05 heads of height (nearly invisible), a slightly narrower torso, longer legs. The only protrusion — the only truly unique silhouette element — is the glasses. Two circular frames protruding from the face plane. This IS distinctive. But here is the problem: glasses are the thinnest part of a character at silhouette scale. The frames are described as 0.025x head width thick. That is a very thin line. At thumbnail size, those frames may disappear entirely, leaving a silhouette that looks like a slightly tall rounded-rectangle figure — indistinguishable from a hundred other cartoon sidekick designs.

The glasses are the character. But the glasses may not survive the squint test. This needs to be tested explicitly, and the document does not document this test.

The cowlick as a "tiny bump at the crown" is nearly worthless as a silhouette identifier. It is too small to read past medium distance.

**Verdict on silhouette:** Cosmo's design works at medium shot and closer. In long shot or thumbnail, he is the least distinctive of the three main characters. This is a significant structural problem for a character who will frequently be in supporting-position in wide shots.

### Shape Language Consistency
The rectangle language is stated but not fully committed to. A "tall rounded rectangle with heavily rounded corners — like a stack of two circles but fused" is not a rectangle anymore. It is an oblong. The distinction matters: if the corners are "heavily rounded," the shape reads as oval-adjacent, not rectangular. Where is the actual geometric tension between his rectangles and Luma's circles? If his head is reading as oval, they are both in the same shape family.

This is fixable. The solution is to be more disciplined about the degree of corner rounding on Cosmo's head — keep it noticeably less rounded than Luma's circle. A 15-20% corner radius reads as rectangular. A 40-50% corner radius reads as oval. The document needs to specify this quantitatively, not qualitatively.

The "organized, stable, cerebral" personality-through-geometry argument only holds if the rectangularity is visible. If it softens into oval-territory, the shape language collapses.

### Appeal
Cosmo is the most conceptually interesting character in the trio. The "deadpan to camera" comedic beat is correctly identified as his signature. The detail about his glasses becoming more level when he smiles — because his cheeks press them up — is the kind of physical specificity that makes a character feel like a real entity rather than a collection of traits. Good work.

The asymmetric eyebrow as his primary expressive tell is correct and is a reliable tool in the animation vocabulary.

**Problem:** Cosmo's appeal depends almost entirely on expression and performance. His static design — the thing that has to work in a promo image, a merchandise sheet, a logo — is the weakest of the three. Orange hoodie, giant hair, pixel pattern: Luma pops at zero frames. Giant glowing cyan cube floating in space: Byte pops at zero frames. Slightly tall kid with glasses: Cosmo does not pop. He requires context or expression to land. This is the fundamental limitation of the "restrained sidekick" archetype, and this document has not solved it. The glasses need to work harder in still contexts. Consider whether the glasses need a stronger visual treatment — perhaps a glare or light-catch that is always present as a design element, not just in close-up.

### Expressiveness
The expression sheet is where this design shines. The "Deadpan/I Told You So" expression is perfectly constructed and the fourth-wall break is correctly identified as a recurring comedic anchor. "Nervous Courage" is the emotional peak of the character — the contradiction between wide-open fear-eyes and pulled-down determination brows is specific and right.

The "Genuine Delight" expression being described as a moment where he "forgets to be restrained" is the correct escalation structure. When his biggest smile is still described as smaller than Luma's baseline excitement, the range between his neutral and his maximum is well-calibrated.

**Problem:** The range between "Deadpan" and "Anxious Overthinking" is too small. Both expressions feature relatively controlled faces with small tells. Without a strong neutral baseline established in animation, these two expressions — which represent very different emotional states — may not be sufficiently readable at broadcast size. The document could address this more directly in terms of what the KEY differentiators are at reduced scale.

### Distinctiveness
In the ensemble: strong. Against the broader animation landscape: weak. Bookish kids with large glasses are common. The cowlick is generic. The striped shirt is common. His distinctiveness lives in his behavioral system (the tell-by-glasses, the fourth-wall look, the asymmetric brow) — not in his static visual design. This is a real concern for merchandise, promotional art, and any still context where performance is absent.

The one genuinely distinctive static element is the perpetually crooked glasses at a specified consistent angle. This should be leaned into more heavily — make it more extreme, more consistently readable. If the glasses tilt 3-5 degrees in neutral and only reach 6-7 degrees in maximum embarrassment, the range is too compressed. Consider expanding the neutral tilt to 5-7 degrees so the maximum becomes 10-12 degrees — bigger comedy range, more distinctive static image.

### Production Durability
The striped shirt will be a production nuisance. Horizontal stripes on a cylindrical body form require careful wrap-around handling in every angle. The document addresses this with flat-graphic stripe logic, but "stripes do NOT converge at the center or follow the body's curvature — they are flat graphic lines" will produce a visually incorrect result at sharp 3/4 and near-profile angles if applied rigidly. This either needs a specific callout for how stripes are handled near the side edges in 3/4 view, or simpler: reduce the stripes to fewer, bolder bands that survive rotation more cleanly.

The shadow color specification for the striped shirt — two separate shadow tones for two stripe colors, two highlight tones — is correct in theory and will be a production headache in practice. Every lighting revision doubles the color correction work. This needs a production-specific note about which shots can simplify to a single shadow tone applied uniformly.

## Byte — Critique

### Silhouette Clarity
Byte has the strongest silhouette of the three, and this is entirely correct given his design constraints. A chamfered cube with angular protrusions, a specific spike at the top, stubby triangular limbs extending in four directions, and a floating gap beneath — this reads at any size. The pixel confetti beneath him adds a signature that is unique to this character. At the scale where he appears on Luma's shoulder in a medium shot — approximately 40-60px as the document correctly notes — the cube reads, the spike reads, the limbs read. This passes the squint test.

The asymmetry of his body — clean left side, notched right side — is a strong design decision that is more legible in mid-shot and closer. In long shot, this asymmetry will vanish, and that is acceptable. The spike and cube with limb-protrusions is sufficient for long-shot identification.

**However:** The document makes an enormous claim: "every key design element must survive that scale" of 40-60px. Let me apply actual pressure to that claim.

At 40-60px total height for Byte, his face — which occupies 70% of the front cube face — is approximately 28-42px tall. Within that space, his two eyes must be distinguishable, and critically, his cracked eye must be displaying a readable pixel symbol. A pixel-art symbol at 0.25 units x 0.20 units, scaled to a face that is 28-42px tall in a standard broadcast environment, is working at approximately 7-10px per symbol width. The heart symbol, the warning triangle, the question mark — these are going to be noise at that scale.

The document does not solve this problem. It identifies it, then asserts the design handles it. That assertion is not supported by any scale-testing specification. The pixel-eye system is the character's primary emotional expression mechanism and it may be functionally unreadable in the single most common shot type in the show (medium shots with the trio together, Byte on shoulder).

This is a critical design failure that needs immediate attention.

### Shape Language Consistency
The chamfered cube with triangular limbs and geometric corruption is correctly in the triangle/jagged-polygon family specified by the style guide. The rationale — "triangles convey danger, sharpness, and unpredictability; at 6 inches tall, danger becomes mischief" — is intelligent design thinking. The size-personality inversion is one of the oldest tricks in cartoon character design and it works here.

The corruption asymmetry (cleaner left side, damaged right side with notches) creates a directional read that is genuinely unusual. Most cartoon characters are roughly bilaterally symmetrical. Byte's deliberate structural asymmetry encodes his history into his geometry. This is excellent.

The mouth as a "command line interface" gap is the most original design idea in any of these three documents. The distinction between an organic mouth that deforms like flesh and a digital gap that opens in hard increments is a meaningful design choice that should be reinforced in animation notes.

### Appeal
Byte is the most conceptually original character and the hardest to make appealing. The document is aware of this tension and attempts to navigate it: "not cute-ified — genuinely strange, but strange in a way we learn to love." The pixel-eye system is the primary appeal mechanism — it is the window to his interior. A blank Void Black cracked eye reading as "somehow MORE expressive than any symbol" is a smart observation about negative-space expression.

The heart symbol appearing briefly and then being switched off is the single most appealing beat in the entire design system. It is the correct comedic peak for the character. It should be explicitly flagged as a "signature moment" in the design document with a note that it should never be overused — it works because of rarity.

**Problem:** Byte's appeal in still contexts depends almost entirely on pose and pixel-eye state. The cube body reading as "grumpy neutral" before any eye state is established is a design gap. If the cracked eye display is illegible in wide shots, Byte reads as a floating cyan box with an expressive normal eye and an unreadable dark rectangle. This is not a character — it is an object with one eye. The design needs a fallback appeal mechanism that works without the pixel-eye system functioning. The normal eye alone needs to carry more expressive weight than currently specified.

### Expressiveness
The dual-eye system is original and potentially very rich — assuming the pixel-eye reads at scale. The normal eye behaves like a standard cartoon eye; the cracked eye displays symbols. The asymmetric expressiveness between the two eyes creates a character who seems to simultaneously present a controlled exterior (normal eye behavior) and an involuntary emotional readout (pixel-eye display). This is personality-through-design at a sophisticated level.

The pixel-eye symbol set is well-chosen. The flat horizontal line as "at rest" is correctly minimal. The exclamation for anger, question mark for confusion, heart for accidental affection — these are legible at standard sizes. The rotating loading dots as "processing" is a clever system beat.

**Problem:** The limb expression vocabulary is underdeveloped relative to the facial system. "Arms pinned to sides = closed, defensive, grumpy / arms raised and pointing = excited, alarmed" is described in four bullet points and nothing more. At the scale Byte appears on-screen, limb angle may be the most reliably readable body-language system he has. This needs the same rigor applied to the expression sheet: six to eight key body positions with limb angles specified, similar to the full expression documentation given to Luma and Cosmo.

### Distinctiveness
At the ensemble level: maximum. Nothing in the trio looks remotely like Byte. Nothing in the current animated landscape looks particularly like Byte — a chamfered cube Glitchkin with a dual-eye asymmetric expression system and a floating confetti locomotion mechanism. This is the most original character design in the project.

At the solo level: strong, as long as the pixel-eye is legible. Without it, medium-strong.

### Production Durability
The highest-risk character in the production. Every shot with Byte requires:
1. Floating confetti particles beneath him — specified as 8-12 squares at all times. This is a persistent animation overhead on every shot.
2. Pixel-eye symbol selection and display — requires a creative decision on every beat.
3. Jitter/glitch line treatment on his silhouette — described as "a slight zigzag or dual-offset line." This needs a specific technical spec: is it a separate overlay layer? A filter applied in compositing? A hand-drawn treatment? Without a technical production decision, this will be inconsistent across animators.
4. The body's structural asymmetry must be maintained in every rotational position — the notched right side must be the right side regardless of camera angle. This requires rigorous model sheet turnarounds that are NOT in this document yet.

The production cost-per-frame for Byte is the highest of the three. This should be flagged explicitly in the production plan, because it will affect how many scenes he can appear in, at what shot scale, and with what level of animation complexity.

## Ensemble Dynamics — Critique

### Silhouette Contrast
The trio silhouette claim in character_lineup.md — "three distinct entities readable even at thumbnail size" — is partially true. Luma and Byte are both strong at thumbnail. Cosmo is the weak link, as documented in his individual critique. The claim that "these three silhouettes do not fail" is premature. The documents acknowledge this is a first pass; the silhouette test should be an explicit, documented test with actual output, not an assertion.

### Shape Hierarchy in Frame
The observation that Byte reads first (jagged shapes draw the eye), Luma resolves second (circles rest the eye), and Cosmo grounds the composition (rectangles recede) is functionally correct as a compositional hierarchy and represents genuine understanding of how shape language operates in a shared frame. This is the document's strongest analytical section.

### Color Temperature Architecture
The warm/cool/electric distribution is the strongest strategic decision in the entire design system. Luma integrates in warm environments, Byte integrates in glitch environments, Cosmo belongs in neither — this is not just color design, it is character positioning. The insight about Cosmo's "neutrality" in color encoding his "everyman thrust into chaos" archetype is one of the few moments in these documents where design and story are genuinely unified at the same level.

The cyan thread connecting Luma (hoodie pixels, laces) and Byte (body color) is smart. It is the kind of quiet system that rewards attentive viewers. It has not been over-explained, which is also correct.

**Problem:** The color temperature distribution has no formal specification for how it degrades in extreme lighting conditions. In "Full Glitch Chaos" palette mode (as described in the style guide), Byte integrates into the environment and Luma pops. What happens to Cosmo's sage green and cerulean blue under heavy acid-green/hot-magenta domination? If his colors desaturate or shift toward the glitch palette, his "neutral" position in the color system may break. The lineup document does not address this.

### The "Tell" System
The three-character tell system — Luma's hair, Cosmo's glasses, Byte's pixel-eye — is the best ensemble-design idea in the project. Having three different types of tell (physical/involuntary, mechanical/external, technological/internal) that each communicate emotional subtext independently of dialogue is sophisticated and specific to this show's premise. If executed correctly in production, this becomes the show's signature visual language.

No critique here. This is the right call, made well.

### Ensemble Interaction Poses
The three described trio configurations (calm, high-stakes, quiet) in character_lineup.md are well-conceived as emotional staging guides. The "quiet three-shot" being described as rare and earned is correct instinct. The detail that Byte's floating gap reduces in quiet moments — "as if he's landed, as if he doesn't feel the need to be ready to flee" — is beautiful character-through-design. It costs nothing in production (it is a minor positional adjustment) and communicates volumes about character growth.

## Critical Issues (Must Fix)

**1. Byte's pixel-eye legibility at shoulder scale — PRIORITY ONE.**
The cracked eye symbol system is the character's primary emotional expression mechanism. At the documented standard shot scale (40-60px total character height), the pixel-art symbols — specified at 0.25x0.20 units within a face that occupies 70% of a cube — will render at approximately 7-10px effective display area per symbol. This is below reliable legibility for complex symbols (heart, warning triangle, question mark). The design must specify: a) what symbols are simplified or replaced at reduced scales, b) whether the pixel-eye is a supplementary system rather than primary system in wide shots, c) how the normal eye picks up expressive slack when the cracked eye is not legible. Without this, Byte's expressiveness collapses in the show's most common shot type.

**2. Luma's pixel hoodie pattern — simplified version required.**
The pattern is the character's visual hook. It also has no simplified-version specification. Production needs a documented tier system: FULL pattern (close-up, character featured), SIMPLIFIED pattern (medium shot, readable grid with color), ABSTRACT pattern (wide shot, color zone only). Each tier needs hex colors and grid density specified. Without this, the pattern will be rendered inconsistently across the production by different artists and across different shot scales.

**3. Cosmo's silhouette at long shot.**
In shots where the trio is at 15% of frame height or smaller, Cosmo's glasses are likely to disappear. He becomes an indistinct tall figure. A specific solution must be designed: either increase the glasses frame thickness specification (from 0.025x to 0.04x or greater), or add a permanent secondary silhouette element visible at long shot. The glasses glare (Static White crescent) may help if it is specified as sufficiently prominent at reduced scale.

**4. Byte's limb expression vocabulary.**
The current limb expression documentation is insufficient. Four bullet points of rough descriptions. A character whose body is a non-deforming cube is entirely dependent on limb angle and energy for body-language communication. This requires the same rigor as the facial expression sheets — at minimum six key limb configurations with angles specified, similar to how the expression sheets handle facial position.

**5. Byte's production overhead must be formally acknowledged.**
The floating confetti (8-12 particles per frame), jitter-line treatment (no technical spec), and pixel-eye animation (per-beat creative decision) make Byte the most expensive character per frame in the production. The character design document must include a production-cost note flagging which of these effects can be simplified in crowd shots, background appearances, and standard action coverage. Without this, the production will either overspend on Byte or under-render him inconsistently.

## Recommendations

**For Luma:**
- Document the pixel hoodie pattern simplification tiers explicitly. This cannot wait for production to solve ad hoc.
- Lock the hair curl indicator count to 5 (not a range of 4-6). Add a maximum flyaway count per emotional state.
- Test the front-facing silhouette: confirm that the torso reads as distinctly separate from the head when rendered as a solid black shape. If it merges, increase the shoulder-to-waist width differential.

**For Cosmo:**
- Specify the corner radius of his head rectangle quantitatively. This is the foundation of his shape language and "heavily rounded" is not a production spec.
- Increase the glasses frame thickness specification from 0.025x to at least 0.035x head width. They need to survive reduction.
- Increase the neutral glasses tilt from 3-4 degrees to at least 6 degrees. More distinctive static image, larger comedy range for escalated states.
- Consider adding one static design element that reads at long shot other than glasses. A consistent prop (the notebook could be held, not just a prop in action poses), a collar or shirt detail that creates a silhouette break at the neck region, or expanding the cowlick from a "tiny bump" to a more prominent curl that adds to silhouette height.
- Resolve the overlap between Luma's Guilty Sheepishness grin and any of Cosmo's forced-grin expressions. These need to be demonstrably different in execution.

**For Byte:**
- Complete the limb expression documentation. Six to eight key configurations, angles specified.
- Specify the pixel-eye symbol scale thresholds: at what character height (in pixels at broadcast resolution) does the symbol system get simplified or replaced with full-eye expression on the normal eye?
- Commission (or specify) a formal technical approach for the jitter-line silhouette treatment. Is it hand-drawn, procedural, or composited? This needs a production decision, not a description.
- Complete turnaround documentation. The asymmetric damage detail (notched right side, spike top) requires at minimum front, 3/4, profile, and back views with the asymmetry explicitly marked.
- The "heart briefly displayed then switched off" beat needs to be flagged as a "use maximum twice per episode" beat in the design notes. Its power is in scarcity.

**For the Ensemble:**
- Document how the color temperature system handles "Full Glitch Chaos" palette mode — specifically Cosmo's behavior under extreme glitch-palette domination.
- Add a group silhouette test page to the lineup document. Render all three as solid shapes at three scales (standard, 15% frame height, thumbnail) and confirm readability. Document the results. If Cosmo fails at reduced scale, fix it before production.
- The "Tell" system is the strongest ensemble design idea. Consider adding an explicit production note: "In any scene where character emotional subtext is important, ensure at least one tell element is visible on each character in frame."

---

## Verdict

**Luma:** Conditionally passing. The design concept is strong, the shape language is disciplined, the emotional hair system is excellent. The pixel hoodie pattern is an unresolved production problem that must be solved before the design can be called production-ready. Fix the simplified tier system. Test the front silhouette. Lock the hair curl count. Then this design is genuinely good.

**Cosmo:** Not passing as specified. The design concept is correct and the character's appeal potential is real, but the silhouette fails at distance, the shape language is insufficiently committed to rectangle geometry, and the static image does not pop without expression context. These are fixable structural problems, not style problems. Fix the corner radius specification, thicken the glasses frames, expand the tilt range, and add a long-shot silhouette element. Then resubmit.

**Byte:** Conditionally passing on concept, failing on production specification. The design idea is the most original in the project. The execution documentation has significant gaps: missing limb expression vocabulary, no pixel-eye scale threshold specification, no technical spec for jitter-line treatment, no production-cost mitigation plan, incomplete turnaround documentation. The pixel-eye legibility at shoulder scale is a potential fundamental flaw that requires testing, not assertion. Fix the production documentation. Test the scale. Then this design can be called ready.

**Ensemble:** The trio works as a system. The shape contrast, color temperature distribution, and tell-system are all correct structural decisions. The cyan thread connecting Luma and Byte is smart. Cosmo's "belongs to neither world" color identity is right. The ensemble reading — action, understanding, wisdom — maps cleanly onto the three designs. If the individual character problems are resolved, this is a strong ensemble.

**Overall:** Promising first pass with critical unresolved production issues. The design intent is sound. The execution documentation is incomplete. The team is thinking at the right level conceptually but has not yet done the hard work of making that concept production-proof. Do the work. This show has the bones of something distinctive. Don't ruin it with sloppy specification.
