## METHOD

Blank test applied to all sequential and staged assets. Every caption, dialogue line, arc label, and annotation text was ignored. I read each panel as a silent image. If I cannot reconstruct the story beat from the image alone, the work has not done its job.

This review covers the complete pitch — all cold open standalone panels (P03–P24), the pilot contact sheet, Act 1/Act 2 contact sheets and panels, all six style frames (SF01–SF06), the COVETOUS storyboard, and the character lineup v010. What I understand from P18: CU/INSERT. A notebook page. Lined paper, pencil doodles. The doodles show: a face in a CRT screen (the "HERO DOODLE" — a crude drawing of the creature's face in static), smaller margin doodles of eyes (one broken), question marks, arrows. **Blank test result:** Without reading the handwritten text, the visual content of this panel is partially legible. The notebook page reads as: someone drew a face they saw in a screen. The doodle of the CRT with a face in it communicates the concept. The margin sketches of eyes (one cracked) connect to the creature we have been watching. The cheese puffs connect to the sleeping figure from earlier panels.

**However:** This panel is fundamentally text-dependent. The emotional beat — that Luma drew Byte before she knew what he was, that she has been processing this encounter through her own visual language — relies on the viewer reading "saw it again tonight" and "it LOOKED at me." Strip the handwriting and the panel becomes: a page of doodles. The doodles are recognizable as the creature, but the temporal information (she drew this BEFORE tonight, she has been watching for longer than we knew) is entirely carried by text. This is the one place in the new panels where the staging fails the blank test.

- The panel needs a visual timestamp. A date on the notebook page that is clearly NOT tonight. A different pen color for the older doodles vs. tonight's annotations. Something that communicates "these drawings are from before" without requiring the viewer to read the words.
- The "HERO DOODLE" (CRT with face in static) is the right focal point but is placed at center-left mid-frame, competing with the upper annotations for eye entry. It should be the eye's landing point — either larger, or positioned at the natural reading entry (upper-left or center).

**Score for P18 alone: 62/100.** The concept is excellent. The execution makes the text do the storytelling work that the staging should do.

What I understand from P19: Medium shot. Left: the creature, large in frame, facing right, expression labeled "OFFENDED" — mouth open, posture upright, one arm gesturing. Pixel debris around it. Right: the girl, sitting on the floor, notebook visible ("notebook from P18"), looking at the creature with — the label says "INTRIGUED" — a half-smile. Two dark rectangles upper-right (CRT monitors, static). The dialogue box reads: BYTE: "The preferred term is 'Glitchkin.'"

The comedy beat lands visually: a six-inch digital being correcting a twelve-year-old with wounded dignity is inherently funny in staging alone.

- Concern: the two CRT monitors in the upper-right background are dark and static. They should show SOMETHING — even faint cyan activity — to plant the escalation that P20-P24 will deliver. Dead monitors in this panel imply the breach is over. It is not.
- The warm/cool split is correct: warm amber floor and background on Luma's side, cool teal zone beneath Byte. The labeled "COOL [Byte zone]" floor annotation at lower-left confirms this was intentional. Good.

**Score for P19 alone: 74/100.** The comedy staging works. The background monitors are a missed opportunity for foreshadowing.

**Sequence-level assessment:**

Read in order with text stripped, the sequence communicates: a digital intrusion begins small (one pixel), escalates through a screen breach, introduces a small digital creature, builds to mutual discovery between the creature and a sleeping child, erupts into physical comedy, settles into wary mutual assessment, and bridges through a processing beat (notebook) to first contact/dialogue. The escalation arc (NONE through CHAOS) reads clearly.

**Remaining sequence gaps that hurt the visual narrative:**

Comedy that skips its escalation becomes slapstick without structure.

Who decided to stop moving? The story bible says Byte's agency in this moment is character-defining. Currently, the sequence implies it was simply mutual exhaustion. That is a lesser story.

## STYLE FRAMES — SF01 through SF06

**Score: 72/100**

**SF01 Discovery:** My C37 concern (Luma reaching AT the CRT rather than LOOKING AT it — no sight-line between her eyes and Byte's face in the screen) has not been addressed. The panel still reads as "child interacts with object" rather than "child perceives being." This remains a staging problem, not a rendering problem.

**SF02 Glitch Storm:** My C37 concern (characters as noise within environment, unclear movement direction) persists in the current version. The visual hierarchy still inverts — the environment dominates, the characters submit. However, the warm/cool separation at 6.5 is correct and the tonal argument (warm below, cool above — warmth has not surrendered) reads without text.

**SF03 The Other Side:** Still the pitch's most atmospheric and least narrative frame. Two small figures on a platform in a digital void. Strip text: no story information beyond "these two are somewhere alien." The Glitch character (right side, amber diamond shape) has clear staging — it is large, it occupies space with authority, it faces the small figures. This is improved from C37. The three-body staging (Luma small-left, Byte mid, Glitch large-right) communicates a power gradient. But the scene's emotional content (the Other Side is ordered silence, and that order is the threat) remains invisible. Order looks like calm. The frame looks calm. That is not the same as communicating that calm-as-threat.

**SF04 Resolution:** Correctly restores warmth. The residue elements (Byte ghost, hoodie pixel streak, lamp amber) communicate that the world has changed even though it looks safe. This frame passes the blank test: a child stands in a warm room, but there are traces of something else. The viewer feels the "after" without needing the "before" explained.

**SF05 The Passing (NEW):** Miri (left) and Luma (right) in a kitchen. CRT monitor between them, slightly behind. The warm/cool split is horizontal — warm amber upper, cool teal-spill lower. Miri is smaller, seated or standing at a lower position. Luma is taller, standing.

Blank test: Two figures in a domestic space with a screen between them. The generational relationship reads (one older, one younger). The CRT as the object that connects them reads. But: the staging is static. Both figures face forward, not each other. There is no gesture, no sight-line, no physical action that communicates the "passing" — the transmission of attention, the inheritance of perception. The frame title promises a relationship beat. The staging delivers two people in a room with a television. A "passing" requires one figure to be giving and one to be receiving. I see no directionality in the exchange.

**SF06 The Hand-Off (NEW):** Miri (left, hand on or near CRT) and Luma (right, attentive). CRT is center, large, dominant. The warm/cool split is vertical — warm left (Miri's side, SUNLIT_AMBER), cool right (CRT spill). Miri's hand-on-CRT is the key gesture.

Blank test: This is better than SF05. Miri's hand on the CRT is a possessive/protective gesture — this is her object, her relationship with it. Luma, positioned opposite, is attentive. The CRT between them functions as the object being handed off. However: Luma's expression reads as guarded or confused, not as receiving. The "hand-off" requires Luma to be open to it. Her current posture (arms slightly away from body, facial expression uncertain) reads as "not yet ready." If this is intentional — if SF06 is meant to show the moment BEFORE the hand-off lands — then the staging is correct but the frame is incomplete. A hand-off that hasn't happened yet is not a hand-off.

Combined, SF05 and SF06 begin to address my C37 note that the Miri/Miri connection had no visual presence in the pitch. They do not fully resolve it. Neither frame shows the moment of transmission — only the conditions for it.

## COVETOUS STORYBOARD (EP05)

**Score: 77/100**

Three-character triangulation: Glitch (left, COVETOUS expression, large, leaning forward), Byte (center, barrier posture, arms extended), Luma (right, warm zone, smaller). ACID_GREEN sight-line from Glitch to Luma's head.

Blank test: A large threatening figure wants something that a small figure is protecting from it. The middle figure is the barrier. The sight-line makes the desire vector explicit. The color arc (amber/void left through teal center through warm orange right) encodes the power gradient. Glitch is NOT lit by Luma's warmth — this communicates exclusion, which is correct for COVETOUS (wanting what you cannot have requires not having it).

- The gap between Byte and Luma is wider than the gap between Glitch and Byte. This is correct — Byte is closer to the threat than to the protected, which is where a protector stands.
- Luma's posture is slightly too passive. She stands, she watches. In the story bible, Luma is not someone who stands behind a protector. She is someone who stands beside one, or in front of one. Her staging here makes her the object of protection rather than a participant in the standoff. This flattens her character.

**Bottom line:** The triangulation staging works, but Luma is staged as a prize rather than an agent.

## CHARACTER LINEUP v010

**Score: 70/100**

The dual-warmth tier depth bands (warm amber FG, cool slate BG) are a genuine improvement — the spatial depth reads at thumbnail scale, which it did not before. The five characters are differentiated by silhouette, scale, and color temperature. Byte and Luma in the warm FG tier, Cosmo/Miri/Glitch in the cool BG tier — this staging decision communicates the show's central pairing without labeling it.

However: the lineup is a static reference sheet. It communicates who these characters are physically. It does not communicate who they are to each other. No character is looking at any other character. No gesture connects any two of them. Five figures standing in a row is a police lineup, not a cast. A pitch lineup should plant at least one relational cue — Byte on Luma's shoulder, Cosmo's notebook open toward the viewer, Miri's gaze toward Luma, Glitch's gaze toward Byte. Something that says: these people have a story together.

**Bottom line:** Correct reference document, weak pitch image.

## OVERALL PITCH ASSESSMENT — VISUAL NARRATIVE

**Combined Score: 76/100 (up from 72 in C37)**

**What has improved since C37:**
- P10 remains the sequence's best panel and has not been degraded.
- P16 and P17 are strong new additions. P16's single-eye floor-level ECU is the cold open's second-best compositional decision after P10. P17's mutual stillness with the falling chip as sole motion is correct visual storytelling — silence as content.
- P19's comedy staging (offended dignity vs. intrigued amusement) works without dialogue.
- The cold open gap log is an honest, precise inventory of what is missing.
- The story bible and storyboards are now telling the same story.
- SF06's hand-on-CRT gesture begins to plant the Miri inheritance motif.

**What still holds the pitch below its potential:**
1. **P13 has no standalone panel.** This is the cold open's thematic fulcrum — the visual proof that Luma and Byte are mirrors of each other — and it exists only as a small cell on a contact sheet. Until this panel is rebuilt as a standalone with its specified symmetry, the pitch's central visual argument has a hole in it.
2. **P18 fails the blank test.** The notebook insert is text-dependent. The temporal information (Luma drew Byte before tonight) must be communicated visually — through ink color, aging, or compositional layering — not through handwritten words.
3. **SF01's sight-line failure persists.** Luma reaches at the CRT, not toward the being in it. The show's premise is perception. The founding image should show perception, not interaction with hardware.
4. **SF05 and SF06 plant the Miri inheritance but do not execute it.** Neither frame shows the moment of transmission. The hand-off has not happened in either "hand-off" frame.
5. **The lineup is a reference sheet, not a pitch image.** No relational staging, no gaze connections, no gesture that communicates story.

**Bottom line:** The cold open's visual storytelling has materially improved. The new panels (P16, P17, P19) demonstrate that Diego Vargas understands staging as narrative. But the pitch still lacks its single most important image: a standalone P13 that executes the symmetrical mirror composition. Without it, the relationship that drives the show has no visual thesis statement.
