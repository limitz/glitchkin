# Critique — Cycle 17
**Critic:** Amara Diallo — Cultural Representation and Authentic World-Building
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Scope:** Full pitch package: character designs, environments, style frames, storyboards
**Note:** I review cultural specificity, identity accuracy, and world-building authenticity — not color fidelity or technical pipeline.

---

## PRELIMINARY NOTE: THE CONSULTATION GAP

Before reviewing individual assets, I must document the structural problem that underlies all of the following assessments: there is no evidence anywhere in the production documentation of cultural consultation with any community whose identity markers are drawn on in this production. No credits, no advisory notes, no acknowledgment of primary sources. "Luma & the Glitchkin" carries specific cultural identity signals — West African surname, natural-hair aesthetics, West African naming, Mediterranean features, Japan-adjacent design sensibilities — and the production documents are silent on who was consulted to verify these signals are being handled correctly.

This is the foundational concern. Every score below is lower than it would be if consultation were documented.

---

## 1. Grandma Miri (Miriam Okonkwo-Santos)

Score: 48
- The surname Okonkwo-Santos is a specific, culturally meaningful choice. Okonkwo is an Igbo surname — specifically Igbo Nigerian. Santos is Portuguese/Brazilian. The name implies a specific diaspora history: Nigerian-Brazilian, or Nigerian-Portuguese, or a marriage across these traditions. The production has named this character with precision and then built her entire world in culturally generic "vintage American cozy." Her home is described as a "small-town USA" house with CRT monitors, VHS tapes, and a magazine called "MODEM WORLD." None of this is wrong — diaspora families live in suburban America — but there is not a single visual marker anywhere in her environment, her clothing, her food, her objects, that acknowledges the specific cultural history her name implies. Her house is indistinguishable from that of a white American grandmother of the same era.
- The chopsticks in her hair (MIRI-A canonical design) read as East Asian styling applied to a character with a West African/Brazilian name. Chopsticks as hair accessories are a specific aesthetic convention from East Asian cultures. On a character named Okonkwo-Santos, this reads as the production pulling from the nearest "non-Western" design bin without asking which bin was appropriate. This is the most specific error in the pitch package and it needs a direct decision: is Miri's design heritage being revised, or is the East Asian styling a deliberate story choice with in-universe explanation? The production documents contain no answer.
Bottom line: Miri has one of the richest cultural identities available in this cast and the production is spending none of it.

## 2. Luma (Protagonist)

Score: 62
- Luma's design carries clear visual identity signals: warm caramel skin (#C8885A), natural dark curly hair described as "a massive, exuberant cloud," amber eyes. These signals are consistent with a Black or mixed-heritage girl — the character document does not state her ethnicity, but the design choices accumulate into a specific cultural read that viewers from those communities will recognize immediately and evaluate for authenticity. The design is not incoherent — the shape language, the hair's emotional reactivity, the warm palette are all internally consistent. The question is whether this consistency was built with knowledge or by assembling "brown girl" signifiers without accountability.
- The hair is the strongest single design element in the pitch package. The "5 curl lock," the emotional reactivity rules (hair lifts when excited, flattens when scared), the crown highlight — these suggest a designer who engaged with natural hair as a subject rather than a texture. This is the most culturally committed element in the entire production. However, the expression sheet labels use generic English emotional descriptors with no cultural nuance. More significantly: there is no documentation of whether any member of the creative team has the cultural proximity to the hair aesthetics being depicted, or whether this was researched or assumed.
Bottom line: Luma's design has genuine warmth but no documented accountability — the hair works, the rest of the cultural identity has been left implicit and unverified.

## 3. Cosmo (Best Friend)

Score: 55
- Cosmo is described as having "Light Warm Olive" skin (#D9C09A) and "Blue-Black" hair with a precise center-right part. The character doc calls this a "warm Mediterranean neutral." This is doing a quiet but significant amount of work: "Mediterranean" is a cultural identity that spans North Africa, Southern Europe, the Levant, and West Asia — it is not a single coherent culture, and using it as a visual adjective ("warm Mediterranean neutral") treats it as a generic stand-in for "ambiguous brownish." The blue-black hair with a cowlick, the thick glasses, the anxious-overthinking personality mapped onto this visual palette — these choices, without cultural specificity, risk sliding into a familiar and reductive design pattern.
- Unlike Miri, Cosmo's design is not incoherent. The problem is that it is not *committed*. He is ethnically ambiguous in a way that reads as deliberate erasure of specificity rather than genuine ambiguity. He has been designed to be "diverse-ish" without taking responsibility for what specific identity he represents. This is the definition of what I call the "safe vague" character — identifiable as non-white, placeable nowhere, accountable to no one.
Bottom line: Cosmo's design evades cultural commitment in a way that protects the production from critique while delivering none of the richness specific identity brings.

## 4. The Grandma's Kitchen Environment

Score: 54
- The kitchen is described as a "warm morning" small domestic space with "old-fashioned appliances, pre-digital gas stove, porcelain sink." The implementation reads as a generic American mid-century kitchen rendered in warm terracotta and cream. The food in the scene is not specified. The cultural objects are not specified. The décor is not specified beyond "crossword puzzle, tea mug, toast, fridge magnets." For a character whose surname places her in a specific West African-Brazilian diaspora tradition, the kitchen is the primary place where cultural specificity would be most naturally expressed — in cooking tools, in the food implied by the smells and remains on the counter, in the art on the walls, in the calendars or the objects that accumulate in a home where someone has lived for decades. None of this is present. The kitchen is culturally neutral by default, which is a production choice, and it is the wrong one.
- The CRT television "visible through the doorway" is specified as a "story element" — the implication being this is part of the Glitch Layer setup. There is no cultural story in any of the objects. Every single item in this kitchen could belong to any grandmother in any American suburb. This is the erasure I cannot accept.
Bottom line: A kitchen that tells us nothing about who cooked in it is a missed opportunity that diminishes the character it belongs to.

## 5. Millbrook as a Setting

Score: 44
- The production document describes Millbrook as "a small, slightly eccentric town that feels like it was built by someone who had seen a real town described to them but never actually visited one." This is offered as whimsy, but it is also an accurate description of generic small-town American visual shorthand: terracotta and cream architecture, power lines, a "town center," a junkyard on the edge of town. There is no cultural specificity to Millbrook. It is American small-town, undefined ethnicity, undefined region, undefined history. The show explicitly notes that the town "is unknowingly built on a node of glitch energy" — this is a premise that could connect meaningfully to any number of indigenous, colonial, or local history narratives. The production has chosen not to engage with any of them.
- The school is "Millbrook Middle School" — sage green and dusty lavender. The classroom has "binary/math board content." The hallway has metal lockers and linoleum checkerboard. These are not culturally specific — they are the platonic form of a generic American school. For a show with a racially specific protagonist and a culturally named mentor, the world they inhabit should have more specificity than this.
Bottom line: Millbrook is a visual setting that belongs to no real place and no real community, which means it cannot represent anyone's lived experience.

## 6. Style Frame 01 — Discovery (SF01)

Score: 59
- The SF01 "Discovery" frame is set in Grandma Miri's study — a warm room with a CRT monitor. The color temperature execution is sound (warm amber dominant, cyan intrusion from the monitor). My concern is that this is the frame that establishes Miri's domestic world most fully, and the production has filled that world with generic vintage-tech domestic objects. The setting communicates "nostalgic American grandmother" without any additional cultural layer. Given that Miri's name indicates a specific diaspora identity, and given that the SF01 frame is a pitch document that will be seen by network executives, this frame is representing her as culturally generic when she should be culturally specific.
Bottom line: SF01 passes as atmospheric but fails as representation — it shows us a grandmother's room, not this grandmother's room.

## 7. Character Lineup

Score: 65
- The character lineup (v008, C42) places five characters in a consistent staging. Viewing the lineup as a whole: the cast has visual diversity that reads as deliberate and modern (Brown girl protagonist, ambiguously-Mediterranean best friend, Black grandmother figure, non-humanoid companions). The visual contrast among the human characters is functional — they read as a diverse ensemble without any single character bearing the full weight of "the diverse character."
- The limitation is that this diversity operates at the surface level: skin tone, hair texture, eye color. The design choices communicate "representation is present" without communicating any specific cultural commitment. A production with genuine cultural accountability would have characters whose visual design can be traced to specific cultural traditions, not assembled from abstracted diversity signals.
Bottom line: The lineup looks diverse; it does not yet mean anything culturally specific.

## OVERARCHING FINDINGS

**What is working:**
- Luma's natural hair design is genuinely engaged, not merely gestured at.
- The visual DNA system connecting Luma and Miri through iris color, eye highlight position, and garment warmth is sophisticated relationship-building in the design language.
- The dual-blush rule for Miri is a remarkably specific emotional characterization for a supporting character.

**What is not working:**
1. The name Okonkwo-Santos is carrying enormous cultural weight that the visual design has not matched. The most urgent single action this production can take is to decide, explicitly, what Miri's specific cultural heritage is and build it into her environment.
2. The chopstick hair accessory on Miri is a cross-cultural design error that needs to be resolved with a clear production decision.
3. Millbrook is culturally generic in a way that prevents it from functioning as a real place. A town without a cultural history is a backdrop, not a world.
4. There is no evidence of cultural consultation anywhere in the production documentation. This is the structural problem underlying all the above.

**The ask:**
Before the next critique cycle, the production should produce a one-page cultural accountability statement that answers: (a) What is Miri's specific cultural heritage? (b) Who was consulted? (c) What is the design decision on the chopsticks? Without this document, the same findings will repeat in Critique 18.
