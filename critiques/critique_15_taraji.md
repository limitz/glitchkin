# Critique — "Luma & the Glitchkin" Pitch Review
**Critic:** Taraji Coleman — Educator Observer, 4th-grade teacher
**Cycle:** 37
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Materials reviewed:** story_bible_v001.md, luma.md, cosmo.md, character_lineup.md, storyboard ep01_cold_open.md, act2_thumbnail_plan.md, style_frame_01_discovery.md

---

## LUMA (protagonist)

**Score: 78 / 100**

- "Okay but what if we just —" is exact. I hear this sentence every day. The voice guide knows this kid. But Luma's *interiority* as described skews toward a writer's concept of recklessness — she reads more like a heroic archetype than a real messy twelve-year-old. Real Luma would also be embarrassed sometimes. Real Luma would have one thing she's scared of that has nothing to do with the plot. Where is her version of "I stopped trying to show people"? The bible names it once and immediately dissolves it. That wound needs to stay open longer.
- The sleeping pose in Panel 04 — chip puff in her mouth, notebook mid-sentence, hair eating the couch — is the single most accurate thing in this entire pitch. That is exactly how a child who cannot stop looks when she finally does. Protect that image. It says more than fifty pages of character notes.
- "She stopped doubting her own perception the moment Byte looked back." This is stated as a resolved fact. For a 12-year-old, certainty this clean is an adult's wish, not a child's experience. Kids that age doubt themselves the hardest in the exact moment they are most right. Luma should carry that doubt longer than the pitch currently allows.

**Bottom line:** Luma's voice is correct but her psychology is tidied up — the mess that would make her feel truly twelve needs to stay in the frame longer before the story grants her its validation.

---

## COSMO (best friend)

**Score: 72 / 100**

- Cosmo is Luma's counterweight but the relationship dynamics don't yet show the specific texture of a real 12-year-old friendship. These two children have presumably been friends for years. What do they fight about? The bible gives us their dynamic in opposition but not in friction. Real best friends at this age argue about stupid things and then stand beside each other through real things without ever acknowledging the repair. I need one scene where Cosmo is genuinely annoyed at Luma — not resigned, not exasperated in a comedy sense, but actually hurt. That's what makes Act 2's shoulder touch (after the notebook closes) land as something other than a nice moment.
- The "I have some notes on that" voice is funny and specific. The detail that he never says "I told you so" even when he did is excellent — that's a writer who has watched a real careful child choose kindness.
- At 4.0 heads vs. Luma's 3.2, Cosmo reads visually older. My students would immediately assign him adult-adjacent credibility. That's a representation note: the taller, more controlled, more articulate character is also the one who looks least like half my classroom. Whether that's a problem depends on what the show does with him over time — but it's worth naming.

**Bottom line:** Cosmo's voice is accurate but his emotional relationship to Luma needs one real wound in it before I believe these are children who chose each other.

---

## FRIENDSHIPS AND SOCIAL STAKES

**Score: 65 / 100**

- The show's central social problem — "the specific loneliness of perceiving the world differently" — is real and children this age feel it acutely. The pitch earns that description. But the friendships as structured are currently a support system, not a social world. Where is anyone outside this trio? 12-year-olds live and die by the wider social ecosystem: who's watching, who knows, who cannot know. The vending machine incident (A1-05) has Luma and Cosmo but no reaction from any other student. That's a missed opportunity. Someone sees something they can't explain, makes fun of Luma for reacting, and then walks away not knowing what they just avoided. That's the texture of being this age.
- Byte saying "you can actually see me" is the emotional hinge of the pilot. It works. It's the first moment the show tells Luma that what she sees is real — not broken. My kids who feel seen by a story go still the same way Luma goes still in that moment. I felt it reading the page.
- What is the social cost to Luma if Cosmo finds out she's been hiding Byte? The bible doesn't make that cost specific. With 12-year-olds, secrets between best friends are not neutral — they are a kind of debt. That needs to be on the table.

**Bottom line:** The emotional premise of being the kid who notices what others miss is exactly right, but the social world around the trio is currently too thin to give that premise its full weight.

---

## REPRESENTATION

**Score: 60 / 100**

- Luma's dark curls and warm caramel skin are visible in the design documents. Grandma Miri's deep brown skin and terracotta cardigan place her clearly. Cosmo's olive skin is named. These are specific choices and I notice them. This is a show where the kids who look like my students are at the center, not the edge. That matters enormously and I do not want to undersell it.
- However: the classroom in A1-04 has a teacher named Ms. Okafor described as "warm, sharp" — and then nothing. She could be a powerful recurring adult who sees Luma clearly, or she could be set dressing for a near-miss beat. As written she is currently the latter. That's a wasted asset.
- There are no children outside the main trio who have faces, names, or any specificity. My concern is not diversity as a checklist — it's that children know immediately when a show's background is populated with props vs. people. Give two background students one specific detail each and the whole world becomes more real.
- "Grandma Miri has never once spoken to Luma like a child." This is the line I want stitched on a wall. Grandma Miri as written is the best adult in this pitch.

**Bottom line:** The core trio's representation is specific and intentional; the world around them is not yet inhabited enough for children to find themselves in it.

## CONFLICT MODEL AND PREACHINESS CHECK

**Score: 74 / 100**

- The pilot's conflict resolution — Luma uses her own perception to solve the problem using skills she already had — is correct. The show is not telling her she was right to charge in; it's telling her she was right to *see*. That distinction is real and children feel it. The show does not congratulate itself, which is rarer than it should be.
- Act Two's structure is strong: the wrong plan fails, Cosmo absorbs the failure, Luma acknowledges it without lying about it. "She does not say 'it's okay' — she knows that is not what he needs." That sentence alone tells me the writers have watched children care for each other. This is exactly how 12-year-olds comfort each other when they're paying attention. It does not condescend.
- The Corruption is described as "a decision — something in the Layer chose order over chaos." For the 9-12 bracket, that philosophical stakes register lands well. It is not preachy; it is a real idea. Children this age have strong opinions about fairness and systems and whether rules are good. The Corruption as "ruthless optimization" is the right villain for this audience.
- One flag: Byte's emotional arc as described resolves in the finale with him choosing to stay "out loud, using words, which costs him everything." That resolution is too verbal. Children this age understand the person who finally shows up for you without saying they will. Let him stay in the action, not the speech.

**Bottom line:** The conflict model is healthy and specific — the show earns its emotional moments through cost, not convenience — but Byte's arc resolution risks an announced vulnerability that undercuts the character's more honest silence.

---

## OVERALL ASSESSMENT

**Score: 70 / 100**

This pitch is doing several things that children's media almost never does: it treats the kid who sees differently as correct rather than as someone who needs to learn to fit in; it gives the grandmother full agency and intelligence; it earns its emotional beats through actual character cost; and it has a visual language specific enough that children will recognize it as theirs. The sleeping Luma in Panel 04 is one of the most accurate character introductions I have read — not because it's cute, but because it is true.

What is missing: the social ecosystem outside the trio, the specific weight of friendship with history in it, and a Luma who doubts herself the way real 12-year-olds do even when they're right. The show is currently protecting Luma from the harder version of her own experience. I understand why — the story wants to give her validation, and she deserves it — but children know when they're being shown a sanitized version of loneliness. Let her be wrong about something that isn't the Glitch Layer. Let Cosmo have a real grievance. Let the world outside Grandma Miri's house have at least two other people in it who notice things.

When this show trusts its premise fully — when it lets Luma be twelve instead of a twelve-year-old who is ready to be the protagonist — it will be the show it says it is.

**Bottom line:** "Luma & the Glitchkin" has genuine emotional intelligence and accurate social instincts that are rare in children's animation, but is currently protecting its protagonist from the specific vulnerabilities that would make her feel like a real child to the audience watching her.
