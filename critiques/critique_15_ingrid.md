## STORY BIBLE v001 — Narrative Architecture

**Score: 78/100**

- The three-world structure is clearly articulated and the escalation logic (chaos → order-as-threat) is genuinely distinctive. The Bible promises visual specificity — Byte's cracked eye glitching when he's pretending not to care, the hoodie as a bridge object — but most of these are written promises, not yet demonstrated in assets.
- The dual-Miri seed is the Bible's strongest narrative device, and it is entirely absent from every visual asset produced to date. Not one storyboard panel, style frame, or character sheet plants this connection. A promise the audience cannot see is not yet a story.
- Story beat descriptions in section 6 (pilot concept) describe a different cold open from the one storyboarded. The Bible cold open opens at school in daylight; the storyboard opens at night in Grandma Miri's den. These are irreconcilable. One of them is wrong, and the artist working from the Bible will produce the wrong sequence.
- The three flags at the end of the Bible (Byte's shape language discrepancy, unnamed "Glitch" character, visual plant for Miri/Miri) are all real problems and all three remain unresolved in the visual assets. The Bible is flagging failures it has not forced anyone to fix.
- **Bottom line:** The Bible has strong bones but writes checks the visual assets cannot yet cash, and contains a direct contradiction between its own cold open description and the storyboarded one that must be resolved before pitch delivery.

## PILOT COLD OPEN STORYBOARD (ep01_cold_open.md + panels P01–P25)

**Score: 71/100**

**Blank test applied: all captions removed, panels read in order.**

- **P01 to P04: PASS.** Establishing exterior → house exterior → CRT pixel → sleeping Luma. Without text, this reads as: quiet town / specific house / something electronic is waking up / sleeping child nearby. The causal chain is legible. One problem: P01 renders as a single house in a near-void. The script calls for "a town" but the panel delivers a single building. Without the caption "Millbrook," a viewer reads: isolated farmhouse. The world-building promise fails in the first panel.
- **P05 to P07: PASS with reservations.** The escalating pixel count reads clearly. The "we are on the monitor's side now" spatial shift in P05 works — the low-angle shelf POV is the sequence's strongest staging decision. P07's Dutch tilt is earned. However: P07's composition places Byte's breach point at extreme upper-right and sleeping Luma at extreme lower-left, with the frame nearly empty in the center. The cause-effect relationship (Byte bursting through → Luma's world is about to change) is not reinforced by proximity or sight-line. These two characters share no visual axis in this frame despite being in the same room.
- **P08 to P10: PASS.** P08 high-angle correctly establishes Byte as small in this world. P09 eye-level elevation shift is the right call — by moving the camera to Byte's 18" height, we validate him as a character before he has earned it. P10 OTS is the sequence's best compositional decision: the cool angular silhouette framing the warm sleeping face does the thematic work without words. This is the only frame where the warm/cold palette opposition functions as storytelling rather than decoration.
- **P11 and P12: PARTIAL FAIL.** P11 (eyes closed, then snap open) is correct in concept but the panel as rendered shows Luma's closed eyes with Byte visible at cheek level as a small glowing square — the proximity that the script specifies ("5 inches from her face") is not conveyed. The rendered panel reads as Luma asleep and a small icon nearby, not two beings at intimate range. P12, the pre-scream freeze, is described as a "CU two-shot, both faces taking half the frame" — the rendered panel shows a WIDE shot with Luma standing full-body and Byte floating in open space. The close intimacy that makes the comedy beat work has evaporated. This is a staging failure, not a drawing quality failure.
- **P13: CRITICAL FAIL.** This is the sequence's most important panel and it does not deliver its promise. The script specifies a perfectly symmetrical wide two-shot with Luma airborne left and Byte mirroring her right, couch as dividing axis, simultaneous scream. The rendered panel shows Luma forward-center with arms raised and Byte to her right, frozen in place. There is no symmetry, no mirror, no dividing axis. The visual argument — "these two beings are having the exact same reaction, they are more alike than they know" — is simply not there. Without this visual rhyme, the comedy beat is just two characters yelling. The thematic core of the moment is absent from its own key frame.
- **P17 to P20: PASS.** The quiet beat sequence works. P17 chip-falls-between-them is staged cleanly. P19 Byte's character moment (CU, expression cascade) is the sequence's strongest character frame — the panel correctly gives him the frame, the sizing is right, and his expression cascade concept communicates his interiority without dialogue. P20 two-shot with space-between-them as compositional invitation for their relationship is correct.
- **P23 to P25: PASS with one concern.** P23 (backs to camera, Luma+Byte facing the chaos together) is correctly staged as a "promise shot" and it works. P24 (breach pouring out, Byte on shoulder) is the hook frame and the composition functions. P25 title card is clean. Concern: there is no panel between P22a (Byte accidentally landing on Luma's shoulder) and P24 (the established shoulder position) that shows Luma's reaction to Byte being on her shoulder. We jump from accidental contact to "they are a unit now." The emotional beat that earns their partnership has no visual representation in the sequence.

**Blank test summary:** The escalation arc (NONE → TRACE → PRESENCE → BREACH → CHAOS) reads clearly without words. Character agency is legible: a digital creature entered this world and found a human child. The comedy and chaos are readable. What does not survive the blank test: (1) the town as a world — it reads as one house; (2) the intimacy of P11/P12 that gives the comedy its weight; (3) the thematic symmetry of P13 that earns the relationship; (4) the transition beat that makes Luma's acceptance of Byte on her shoulder feel chosen rather than accidental.

## STYLE FRAMES — Visual Story Consistency

**Score: 66/100**

- **SF01 Discovery (v005):** Luma reaches toward the CRT, ghost-Byte visible in the screen. This is the show's founding image and it works — perception as action, the digital world visible to this specific child. However, Luma's body language reads as reaching for an object, not reaching toward a being. There is no sight-line between Luma's eyes and Byte's face in the screen. She is pointing at it, not seeing it. The story bible's central promise — "she is the only one who can see it" — requires her to be LOOKING, not pointing.
- **SF02 Glitch Storm (v008):** Three characters running against an abstract crisis, hard to read which direction they're running and what they're running from or toward. The Glitch Storm confetti overwhelms the character silhouettes. Without captions: three shapes are moving laterally across the screen. There is a glowing window in the lower right that draws more eye attention than any character. The visual hierarchy is inverted — the environment is the subject, the characters are noise.
- **SF03 Other Side (v005):** This is the pitch's most atmospheric frame and its least narratively functional. Strip the text: two very small figures stand on a platform in an abstract digital space. Nothing about their staging communicates why they are there, what they want, or what they fear. The "Other Side is ordered silence that is more frightening than the Layer's chaos" is not readable. It reads as: beautiful digital landscape, tiny figures, no story information.
- **SF04 The Dynamic (luma_byte v004):** The strongest narrative frame in the pitch. Cosmo points, Byte floats between them, the CRT looms. The sight-lines actually work here — all three visual elements are in a clear left-to-right reading axis. The problem: Grandma Miri's kitchen is described in the Bible as the emotional anchor of the Real World, but this frame is set in a generic warm interior. The story bible's specificity ("kitchen smells like crossword puzzles and burned toast") has no visual corollary anywhere in the pitch.

## ESTABLISHED ASSETS — Fresh Review

**Character lineup v007:** The size relationships communicate the show's logic (Byte is tiny, Luma is mid, Cosmo is tallest, Grandma Miri is broad). Glitch as a character is present in the lineup but absent from the story bible's cast section — this is Flag 03 from the Bible and it remains live. A character whose visual design is developed enough to appear in the lineup should have narrative clarity by pitch stage.

**Luma expression sheet v010:** THE NOTICING expression is the critical new addition and it functions — the head-tilt, slightly-narrowed eyes, and forward lean communicate active perception rather than passive observation. This is the expression the show is built on. However: the sheet contains 7 expressions and only one (THE NOTICING) is specifically tied to the show's central premise. The other six (CURIOUS, DETERMINED, SURPRISED, WORRIED, DELIGHTED, FRUSTRATED) are correct but generic. A character whose superpower is perception should have more expressions in the register of "seeing what others miss."

**Byte expression sheet v005:** RESIGNED/RELUCTANT DISCLOSURE (expression 8) is the most story-useful addition to the sheet and it is correctly differentiated from RELUCTANT JOY. The SEARCHING/PROCESSING expression is correctly deployed in P10. The sheet is functional.

**Grandma Miri expression sheet:** The "SURPRISED → KNOWING" expression required for panel A2-08 (the doorway reveal) is present and the panel executes it correctly. A2-08 is the pitch's single best-staged emotional beat — the warm backlight framing her as someone who belongs in this story, the CRT glow catch on her left cheek, the "recognition not fear" expression note. This panel passes the blank test: even without the caption, Grandma Miri in that doorway reads as someone who expected this. This is exactly right.

## OVERALL PITCH ASSESSMENT

**Combined Narrative Score: 72/100**

The cold open's escalation arc is sound and the sequence's best staging decisions (P05 shelf-level POV, P10 OTS, P19 Byte CU) demonstrate genuine visual storytelling instinct. The warm/cold palette opposition is correctly understood as emotional language. The act 2 panel for Grandma Miri's reveal is the pitch's strongest single image.

What holds the pitch below its potential: the pitch's central emotional argument — that Luma sees what others miss, and that this perception is both her loneliness and her gift — is stated everywhere in the documents and demonstrated nowhere in the staged images. Panel P13, the sequence's thematic fulcrum, fails to execute its own compositional argument. Style frames SF02 and SF03 read as environments without characters rather than characters within environments. The Miri/Miri connection, the show's most resonant long-form storytelling device, has no visual seed anywhere in the pitch package.

**Bottom line:** The cold open storyboard has a functional story but a broken key panel, and the style frames communicate art direction without communicating story.
