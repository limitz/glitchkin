# Storyboard Critique — Cycle 4
**Critic:** Carmen Reyes, Storyboard & Layout Supervisor
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Subject:** First actual panel images — "Luma & the Glitchkin," Ep01 Cold Open

---

## Overall Assessment

These panels exist. That's not nothing — it's the first time I'm looking at actual rendered images instead of words describing images, and that shift matters enormously. A director can now put a finger on glass and point at something real.

But would I hand these to an animator today? No. Not yet. The bones are right on two panels. The rest are placeholder thinking dressed up in pixels. What I'm seeing is a board that knows what it wants to *say* but hasn't yet committed to *how it feels* to be inside those shots. The staging lacks body language. The camera choices lack conviction. There's a tremendous amount of white space — compositional and emotional — where the drama should be living.

The visual language is coherent. That is meaningful. Dark palette, cyan as the signal color, the pixelated glitch aesthetic — all of that reads immediately and consistently. The art direction team has done its job. Now I need the *staging* team to show up.

---

## Panel-by-Panel Assessment

### P01 — Exterior (EWS)

**What's there:** A silhouetted house against a star-field. One window glowing cyan. Panel label, shot designation (EWS), caption text at bottom.

**Does it communicate the shot?** Yes. This is the cleanest read on the sheet. Establishing Wide Shot, night, Millbrook, one anomalous light source. Any director would understand this in under a second.

**Is the staging clear?** The house silhouette is centered and grounded. The cyan window glow acts as the exact compositional focal point it needs to be — nothing competes with it, the stars recede properly, the dark foreground grounds the frame. The hierarchy of information is correct.

**Is there energy?** This is a static establishing shot, so "energy" means tension, not movement. And there *is* tension — the wrongness of that single cyan glow in an otherwise dead-quiet exterior reads. The isolation is doing real work.

**What works:** The simplicity. A lesser board would cram this with detail. Restraint here is a directorial choice I respect. The star field density is appropriate without being decorative noise.

**What fails:** The house has no specific character. It's *a* house, not *Luma's* house. At EWS this may be acceptable — but if this is the only exterior we get in the cold open, it needed one more beat of personality. A satellite dish. A dark yard. Something that places us in a specific child's world. Right now it reads as "generic suburban night." That's 80% of the job. I need 100%.

**Score: B+** — Functional, clean, does what it says. The weakest emotional miss, but the strongest technical execution on the sheet.

---

### P03 — First Pixel (ECU)

**What's there:** A nearly-black screen with a single tiny cyan pixel in the upper-right quadrant. Caption: "One pixel. Cyan. Wrong. It pulses."

**Does it communicate the shot?** Yes and no. The concept communicates. An extreme close-up of a monitor showing the anomalous first pixel — the birth of the Glitchkin. I understand it.

**Is the staging clear?** This is where I get loud. The pixel is placed in the *upper right*. Why? There is no compositional logic to that placement. Upper-right is where the eye *exits* a frame, not where it rests on a mystery. This pixel should be center or center-left — somewhere that feels trapped, contained, watched. Upper-right reads as escaped, peripheral, unimportant. The staging is fighting the emotional intent.

**Is there energy?** No. And I understand that the pulse is meant to come from animation, but a storyboard panel has to *suggest* the pulse. There's nothing here — no radiating lines, no subtle gradient bloom, no motion trail indication — that tells me this pixel is *alive*. It looks like a dead pixel on a broken monitor. That's the opposite of the scene.

**What works:** The near-black screen with scan lines is correct. The scale contrast — this tiny thing in all this darkness — is conceptually right. The idea of the shot is good.

**What fails:** Placement is wrong. The panel gives no visual indication of the pulse — the single most important action in this shot. An animator handed this panel has no guidance on where the energy radiates from or how. The caption says "it pulses" but the image doesn't. A storyboard cannot outsource its storytelling to caption text.

**Score: C** — The concept works. The execution of it as a *board panel* does not. Needs a redraw with corrected placement and pulse indication.

---

### P11 — Nose to Nose (CU)

**What's there:** Close-up of Luma's face — large round head, two white eyes snapping open, brown skin tones, minimal features. The Glitchkin pixel floats at nose level to her right. Concentric cyan rings radiate outward from the central meeting point. Caption: "Eyes snap open. Zero anticipation. Nose to nose. BEAT."

**Does it communicate the shot?** Yes. This is the best panel on the sheet. I feel the BEAT. The "zero anticipation" note is correctly visualized — those eyes are *snapped open*, not widening, not in mid-blink. The suddenness is baked into the static image. That's good boarding.

**Is the staging clear?** The concentric rings are a smart choice for communicating the energetic collision of these two presences — girl and pixel, two worlds touching for the first time. The radial composition naturally centers the eye at the moment of contact. This is intentional staging.

**Is there energy?** Yes. This is the only panel on the sheet where I can *feel* something. There's held breath in this image. The scale relationship — Luma's massive face against the tiny pixel — is correct and gives the Glitchkin enormous power despite its size. Small thing, enormous force.

**What works:** The eyes. The scale contrast. The concentric rings as energy indicator. The shot framing at CU is exactly right — we're in Luma's face, there's no escape, this moment is everything.

**What fails:** Luma's expression is blank beyond the open eyes. I understand the minimal design language, but "zero anticipation / BEAT" is a moment that carries emotional weight — shock, wonder, terror, or some collision of all three. The face gives me nothing but "eyes open." Even within this minimalist style, there should be a micro-expression communicable. A slight parting of a mouth shape. A lean-back in the neck. The body language available is unused.

**Score: A-** — The strongest panel. Proves the nose-to-nose concept works visually. Needs one more beat of expression to earn the full grade.

---

### P13 — The Scream (MED WID)

**What's there:** A multi-panel split — three vertical sections visible plus a partial fourth. Luma on cyan background, arms splayed in starburst pose, left side. A solid magenta panel, center (empty, apparently representing the scream itself as negative space or audio impact). A teal panel with the Glitchkin floating, right. A purple partial panel far right. Radial energy lines burst from multiple points. Caption: "Simultaneous scream. Symmetrical. Monitors activate at the sound."

**Does it communicate the shot?** Partially. The energy of chaos reads. But I don't understand what I'm looking at structurally. Is this a split-screen composition within a single animation frame? Is this four separate panels represented in one board panel? The panel label says MED WID, suggesting a single shot, but the composition reads as a title sequence split-screen, not a cartoon medium-wide shot of a child screaming.

**Is the staging clear?** No. This is my biggest problem with the sheet. If this is meant to be Luma screaming in a room and the Glitchkin reacting, I need to *see a room*. I need spatial logic — where is Luma's body in space, where is the Glitchkin relative to her, what is the camera angle. The vertical color-block approach is graphic design, not storyboarding. It communicates *mood*. It does not communicate *staging*.

**Is there energy?** Yes — the radial burst lines, the limb-splayed pose of Luma's stick figure, the collision of color panels all convey explosive energy. Viscerally this works.

**What works:** The starburst pose on Luma's silhouette is genuinely good. Arms and legs thrown wide is a universal "scream" body signal that reads across cultures and age groups. The energy lines reinforce it. The color violence — that magenta dead center — hits hard.

**What fails:** I don't know what the camera is doing. I don't know where anything is in 3D space. An animator cannot build this shot from this board. Is the camera low? Eye-level? Are we looking through a monitor? The color blocks are evocative but they're an *art direction note* wearing a storyboard panel's clothing. Those are two different things.

**Score: C+** — High visual energy, low spatial information. The emotional intent is right. The staging execution is wrong for a working storyboard. This needs a complete redraw as an actual camera-positioned medium-wide shot.

---

### P25 — Title Card (TITLE)

**What's there:** The show title "LUMA & THE GLITCHKIN" in bold cyan/white glitched typography over a dark background. "[SMASH CUT]" in brackets below. Horizontal glitch-stripe decorative elements. Caption: "SMASH CUT. Glitch construction. LUMA & THE GLITCHKIN."

**Does it communicate the shot?** Yes. Completely. Title cards are the one place where graphic design *is* the storyboard. This works.

**Is the staging clear?** For what it is, yes. The hierarchy — show title dominant, cut annotation subordinate — is correct. The glitch aesthetic is on-brand. The cyan accent colors match the established visual language perfectly.

**Is there energy?** The glitch treatment on "THE GLITCHKIN" — the chromatic aberration shift, the off-registration color layers — gives this genuine energy. It doesn't feel like a placeholder title card. It feels like the actual on-screen graphic.

**What works:** The glitch typography. The color palette consistency with the rest of the board. The "[SMASH CUT]" instruction is clear and properly formatted as a production note within the panel. The smash-cut logic — from P13's explosion of energy directly into this title — will work rhythmically.

**What fails:** This is a title card, not a storyboard challenge, so I'm grading on a different curve. My only note: the title card doesn't tell me anything about the *transition into* it. P13 ends in chaos. How does this card hit? Is it instantaneous? Does the glitch construct itself? The "[SMASH CUT]" note addresses timing but not the visual construction described in the caption. If the Glitchkin's scramble builds this title pixel by pixel, that's one set of animation notes. If it's a hard cut, that's another. The board needs to specify.

**Score: B+** — Strong as a title card. Needs one more annotation clarifying the construction method.

## Contact Sheet Assessment

Reading left to right: P01 → P03 → P11 → P13 → P25.

The emotional arc reads. I can track it — quiet mystery, anomaly discovered, confrontation, explosion, title. The pacing *suggestion* is there in the thumbnail row. Cold open logic is intact.

What the contact sheet reveals that individual panels hide: the tonal jump between P11 and P13 is enormous and abrupt. In the thumbnails, you go from the intimate, almost tender nose-to-nose moment straight into the color-block chaos of the scream with no visual transition beat. There should be an intermediate panel — or at minimum P13 should contain spatial echoes of P11's composition — to give the audience a half-second of recognition before the explosion. Right now P13 feels compositionally unrelated to everything before it. The abruptness may be intentional for comedic effect, but on the contact sheet it reads as a continuity break, not a comic beat.

The jump from P03 (single pixel on a dark screen) to P11 (nose-to-nose) is also missing connective tissue. What happened in between? How did the pixel get from the monitor to Luma's nose? That gap in the contact sheet is a gap in the story logic. Even one bridging panel — Luma leaning toward the screen, pixel floating out — would close it.

The overall pacing from thumbnail scale feels front-heavy on negative space (P01, P03 are both dark and spare) and then overcrowded with competing visual information (P13). The rhythm wants one more mid-energy moment between P03 and P11 to breathe.

## Staging Quality

Two panels demonstrate intentional character staging: P01 (the house composition) and P11 (the nose-to-nose). In both cases the characters — or in P01's case, the single light source standing in for character presence — are placed with purpose. The rules of the composition serve the story.

P03 fails at staging because the pixel is arbitrarily placed.

P13 fails at staging because it abandons camera space entirely in favor of graphic design panels.

P25 is exempt from character staging standards as a title card.

Overall staging quality: inconsistent. Two strong panels, two that need significant work, one exempt. The vocabulary exists — P11 proves someone on this team understands how to use a frame — it just isn't being applied uniformly.

---

## What These Panels Prove

For the first time, we can confirm the following with images, not just words:

1. **The cyan-as-anomaly principle works visually.** In P01, in P03, in P11 — that cyan is always the signal. The eye finds it immediately and correctly in every panel. This was described in the production bible. Now it's proven.

2. **Luma's minimalist design reads at close-up scale.** P11 shows that the simple circle-head, dot-eyes, flat-skin approach carries emotional information at CU without needing detail. The character design survives the cruelest test: an extreme close-up.

3. **The scale relationship between Luma and the Glitchkin pixel is dramatically correct.** The contrast of the girl's large head against the tiny pixel in P11 gives the pixel enormous narrative weight. Small thing, enormous scene. That works.

4. **The glitch visual language is coherent and consistent.** From the single pixel in P03 to the title card typography in P25, there is a unified visual grammar. That's a design achievement.

5. **The cold open concept is viable.** Five panels, one contact sheet, and I believe in the premise. A child, a pixel, a bedroom, a scream, a show. The structure holds.

---

## Critical Issues

**Issue 1 — P03 pixel placement.** Upper-right is compositionally wrong for the emotional intent of this moment. Redraw with pixel centered or center-left. Add radiating pulse indication — even a simple two-ring halo — so the "it pulses" action is visible in the still frame.

**Issue 2 — P13 spatial logic.** This panel cannot be handed to an animator. It communicates mood but not shot construction. What is the camera position? What is the spatial relationship between Luma and the Glitchkin in this shot? Redraw as an actual medium-wide shot with readable room geometry. The color energy can survive the redraw but the graphic-design-as-storyboard approach must go.

**Issue 3 — Missing bridging panels.** The gap between P03 and P11 leaves unexplained how the Glitchkin moved from monitor to nose-level. One panel needed. The gap between P11 and P13 is tonally jarring. One panel — or at minimum a revised P13 composition that spatially connects to P11 — needed.

**Issue 4 — Luma's expression in P11.** The eyes-open read is correct for "zero anticipation" but the face needs one additional micro-expression element to carry the full weight of the BEAT. Within the established minimalist style, find one more signal.

**Issue 5 — P25 title card construction ambiguity.** "Glitch construction" is named in the caption but not visualized as a sequence of states. Either add a two-state construction sequence (pixel chaos → title resolves) or annotate clearly whether this is a hard cut hit or a build.

## Verdict

This is a first pass that proves the concept and establishes the visual language. It is not production-ready. It is not animator-ready on panels P03 and P13. It is impressive on panel P11 in a way that gives me genuine confidence in where this board can go.

The work is real now. We are past theory. That matters enormously. But "real" is not the same as "done."

Two panels need complete redraws. Two panels need revision. One title card needs annotation. Two bridging panels need to be created from scratch. That's a week of focused work, not a month. This is fixable. This is *good* fixable, which is different from broken fixable.

The team has proven they understand the visual language. What they haven't proven yet is that they can sustain spatial rigor and staging discipline across an entire cold open — let alone a full episode. The inconsistency between P11 (which is genuinely excellent work) and P13 (which is graphic design cosplaying as a storyboard) tells me the standards are not yet consistent. A supervisor's job is to make the floor match the ceiling.

Raise the floor. Match P11's quality on every panel. Then we talk about handing this to animation.

**Final Grade: B- / 74%**
*(Weighted: two panels passing with distinction, two panels failing as working boards, one title card passing with notes.)*

I can feel the scene in panel 11. I cannot feel it in panels 3 and 13. My job — and the team's job — is to make me feel it everywhere.

Get back to work.
