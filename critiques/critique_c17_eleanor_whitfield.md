## Co-Viewing Test Statement

I am reviewing this as a person who would sit down on a Saturday morning with my grandchildren — ages 7, 9, 11, and 14 — and watch the first episode together. The question I am always asking is: would I feel comfortable in that room? Would the show respect me as much as it respects them?

## GRANDMA MIRI — Character Design (grandma_miri.md + expression sheet v002)

```
Score: 88/100
- The design document for Miri is the most fully realized piece of writing in this pitch.
  Her crow's feet as laugh lines, not tiredness; her hands as working hands; the cardigan
  that knows exactly where it folds — this is a person, not a grandmother-shaped prop.
  Whoever wrote "she has arrived at herself" understood what they were doing.
- The Pride Override (blush fading when Luma is excited) is a small detail that tells me
  the writers understand intergenerational relationships from the inside, not from outside
  looking in. That kind of subtlety does not come from observation alone. It comes from
  having been in that room.
- The expression sheet v002 with five full-body postures — WARM/WELCOMING through
  WISE/KNOWING — is correct in the right way. "SKEPTICAL/AMUSED" with arms crossed and
  a hip tilt is the expression of a woman who has already solved the problem you are about
  to describe to her. That is exactly right.
- One concern: the character document says her expressions "travel from the eyes first."
  This is true of real older adults. Whether the animators can execute this at speed is a
  production question I cannot answer from a design document, but someone should ask it
  before the character is locked.
Bottom line: Miri is written with genuine respect and I did not expect to feel as recognized as I did reading her — she is the kind of grandmother I hope I am, not the kind cartoons usually make.
```

## THE DEN — Grandma Miri's Space (LTG_ENV_luma_study_interior.png + kitchen ENV)

```
Score: 81/100
- The Luma study interior with the CRT key light, warm bedside lamp, and the Miri
  knitted toy Easter egg on the chair — these choices communicate that this room belongs
  to a person who loves someone. The knitted toy is the right kind of detail: small,
  specific, the kind of thing only a person who put it there would notice.
- The kitchen environment with the crossword puzzle, the tea mug, the circuit board
  repurposed as a doormat: I have known this woman. The accumulation of specific domestic
  objects is how you build a life on screen without narrating it.
- The MIRI fridge label (kitchen v005, C39) — the dual-Miri visual plant — is exactly
  the kind of quiet promise good storytelling makes. On first viewing it reads as a
  grocery list. On rewatch, after you know what it means, it becomes one of the most
  economical narrative seeds I have seen in a pitch package. I am genuinely pleased they
  found this solution.
- The tech den as described in the production bible — bookshelves organized by personal
  logic, circuit boards in ceramic dishes like coins — communicates that Miri has a system
  that makes complete sense once you understand it. This is the correct characterization
  of a retired engineer who never stopped thinking.
Bottom line: The environments do the work that good set design always does: they tell you who the person is before the person speaks — and for Miri, what they tell you is worth knowing.
```

## THE INTERGENERATIONAL RELATIONSHIP (cold open storyboard, production bible)

```
Score: 74/100
- "She has never once spoken to Luma like a child." I would cross a picket line for that
  sentence. It is the single most important promise this show makes to grandparents watching
  alongside grandchildren. If the show keeps it — through production, through 13 episodes —
  it will be the show it says it is.
- The cold open establishes Miri's den as a place of warmth, trust, and accumulated
  history before a single character speaks. Luma sleeping sideways on Miri's couch with
  chip puffs in her hair, the notebook mid-sentence: she ran out of energy and landed here.
  That is the posture of a child who feels completely safe. You cannot fake that staging.
- My reservation: Miri is entirely absent from the cold open panels that currently exist.
  I understand this is Luma and Byte's introduction — that is correct. But the pitch package
  as a whole contains almost no scenes where Miri and Luma share a frame. The character
  design document describes their visual relationship in specific and beautiful terms
  (the shared amber eyes, the garment color family, "she came from here"). I have not seen
  this demonstrated in any rendered asset. The promise exists; the proof is still owed.
- Taraji Coleman's note (C15) that "Grandma Miri has never once spoken to Luma like a
  child" is the line she wants stitched on a wall. I agree. But stitching it on a wall is
  not enough. I need to see it in a scene.
Bottom line: The promise of this relationship is the best thing in the pitch; the demonstration of it in actual rendered frames is the largest gap in the package.
```

## THE CRT TELEVISION — Treatment and Meaning

```
Score: 91/100
- The CRT as the show's central object is the most emotionally correct decision in this
  entire pitch. Not a phone. Not a screen. A CRT — warm, analog, slightly dangerous,
  belonging to an older person who kept it when everyone else threw theirs away. I kept
  mine. I know exactly why Miri kept hers.
- The progression from static to single cyan pixel to pressing face to breakthrough
  (Panels 03 through 08) is the best-paced sequence in the storyboard. Each panel earns
  the next. The "impossibly vivid" cyan pixel in a frame of warm analog static is the
  correct visual metaphor for discovery: one wrong color in the right place.
- The CRT as connection point between Miri and the Glitch Layer elder — the dual-Miri
  plant — means the television Miri kept is the television that matters. She did not keep
  it because she is eccentric. She kept it because she knew. That is a grandmother who has
  been carrying a secret for a long time and waiting for the right person to be ready.
  That is a real character.
- The only risk: if the CRT is treated, at any point, as an eccentric prop rather than
  as the repository of Miri's history, the audience will notice. The show's central object
  must be treated with the same seriousness the design documents give it.
Bottom line: The CRT is handled with genuine affection and narrative intelligence — it is not a prop, it is a character in the set, and the show knows this.
```

## STORY STRUCTURE AND EMOTIONAL BEATS (production bible, pitch brief)

```
Score: 77/100
- "Luma is the kid nobody sees" is a real premise. Not a concept, not a logline
  constructed to appeal to a demographic — a real situation that real children live.
  The show earns the right to that premise by not resolving it too quickly.
- "The way she sees the world is not a quirk — it is the only reason any of this is
  possible." This is what children's literature does when it is working. It tells the
  child that the thing they were embarrassed about is the thing that matters. I have
  watched children realize this about themselves in reading programs. It does not get
  old. The show is right to make this its emotional center.
- Taraji Coleman flagged that Luma's interiority is tidied up — that she stops doubting
  herself too quickly. I agree, and I want to add: the same risk exists for Miri. The
  design document gives her six expressions, including CONCERN (described as "a worry she
  hoped she would never have to have"). If Miri is carrying a secret about the Glitch
  Layer, there will be scenes where she watches Luma move toward danger she has been
  trying to prevent. Those scenes need to use the CONCERN expression — not as a hint to
  the audience, but as the honest cost of keeping that secret. The show should make
  grandparents feel the specific weight of knowing more than you can say.
- Byte's cracked eye as an involuntary honesty tell — glitching when he is pretending not
  to care — is the best single character idea in this pitch. He is a machine that cannot
  quite maintain the lie. I know what it is to watch someone try very hard not to show
  you something and fail. The show should use this.
Bottom line: The story is emotionally honest in its premises and at risk of being emotionally tidy in its execution — the harder version of each relationship is available, and the show should not protect the characters from it.
```

## CO-VIEWING TEST — Would I Watch This With My Grandchildren?

Yes. Without reservation.

I would be watching Miri more than they would. They would be watching Byte. We would agree that SF03's floating geometry is actually cool, that SF01's warm room with the single cyan pixel is the best image in the pitch, and that whoever designed Byte's cracked eye was paying attention to something true about the people who try hardest not to need you.

I would not feel like the show's butt of the joke. I would not feel condescended to. I would feel like the show knows that grandmothers are people who were young once and kept things — objects, secrets, old televisions — for reasons that took a long time to become clear.

The one thing I would tell the children next to me: "The grandmother knows more than she's saying." They would believe me. And they would be right.

---

## OVERALL SCORE: 80 / 100

**What is strong:** Miri's character design and written specification. The CRT television as narrative object. The dual-Miri visual plant. The Pride Override. The cold open's staging of Luma's sleep as a portrait of safety. The emotional premise that seeing differently is not a flaw.

**What is owed:** A rendered frame showing Miri and Luma together — the "she came from here" composition the design document describes but no asset currently demonstrates. And one scene where Miri's CONCERN expression means what the design document says it means: the cost of carrying a secret for someone you love.
