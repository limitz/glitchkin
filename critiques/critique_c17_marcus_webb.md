# Critique C17 — Marcus Webb
## "Luma & the Glitchkin" — Conceptual Integrity & Visual Originality Review

**Critic:** Marcus Webb, Concept Originator and Visual Cliché Executioner
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Cycle:** 17 (post-C42 work)
**Assets Reviewed:** All pitch assets — style frames SF01–SF05, character lineup v008, character expression sheets, environments, storyboard panels, logo, story bible v003

---

## Preamble

I have not reviewed this project since Cycle 7. Forty-two work cycles have elapsed. I am reading forty-two cycles of decisions that have compounded, amended each other, and in some cases quietly solved problems they created. I will not pretend to have tracked every cycle. What I will do is ask the question I always ask: does the irreducible visual idea of this show still exist? Can I state it in one sentence? Does every major asset serve that sentence?

The sentence, as best as I can reconstruct it from what is in front of me:

**"A girl whose way of seeing is a mechanism, not a flaw, discovers a corrupted digital dimension that exists because things break in specific and meaningful ways."**

That is a sentence worth building a show around. My question is whether the assets in this pitch are built around it, or whether they are built around the *genre* that sentence inhabits — and hoping the originality is implied.

## SF01 — "The Discovery"
`LTG_COLOR_styleframe_discovery.png`

Score: 72
- The warm/cold split lighting is the single most executed idea in this pitch — Luma as the literal meeting point between warm Real World amber and Electric Cyan glitch light. Her hoodie encoding both palettes on its fabric is genuinely irreducible to its influences. This was the right instinct and it was followed through.
- The composition — two hands almost touching across a threshold — is one of the oldest images in visual storytelling. *E.T.* is the most recent prominent example; Michelangelo is the origin. This team has not found a way to make it theirs. The moment is narratively correct but visually familiar in a way that works against the show's claim to specificity.
- Color fidelity WARNs from QA (BYTE_TEAL LAB ΔE=22.83, UV_PURPLE ΔE=36.19) are a production concern but not an originality concern. I note them because they suggest the output does not yet fully match the intentional palette — and that palette is doing the conceptual work.

Bottom line: The strongest idea in the pitch — Luma as threshold — lands in this frame, but the chosen composition is borrowed from the cultural archive without transformation.

## SF02 — "Glitch Storm"
`LTG_COLOR_styleframe_glitch_storm.png`

Score: 55
- "Small characters running from a large threat, wide shot, Dutch angle" is not a visual idea — it is a format. It has appeared in every action-adventure animated pitch since *The Iron Giant*, and the Dutch angle has been a shorthand for "things are wrong" since Welles. The Glitch Storm sky is rendered with care but the image could belong to a dozen other shows.
- The one thing that should make this frame specific — the Glitch Layer contaminating the Real World's color (building facades cooling from Terracotta to ENV-06 gray-green) — is present in the spec but fights the QA results. SUNLIT_AMBER shows LAB ΔE=47.04, the largest deviation in the batch. The most conceptually interesting element of the frame (the warm world losing its warmth to the invasion) is also the element most at risk from execution drift.
- Byte "in storm-scene VOID_BLACK body variant" is a production decision that flattens the character's most distinctive design element. Byte's BYTE_TEAL oval body is the thing that makes him readable. A void-black Byte in a void-dark frame is a character design trade-off made for atmospheric reasons, and it costs more than it gives.

Bottom line: This frame shows a storm but not a show — the premise-specific visual language (palette invasion, the warm world being outgunned) is present in the documentation but does not yet dominate the image.

## SF03 — "The Other Side"
`LTG_COLOR_styleframe_otherside.png`

Score: 66
- The Glitch Layer as "a failing operating system with patches that contradict each other" is a genuinely original world concept. The visual execution — platform depth system, circuit trace floors, pixel-art flora, ring megastructure — is ambitious and internally consistent. The "Other Side is ordered silence, not chaos" distinction from the bible is critically important and the palette supports it correctly: UV_PURPLE ambient with zero warm tones.
- Inverted atmospheric perspective (far objects brighter, not darker) is the single most specific visual idea in any of the environments. It is the decision that could only belong to this show. I want to see it more prominent, not buried in specification documents.
- The "floating slabs + data waterfall + platform" spatial grammar reads adjacent to the cyberpunk digital dimension genre: *Tron* (1982), *Reboot* (1994), *Steven Universe*'s Gem Homeworld. The team is working in a lineage they have not fully acknowledged or transcended. The pixel flora and the anti-gravity confetti are the differentiating gestures — they suggest a world that is *alive despite being broken*, which is the show's thesis. More of this, less generic floating geometry.

Bottom line: The most original visual environment in the pitch, but it is still in conversation with the digital dimension genre rather than past it.

## SF04 — "Resolution"
`LTG_COLOR_styleframe_sf04.png` (canonical C42)

Score: 74
- The post-crossing kitchen with LAMP_AMBER at GL-07 as narrative contamination is the most conceptually sophisticated decision in the entire pitch. The Real World bearing marks of the crossing — not through spectacle but through a barely perceptible hue shift in the lamp — is the kind of visual subtlety that earns repeat viewings. Sam Kowalski's color review correctly identifies this as internally consistent narrative logic, not a production error.
- Byte as faded CRT ghost in a doorway is the right image for this character's emotional position at this point in the story. The desaturation (from GL-01b to a dimmer approximation) is doing narrative work. This is one of the few character-staging decisions in the pitch that I cannot reduce to its influences.
- Warm/cool 13.2 barely clears the 12.0 threshold. The margin worries me. The entire conceptual argument of SF04 depends on the warm world being recognizably warm even when marked — 1.2 points of separation between the intended reading and a flat palette is not a comfortable place to be.

Bottom line: The best single frame in the pitch — the only one where the visual choices are doing work that could not be done in a different show.

## SF05 — "Covetous Glitch"
`LTG_COLOR_sf_covetous_glitch.png` (v2.0.0, C42)

Score: 58
- Three-character triangulation (Glitch left, Byte barrier, Luma right) is a staging grammar this team has used before — the character lineup uses similar spatial logic. The triangulation communicates the Byte/Glitch relationship correctly but the image lacks the *specific* tension the story bible says this relationship contains. Byte and Glitch are "the same Glitchkin who made different choices when the Corruption appeared." That history is in the spec. I cannot find it in the image.
- G001/G004/G008 spec violations were fixed in C42. The Glitch design itself — diamond body, CORRUPT_AMBER, HOT_MAGENTA crack, organized geometry as horror — is the single most original character design in the pitch. It is the only character whose appearance could not plausibly exist in any other show I can name. The organized-is-scarier-than-chaotic visual logic for Corruption is exactly the right inversion.
- COVETOUS expression as an "interior state" (G008 bilateral eye rule: identical left+right glyphs) is a meaningful specification that distinguishes this character's emotional grammar from human emotional grammar. That distinction should be the image's thesis. It is not yet foregrounded.

Bottom line: Glitch is the most originally designed character in this pitch and the COVETOUS frame does not yet use that originality to its full potential.

## Character Lineup v008
`LTG_CHAR_character_lineup.png`

Score: 64
- Two-tier ground plane staging (C42, Maya Santos) is a real structural improvement — it communicates depth and scale relationships that a single baseline cannot. RPD PASS, silhouette PASS.
- The lineup continues to present five characters who, as a group, do not announce themselves as the cast of a specific show. Luma's circle language, Byte's oval, Glitch's diamond — these are distinct from each other. But Cosmo (rounded rectangle, glasses, lavender) and Miri (compact, cardigan) read as residents of the animated middle-school-drama genre, not as characters with a visual claim to this specific story. The line between "supporting character who fits the world" and "character who could be in any show" has not been clearly drawn for these two.
- G006 WARNs from the Glitch Spec Lint (organic warm fills detected in character_lineup.py) suggest the Glitch character's correct palette is not fully implemented at lineup scale. The lineup is the first image in any pitch deck. It must not contain palette drift on the antagonist.

Bottom line: Luma and Byte pass the "could only belong to this show" test; Glitch passes with reservations; Cosmo and Miri do not yet pass it.

## Logo
`LTG_BRAND_logo_asymmetric.png`

Score: 68
- The "&" connector gradient (SUNLIT_AMBER to ELEC_CYAN) is the correct visual argument — it names the show's central tension in a single character. The asymmetric type treatment communicates digital corruption without being a generic glitch-font (a category that has been exhausted since 2015).
- Color fidelity WARN from QA. The logo is the brand anchor — palette drift here is higher-priority than drift on a single environment background.
- The logo communicates "this is a digital/glitch show" with moderate specificity. It does not yet communicate "this is a show about a girl whose perception is the mechanism." That is a harder brief for a logo, and I do not have a specific fix, but I am noting the gap.

Bottom line: Competent and appropriately distinctive logo treatment; the "&" gradient is doing real conceptual work but the whole is not yet irreducible to its genre.

## Story Bible v003 — Concept Assessment

This document is the strongest single asset in the pitch and I am not asked to score it visually. But I want to note what it is doing conceptually, because it creates obligations the visual assets have not fully met.

The logline's specific claim — "the only way to save both worlds is to trust a grumpy, six-inch rogue named Byte, who would rather not be saved at all" — is a character premise, not a genre premise. Byte's specific relationship to Glitch ("the version of himself that gave in") is original and painful in the way all useful character backstory is. The Corruption's visual signature being organized geometry rather than chaos is a concept that could not have been arrived at generically — it required someone to ask what is scarier than broken, and answer: fixed.

The visual assets do not yet fully cash the cheque the bible has written. The assets, taken as a group, are working harder at the genre than at the concept. The spec documents are where the specific ideas live; the PNGs are where the genre conventions live. That gap is the project's primary problem and it is not small.

## Summary: The Irreducible Idea Test

**What this show is that no other show is:**
1. A world whose corruption is ordered rather than chaotic — organized silence as horror
2. A CRT television as multi-generational heirloom, not MacGuffin — the technology of seeing passed down between women who notice
3. A protagonist whose perceptual difference is the *mechanism*, not the obstacle — the show validates her way of seeing by building a world that can only be reached by it
4. A companion whose emotional arc is "belonging is not surrender" — and whose antagonist is the version of himself that made the other choice

**How many of these survive into the visual pitch?**
- Item 1: Partially. Glitch's diamond body and organized amber geometry points at it. SF03 spec documents it. The images do not yet foreground it.
- Item 2: Absent from visuals. The CRT is present as a prop; its generational weight is not visually encoded anywhere in the pitch. This is the show's most original image — a grandmother's television as a portal to a world that grandmothers understand differently — and it is not a style frame.
- Item 3: Present in SF01 and SF04. These are the two highest-scoring frames. This is not a coincidence.
- Item 4: Present in the bible. Not present in any image.

**Overall pitch assessment:** The conceptual foundations are original and, on their best days, irreducible. The visual execution is currently serving the concept approximately 40% of the time and serving the genre the remaining 60%. C42 is a meaningful improvement over the early cycles — the color system is more disciplined, the character expressions are more specific, the environments have genuine atmospheric intelligence. But the pitch is not yet arguing for itself on the terms of its own originality. It is asking reviewers to look past the genre conventions to the specific idea underneath. That is a losing argument in a pitch room.

**The single highest-priority intervention:** Build a style frame for the CRT as heirloom — the object, its history, the specific quality of its static, what Grandma Miri sees in it that others don't. This is the show's most original image and it does not exist in the pitch package.
