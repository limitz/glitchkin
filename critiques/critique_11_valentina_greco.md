# Critique — "Luma & the Glitchkin" Pitch Package
## Critic: Valentina Greco, Visual Development Director
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Cycle:** 11 (Critique Cycle)
**Package reviewed:** Pitch Package v001 — Style Frames SF01/SF02/SF03 (all versions including _styled), Character Lineup v004, Expression Sheets (Luma v004, Byte v004, Grandma Miri v002, Glitch v002), Pitch Brief v001, Pitch Package Index (Cycle 24), Art Director's Self-Assessment

---

## Overall Verdict

This is a pitch package that knows what it wants to be and has, in certain moments, achieved it. The color arc from warm to contested to cold is conceptually rigorous and occasionally beautiful. The Art Director understands what story they are telling.

But there is a distance — and I want to be very precise about this — between the conceptual intelligence of the written documents and what actually appears on screen. The spec documents for these style frames are among the most thoughtful I have read on a production of this scale. The rendered frames are not worthy of them. Not yet. They are competent. They are correct in the technical sense. They are not — with one significant exception — extraordinary.

A studio executive flipping through this deck will be interested. They will not be stopped cold by something they have never seen before. That is the gap. That gap must be closed.

**Grade: B+**
Strong enough to get a meeting. Not strong enough to close one.

## 1. Concept-to-Execution Gap Analysis

### What the Brief Promises

The pitch brief's Visual Identity Statement says: *"Luma's world is rendered in amber, terracotta, and cream — the palette of analogue life. The Glitch Layer is void-black, electric cyan, and UV purple — the palette of broken data. Every scene the two worlds touch, the color systems collide."*

It also promises: *"Nothing in the show looks like a 3D render. Everything looks like it was drawn by someone who loves cartoons."*

Both promises are partially kept. Neither is fully delivered.

### Where the Gap Lives

**SF01 — The Discovery:** This frame has the strongest concept-execution alignment of the three. The warm/cold split-lighting on Luma's face, the CRT as the sole point of cyan intrusion, Byte's tiny dangerous magenta eye against the cyan glow — these are exactly right as ideas, and the rendering approximates them creditably. The composition spec is sophisticated (three-light setup with the Real World literally keeping its hand on Luma's left shoulder). What the rendered frame gives me is a vector-art approximation of this, and here is my problem: it reads as stock. The depth layers are visible but not felt. The foreground desk clutter is reduced to decorative shapes rather than evidence of a life. The bookshelves behind Luma are a decorative pattern — they do not feel like someone's accumulation of decades. The warmth of this room is stated, not built.

When a frame promises *"the palette of analogue life"* I must feel the weight of things that have been touched and worn. What I see in SF01 is a palette that is technically warm but emotionally weightless.

**SF02 — Glitch Storm:** This is the frame that most honestly delivers the show's energy. The cold/warm contest is visually present — the warm window glows in the lower third genuinely fight the cyan sky above. The compositional logic is sound. But I must say something that will be uncomfortable: Luma and Cosmo, at this scale and in this rendering, are stick-figure approximations of themselves. Their silhouettes read but their bodies do not. The full-body runs lack the squash-and-stretch the spec describes. The spec says "maximum squash-and-stretch" and "classic cartoon run." What the rendered frame shows is a figure posed in a running position. That is not the same thing. The sky is correctly ominous. The characters running through it feel like paper cutouts placed in front of a beautiful painting.

**SF03 — The Other Side:** This frame is the most emotionally effective of the three, and paradoxically the simplest. The vast quiet of the Glitch Layer, Luma's small orange silhouette against the cold purple void — this works. The color story of "warmth as pigment not as light" is genuinely present. But at the scale Luma occupies in this frame (correctly, the spec is right that she should be small), she is nearly featureless. The emotion the spec describes — *"grief-adjacent wonder"* — is not readable in her face because her face, at this rendering size, is four pixels. The spec assigns her an extraordinarily specific emotional state and the rendered character cannot deliver it. This is not a failure of the concept. It is a failure to solve the storytelling problem that the spec correctly identifies and then does not solve.

## 2. What the Pitch Package Communicates vs. What It Should

### What It Currently Communicates

- A coherent two-world color grammar with clear internal logic.
- A protagonist who is warm, round, and kinetic in isolation, but who becomes generic in action.
- A Glitch Layer that is visually distinctive and spatially consistent.
- A supporting cast that exists.
- A team that has done a great deal of revising.

### What It Should Communicate

- A show with a distinctive visual voice that cannot be confused with anything currently in production.
- Characters whose personality is legible at every scale, in every context, without consulting the spec.
- A world that feels inhabited, not designed.
- The specific emotional register of the show's central relationship — Luma and Byte — which is, per the brief, built on trust between an unlikely pair, one of whom is technically imaginary.

I have now reviewed the character sheets, the lineup, the expression sheets, and all three style frames. In none of them do I see Luma and Byte in relationship. I see Luma. I see Byte. I see them occupying the same frame. The most important emotional truth of this show — that these two unlikely beings choose each other — is not present in the visual package. The brief articulates it precisely. The visuals do not demonstrate it.

This is the most significant gap in the entire pitch package.

## 3. Specific Required Improvements — Numbered Failures

**Failure 1: The relationship between Luma and Byte has no visual home in this package.**

SF01 has them almost touching across a threshold. That is a correct and even beautiful image. But the almost-touching is a compositional device, not an emotional statement. The rendered execution does not render the moment as charged — it renders it as a composition. I do not feel what it costs these two characters to bridge this distance. In the spec, Byte is being pulled through against his will. His reluctance is the source of everything funny and everything sad about this show. The rendered image does not convey reluctance. It conveys: a small character near a large screen.

I require a dedicated character interaction pose or key image — not a style frame, but a character illustration — in which Luma and Byte are in the same shot at a readable scale and we understand from their body language alone that they do not entirely trust each other and cannot stop gravitating toward each other anyway. This is the show. It must be in the pitch package.

**Failure 2: The styled frames (the _styled.png deliverables) are not ready and should not be in this index as pitch assets.**

The SF02 and SF03 stylized PNGs are flagged in the team's own documents as having hue rotation artifacts. The stylization fidelity report from Cycle 24 acknowledges SF02 and SF03 stylized PNGs require rework. Yet the pitch package index presents the _styled deliverables as current. A critic looking at the package for the first time is receiving assets the team has already internally flagged as failing. This is not a minor housekeeping issue. This is the pitch deck presenting known-defective material as finished work. I will not accept this, and neither will any studio executive who asks to see the full package.

Either repair the styled frames or remove them from the pitch deck index. The unstyled base versions (SF01 v003, SF02 v005, SF03 v003) are pitch-ready by the team's own assessment. Use those.

**Failure 3: The Glitch expression sheet is an embarrassment at this scale.**

I have now seen the Glitch expression sheet v002 with my own eyes. The character, at the rendered size on that 800x800 canvas, occupies approximately the area of a postage stamp in each quadrant. The four expressions — NEUTRAL, MISCHIEVOUS, PANICKED, TRIUMPHANT — are distinguishable only by leaning close to the screen. The MISCHIEVOUS and TRIUMPHANT panels have different background color tones (dark maroon vs. dark olive-brown) which does more work to distinguish the expressions than the character's face geometry does.

The Art Director's self-assessment correctly identifies this as a functional failure, not an aesthetic preference. That is exactly right. And since it has been identified, it must be fixed before this goes in front of any buyer. The Glitch character is the antagonist of the show. It is being undersold by a factor of approximately four.

**Failure 4: Luma's character design is not consistent between the expression sheets and the style frames.**

In the expression sheet v004, Luma has a dark, full beret hat that occupies significant head real estate. In SF01, the character rendered in that frame has hair — not a beret. Her skin tone in the expression sheets (a warm brown, correctly rendered) and her skin tone in SF01 read as different characters. The character who appears in the style frames and the character who appears in the expression sheets are not obviously the same person. A studio executive comparing these two assets will ask this question. There is no clean answer available in the current package.

The show bible promises consistency. The character design package does not deliver it across its own internal assets.

**Failure 5: The Grandma Miri expression sheet, while improved, does not communicate who Miri is to the story.**

The v002 rebuild is technically competent. Five full-body poses, posture silhouettes, clear emotional range. But Miri's narrative function — she is the bridge character, the grandmother who has been quietly aware of the Glitch Layer for years without fully understanding it — is absent from these expressions. What I see is a warm, expressive grandmother. What I need to see is a warm, expressive grandmother who has a secret. The WISE/KNOWING pose is closest to this, but it is indistinguishable from generic-grandmother-being-wise. The spec says: *"foreshadows the Act 2 emotional turn without announcing it."* The expression sheet does not serve this function. It serves the function of: grandmother who has five feelings.

**Failure 6: The color story document is more compelling than the frames that illustrate it.**

The ltg_style_frame_color_story.md document articulates the three-sentence arc — *"SF01: This is Luma's world. SF02: Neither world owns this frame. SF03: This is not Luma's world."* — with genuine elegance. It is excellent writing about a visual grammar. The problem is that I would rather read this document than look at the frames. The frames should make the document unnecessary. A pitch package's visual assets should make the explainer text redundant, not essential.

I am not saying the frames are bad. SF01 and SF03 have real moments of visual intelligence. I am saying the written argument is stronger than the visual argument. In a pitch setting, the visual argument is the only one that matters. Studio executives do not read the spec documents.

**Failure 7: The show logo question remains unresolved.**

The Art Director's self-assessment acknowledges the show_logo.png vs. LTG_BRAND_logo_asymmetric.png situation. The pitch package index still carries a caveat note on this. After 24 cycles of production, there is no canonical external-facing show logo. This is not a polish issue. It is the first thing a buyer sees on a title card. The show has no face. It needs one. Pick one. Document it. Remove the caveat. Do this before any pitch meeting.

## 4. What Is Genuinely Strong — The One Generous Remark

I have read the style frame color specifications, and they are — without qualification — the best-written production color documents I have seen on a project of this scale. The pixel confetti governing physics rule (source-origin, size/density gradient, no autonomous clustering), the three-light-setup semantic logic in SF01, the "warmth as pigment not as light" framework in SF03, the Corrupted Amber outline exception with its scene-threshold trigger — these are the work of someone who understands that color in animation is not decoration. It is narrative. The thinking is precise, it is consistent across all three frames, and it has a genuine emotional intelligence behind it.

The color arc — warm dominates, warm and cold contest, cold dominates and warmth survives only as pigment memory — is the kind of structural visual poetry that great animated shows are built on. It is not accidental. It is earned through revision and rigorous specification.

This is the foundation. Everything I have criticized is built on top of this foundation and can, in principle, be corrected. The foundation is sound.

---

## 5. Summary — What Must Be Fixed Before Pitch

In order of priority:

1. **Create a character interaction key image: Luma and Byte, readable scale, conveying the central relationship.** This is not optional. It is the show.
2. **Resolve the logo question. One canonical external logo. No caveats in the index.** This is day one work that should have been completed.
3. **Remove or fix the _styled.png versions of SF02 and SF03 from the pitch index.** Known-defective assets in a pitch deck are worse than no assets.
4. **Rebuild the Glitch expression sheet at a larger canvas with adequate character scale.** The antagonist must be legible.
5. **Audit and reconcile the visual consistency of Luma's character across expression sheets and style frames.** She must look like the same person.
6. **Revisit Miri's WISE/KNOWING expression** to add the quality of a character carrying a secret.
7. **Address the emotional flatness of SF01's environment rendering.** The room must feel lived-in, not art-directed.

The visual development team has done substantial work over 24 production cycles. Some of that work is excellent. The color system is excellent. The character design intentions are excellent. The execution of those intentions, under the pressure of actually delivering rendered frames, has produced results that are competent but not yet singular.

Singular is what a pitch must be.

*A beautiful frame that doesn't serve the story is not good design. It is decoration. I am not here to admire your decoration. Fix the relationship, fix the logo, fix the antagonist, and then we will talk about what this show can be.*
