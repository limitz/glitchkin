# Critique — Cycle 12 (First Review)
**Critic:** Nkechi Adeyemi — Target Audience and Emotional Resonance Specialist
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Subject:** *Luma & the Glitchkin* — Pitch Package Emotional Content Review

---

## Opening Statement

I come to this pitch package with one question: will a ten-year-old care?

Not: will they be entertained. Not: will the parents sitting beside them find it charming. Not: will it hold their attention for ninety seconds before something else does. I mean: will a child feel something in their chest when Luma is in danger? Will they recognise something true about themselves when Luma and Byte look at each other? Will this show know what it is like to be twelve?

After reading everything in this package, my answer is: sometimes yes, sometimes emphatically not yet. There is genuine potential here. There is also a design tendency that consistently substitutes legible emotion for legible action, and those are not the same thing. I will explain what I mean as I go.

---

## 1. The Emotional Premise — What the Pitch Brief Tells Us

The pitch brief is thin. This is its central failure and it underlies every problem I raise below.

The brief tells me *what happens*: Luma discovers Glitchkin via a glitching CRT, Byte needs to escape a corrupting force, the real world is at risk. It tells me the *tone*: comedy-adventure, warm, fast, funny. It tells me the *visual identity*: amber vs void-black, analogue vs digital.

What it does not tell me is: **what does Luma want that she cannot get in her ordinary life?**

"Speaks directly to kids who feel like they see the world differently than everyone around them" — this is the only emotional sentence in the entire document. It is also a description of approximately every animated series made in the last twenty years. It tells me nothing specific about Luma. It tells me nothing about what she is carrying before she ever finds that television.

A brief that cannot answer the question "what does Luma need that she cannot name?" is a brief that has not yet found its story. The adventure premise is clear. The emotional premise is absent.

**What is missing:** Does Luma feel invisible? Does she feel too much? Is she grieving something? Does she have a friendship that is failing her? Does she feel like she is strange in a way that has cost her something real? The show apparently targets children who "see the world differently" — but seeing the world differently can mean a thousand things. A 9-year-old and a 12-year-old process that feeling very differently. The brief needs to commit to something specific and true, or Luma remains a concept rather than a person.

**Grade on emotional premise:** C — The adventure frame is solid. The interior life of the protagonist is undeveloped.

## 2. Character Expression Sheets — Emotional Legibility

### Luma (v006) — 6 expressions: CURIOUS, DETERMINED, SURPRISED, WORRIED, DELIGHTED, FRUSTRATED

Luma's expression sheet works at the level of *what the character is doing*. All six expressions are legible as actions: she is leaning, she is contracted, she is jumping, she is crossing her arms. These are readable postures.

They are significantly less legible as *who this person is*.

The six expressions chosen are correct but they are generic. CURIOUS. DETERMINED. SURPRISED. WORRIED. DELIGHTED. FRUSTRATED. These are the six emotions that appear on every children's character expression sheet. They say: "this character has the full range of human emotion." They do not say: this is specifically Luma's version of those emotions.

Here is what I mean. A 12-year-old's WORRIED is not the same as a 7-year-old's WORRIED. A child in late middle childhood worries about whether the people she trusts will let her down. She worries about whether she is embarrassing. She worries about what people think when she is not in the room. Her worry has a social layer that is completely absent from what is described. The WORRIED expression as spec'd — arms crossed over chest, weight centred, slight hunch — reads as generic concern. It does not read as someone who knows what it feels like to realise you were wrong about a person you trusted.

FRUSTRATED similarly: arms crossed, feet apart, backward lean, head lowered. This is cartoon frustration. It is not 12-year-old frustration, which often looks like very controlled stillness punctuated by a single detail (a jaw that tightens, a look that goes flat, a smile that does not reach the eyes). The expression the team has built is the adult's idea of what a child looks like when frustrated.

The strongest expression in the sheet, conceptually, is CURIOUS — forward lean, one arm reaching/pointing, weight shifted. This one has genuine specificity because the physicality is unusual. The reaching arm creates a relationship between Luma and what she is looking at. But the face needs to match: if her pupils are directed precisely at an off-screen point and her brows are raised asymmetrically (one more than the other — the way people actually look when they are caught between wonder and caution), this expression will be excellent. Based on the specification, both brows are raised symmetrically. That reads as pure surprise, not the specific alertness of a child who has just found something she has been waiting for without knowing she was waiting for it.

**What must change:** At minimum one expression on Luma's sheet must be specific to her story. A character who feels like she sees the world differently should have an expression that is her version of perception — not the standard "looking at something interesting" cartoon face, but a face that says: *she is the kind of person who notices things other people walk past.* This could be a very slight narrowing of one eye, a stillness while everything around her moves, a mouth that is almost smiling but not quite because she is still working something out. The expression sheet currently shows a child. It does not yet show *this* child.

**Emotional legibility grade: B-**
Strong on action, weak on interiority. Luma's physicality communicates clearly. Her face does not yet distinguish her from every other cartoon protagonist with big eyes and cloud hair.

### Byte (v004) — 9 expressions: NEUTRAL, GRUMPY, SEARCHING, ALARMED, RELUCTANT JOY, CONFUSED, POWERED DOWN, RESIGNED, STORM/CRACKED

This is the most emotionally sophisticated character sheet in the package. It is also the most successful.

RELUCTANT JOY is doing exactly what it should. The asymmetric body — one arm resisting the upward joy impulse, one arm hanging — captures something that a child knows viscerally: the feeling of something good happening to you that you are not ready to admit feels good. This is emotionally honest. It respects the audience. A child who has ever been in a fight with a friend and then accidentally laughed at something funny knows this state. The design earns it.

RESIGNED is strong. Body tilt, both arms hanging at the same level, the droopy eye — this communicates a specific kind of giving-up that is different from POWERED DOWN (which is shutdown, not defeat). The distinction matters. Children know the difference between being switched off and having decided to stop caring. The design knows the difference too.

STORM/CRACKED — the damaged state — is where the sheet becomes genuinely interesting. The arm asymmetry (6 and 22 — a 16-unit differential) reads as a body that is no longer functioning consistently. The 7x7 dead-pixel glyph replacing one eye is an excellent visual choice for this audience: children understand broken things. They understand the specific sadness of a device that is trying to work and cannot. This expression is emotionally legible to a nine-year-old in a way that many designed "damaged" states are not, because it codes as recognisable malfunction rather than dramatic injury.

**What needs improving:** The range from NEUTRAL/DEFAULT through GRUMPY, SEARCHING, ALARMED are all defensive or reactive states. All of Byte's positive emotional range — everything that is not wariness, not damage, not resignation — is compressed into RELUCTANT JOY. This means that Byte's emotional vocabulary currently skews heavily toward the negative. For a character who is the *emotional partner* of the story, this creates a risk: if Byte can only be reluctantly warm, the friendship between Luma and Byte will feel like work to watch. A child needs to see that being close to Byte is worth the effort. There needs to be an expression — perhaps a CALM expression, an unguarded moment, a state where Byte is simply *present* and not suspicious — that shows the child: this is what Byte looks like when they trust someone. Without this, the friendship's reward is invisible.

**Emotional legibility grade: A-**
Technically precise emotional design. The RELUCTANT JOY and RESIGNED states are genuinely excellent. Needs one unguarded warmth state to make the friendship feel worthwhile.

### Grandma Miri (v003) — 6 expressions including KNOWING STILLNESS (NARRATIVE)

Miri's sheet is a significant improvement over what came before it, and the addition of KNOWING STILLNESS is exactly the right instinct.

The decision to mark it as a NARRATIVE EXPRESSION is important. It acknowledges that Miri is not just a support character — she is a character with a secret, and that secret changes how we read every scene she is in. For a child audience, an adult who quietly knows more than they are letting on is a powerful figure. Children live in a world run by adults who know things they are not telling them. Miri taps into that dynamic at a level that is potentially very rich.

The KNOWING STILLNESS spec — body completely still, weighted glance directed oblique/downward, heavy-lidded eyes, suppressed smile at one corner — is correct. This is what a person looks like when they are containing knowledge. The slightly cooler background for this panel versus WISE is the right instinct.

What I would push the team on: is the suppressed smile too much? A fully suppressed smile that a viewer can see is a slightly different thing from a suppressed smile that only reveals itself over time. If this is Miri's secret, the viewer should not be certain of it in the pitch package. They should feel that something is there without being able to name it. The current spec risks being too legible — which undercuts the secret.

**Emotional legibility grade: A-**
The KNOWING STILLNESS is the single most narratively sophisticated expression choice in this entire package. Do not over-signal it.

### Glitch (v002) — 6 expressions: NEUTRAL, MISCHIEVOUS, PANICKED, TRIUMPHANT, STUNNED, CALCULATING

I have read this sheet carefully and I want to be direct: Glitch is not yet a convincing antagonist for children aged 9–12.

Not because Glitch is frightening — the design is bold and its pixel-glyph eye language is genuinely interesting. The problem is more serious: **Glitch has no interior life that a child can access.** Every one of the six expressions is a performance state. MISCHIEVOUS means "doing something wrong." TRIUMPHANT means "winning." CALCULATING means "planning something." PANICKED means "losing." None of these states tells me what Glitch *wants* in a way that a child can understand from the inside.

The best antagonists for this age group are not the ones children fear most — they are the ones children understand most. A 10-year-old does not find pure malevolence frightening; they find it boring, or they find it incredible in the literal sense (they do not believe it). What frightens a child, and what holds their attention, is an antagonist who wants something they can recognise. Wanting to win. Wanting to not be replaced. Wanting to be the most important thing in a room. Wanting to matter.

None of Glitch's expressions suggest this. CALCULATING reads as scheming toward a goal we do not know. MISCHIEVOUS reads as enjoying causing trouble for its own sake. These are the expressions of a cartoon villain, not a character. A cartoon villain is something a child watches happen. A character is something a child feels.

The STUNNED expression is the most interesting one on the sheet, and I suspect not by accident. A Glitch who is STUNNED is a Glitch who was surprised — which means Glitch had expectations. A character who can be surprised has an inner model of the world. That is more interesting than any amount of scheming. The team should ask: what did Glitch expect, and what happened instead? That question contains a character.

**Is there warmth or complexity?** There is none visible in these six expressions. The crack that runs through the body is present in all states, which is visually interesting, but it is always presented as a mark of Glitch's nature rather than a mark of damage — something that happened to Glitch, something that cost Glitch something. If the crack is damage, Glitch is more interesting. If the crack is just decoration, Glitch is a design.

**Emotional legibility grade: C+**
Visually striking and well-differentiated at the silhouette level. Emotionally hollow. Glitch needs one expression — not on this sheet necessarily, but somewhere in the design documentation — that reveals what Glitch is afraid of or what Glitch has lost. Without this, Glitch is a backdrop for the real story rather than a participant in it.

## 3. Style Frames as Emotional Story

### SF01 — Discovery

The composition of this frame places Luma leaning forward on the couch, looking at a bank of monitors, with Byte's ghost-form visible in two of them. The lean offset (48px in the generator) and the reaching arm are correct instincts — they show directed attention, which is the physical form of curiosity.

The face as described in the generator code is open-mouthed, broadly smiling (a wide arc mouth with white showing inside), blush active on both cheeks. This reads as: delighted to see something exciting. That is not wrong, but it is not the specific texture of the moment the show describes.

Discovery — *the discovery*, the first time — should have uncertainty in it. A 12-year-old who discovers something impossible does not immediately smile. They go still. Their expression has a pause in it before the smile comes, because the mind is trying to process something that does not fit. The frame as described skips straight to the resolution of the emotion (delight) rather than showing its beginning (stunned, uncertain recognition).

The ghost Byte in the monitors — alpha calibrated to 90 after being too light at 55 — is a right correction. But I note that the ghost figure in the monitors is a small semi-transparent oval with two pixel dots for eyes. At the scale it is rendered, does this communicate that Byte is a *being with an interior life*, or does it communicate that there is something unusual on the screen? These are different. The first is a character; the second is a visual effect. A child needs to feel, in this frame, that what Luma is looking at is someone, not something.

**Does this feel like discovery?** Partially. The physical composition is right. The facial expression is one beat too far along the emotional journey.

**Is Luma's emotional state legible?** Yes — but it shows the aftermath of the feeling rather than the feeling arriving.

### SF02 — Glitch Storm

The storm architecture in this frame is well-constructed from a visual standpoint. UV cloud masses blocking the sky, a main crack running diagonally from top-right across the frame, sub-cracks branching from it, warm window glows in building windows competing against the cold storm light — these are all correct tools for communicating threat.

Luma is described as in "full sprint." This is where I need to apply pressure.

A child running from danger can communicate two things: fear or courage. The show's brief says this is "comedy-adventure with genuine emotional stakes." A sprint, on its own, communicates neither fear nor courage — it communicates motion. For a 9–12 year old audience, what they need to see is *why* Luma is running in that particular way. Is she running with her whole body toward something, which means she has decided to act and the fear has not caught up yet? Or is she running with her body angled away, which means she is afraid and moving on instinct? These produce completely different emotional reads from the same action.

The frame as described puts Luma at 45% across the frame, mid-sprint, with Byte floating to her left. There is no description of her face in the storm sprint — the face gets a hair stream in the wind direction (DRW_HAIR_MAGENTA trailing), a CORRUPT_AMBER outline for figure-ground contrast, and the DRW_SKIN_STORM colour suggesting the Glitch Layer's colour correction is falling on her. All of this is environmental. None of it is internal.

**Is the danger real?** The environment communicates danger clearly. The scale relationship between the tiny running figures (char_h = 18% of frame height) and the massive crack in the sky is effective — this is a child-sized person in a situation that is not child-sized.

**Is Luma's fear/courage readable?** No. Not from what is described. The sprint silhouette reads as action, not as a person making a choice. For this audience, those are different things. A child who is watching a character run from danger wants to know if the character is scared, and if so, whether they are running toward something or away from it. Currently there is no answer to this question in the frame.

### SF03 — The Other Side

The specification here is precise and atmospheric: void-black ground, inverted atmospheric perspective (things become more purple and darker with distance), Byte present in their native environment, Luma as the warm-pigment anomaly in a cold world.

This is the most conceptually sophisticated of the four frames, because its emotional content is *spatial*. The idea that warmth becomes strange here — that Luma's hoodie orange is the alien thing in this environment — is an excellent piece of visual storytelling. A child who watches this will feel, without being told, that Luma does not belong here and that what she is carrying (her warmth, her analogue world) is both a vulnerability and a kind of strength.

The critical bug fix (Byte being void black and invisible against the UV Purple background) is a serious issue that is now corrected. This is important not just technically but emotionally: Byte needs to *be seen* in their own world. If Byte is invisible in the Glitch Layer, the show has failed to give Byte a home.

**Does this feel alien and wondrous?** The specification describes what it looks like, and the inverted perspective rule is genuinely unusual — it creates a sense of wrongness that a child will register even without being able to articulate it. The circuit traces on the ground, the data slabs receding, the floating platform fragments — these are correct. The *wonder* specifically is harder to confirm because wonder requires something to be surprised by, and the frame is largely atmospheric. If Byte does something in this environment that is impossible in Luma's world — floats differently, interacts with the geometry in a way that demonstrates belonging — the wonder will follow. Right now it is an impressive landscape. It is not yet a moment.

**Does it feel alien and wondrous, not just technically correct?** It feels technically correct. The wonder needs a focal event.

### SF04 — Luma + Byte (v002)

This is the frame I was most interested in and the one I want to examine most carefully.

The scene: Luma in her bedroom at dusk, Byte on her right shoulder. Luma is leaning slightly forward with a CURIOUS expression, looking at something off-screen right. Byte is looking the *opposite direction* — up at Luma's face — with a WORRIED/skeptical expression.

This is a strong compositional instinct. The divergence of gaze direction is the right formal choice for communicating two people in a relationship who are not always looking at the same thing. It suggests: they are together, and they see the world differently from each other, and that is the story.

What I want to push on is what happens between them.

Luma's CURIOUS face — open-mouthed (arc mouth, 10° to 170°), both eyes open and directed off-screen, blush on both cheeks, raised brows with slight wobble — reads as cheerful, pleasant curiosity. It is the face of a character looking at something interesting. It is not, yet, the face of a character who has let someone into her life who was not there before.

Byte on her shoulder, looking up at Luma's face with WORRIED/skeptical: this is the emotional heart of this frame. But is it legible at the scale at which Byte sits on a shoulder? Byte needs to be large enough that the WORRIED/skeptical state reads. A small object on a shoulder can read as companion, as accessory, or as creature — but it can only read as *character* if the face is visible. If the arm asymmetry and the pixel eye state are not visible at the rendering scale, the relationship is lost.

The dual lighting (warm amber from the window left, cool monitor cyan from the right) splitting across Luma's face is excellent. This is a visual metaphor that works: she is at the intersection of both worlds and the light on her skin shows it. But the same light that makes Luma glow warm makes Byte, whose body is teal, compete with the cyan ambient from the monitor. Is Byte differentiable from the background light at shoulder scale? This is not a resolution question — it is a figure-ground emotional question.

**Does this communicate the friendship at the core of the story?** It is trying to. The gaze divergence is correct. What is missing is a single visual beat that communicates *care* between them. Looking in opposite directions communicates their different natures. But for a child to feel this is a friendship worth caring about, they need to see one moment where the figures are clearly in relationship — not just co-present. Perhaps this is in Luma's hand position (is a hand raised toward Byte? Is she half-reaching without realising it?). Perhaps it is in Byte's body orientation (angled toward Luma despite looking up at her face, not away from her). The frame is currently two characters who are near each other. It needs to be two characters who need each other.

## 4. The Core Question: Is This Pitch Package Emotionally Ready?

No. Not yet. And I want to be precise about why, because the work here is not unskilled — it is skilled work with a specific gap.

The gap is this: **the team has designed what these characters look like when they feel things, but they have not yet established what these characters need.** Those are different problems. The first is a craft problem. The second is a story problem. You can solve the first without solving the second, and that is what appears to have happened here.

Luma has six expressions and a distinctive silhouette and a warm colour palette. She does not yet have a specific interior wound that the adventure will address. Without it, the adventure is just adventure — it happens to her rather than revealing her.

Byte has technically sophisticated emotional design and a genuinely interesting emotional range. Byte is missing one unguarded moment — a state in which Byte trusts, without fighting it — that will make the friendship feel like it cost something and was worth the cost.

Glitch is visually striking and emotionally empty. This is a serious problem at the pitch stage, because Glitch is the structural opposite of Luma and should therefore reveal Luma. A Glitch whose desire is legible will deepen Luma. A Glitch whose desire is just "winning" will make Luma smaller.

Miri is the most emotionally layered character in the package. The KNOWING STILLNESS expression is the single best emotional design decision in the entire pitch. The team should look at why this expression works and ask whether they can bring that same quality of layering — knowingness, containment, something beneath the surface — to Luma's face.

**Overall emotional readiness:** B- / Not ready to pitch as a character-driven show.

Ready to pitch as a *visually distinctive adventure show.* Not ready to pitch as the show it wants to be — the one where a child sees Luma and thinks: that is what it feels like to be me.

---

## What Must Change Before This Is Ready

1. **The pitch brief must include a sentence that says, specifically, what Luma cannot get in her ordinary life.** Not "sees the world differently" — something named, specific, and true to the experience of being 12.

2. **Luma's expression sheet needs one expression that is hers alone** — not a generic emotion but the specific way Luma registers the world. I would suggest replacing one of the six with something that shows her quality of attention: a face that is almost still but watching very hard.

3. **Byte needs one unguarded warmth state.** Not reluctant joy — a state past the reluctance. A child needs to know what it looks like when Byte lets their guard down completely. Even once.

4. **Glitch needs an interior life.** One expression, one design note, one sentence in the brief: what does Glitch want that Glitch cannot have? What is Glitch afraid of? Answer this and the antagonist becomes a character.

5. **SF04 needs a single gesture or body position that communicates care, not just proximity.** Luma and Byte need to be looking at the same thing in different directions *and* need to be in visible relationship. One reaching or protective or unconsciously-close element will do it.
