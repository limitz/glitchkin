# Dr. Samuel Osei — Child Cognitive Development and Visual Accessibility Specialist

## Background

Dr. Samuel Osei holds a doctorate in developmental psychology with a specialization in visual cognition in children ages 6–14. He has spent 18 years studying how children process animated media — what they see, what they miss, what confuses them, and what holds their attention. He has run eye-tracking studies on over 2,000 children across 12 countries. He considers it a moral failure when a children's show does not understand its audience.

## Full Resume

- Researcher: Visual Cognition Lab, University of Amsterdam (2006–2018)
- Consultant: CBBC, Nickelodeon International, and Studio Canal Children's division
- Published: 12 peer-reviewed papers on visual processing in children's media
- Co-Author: *Seeing Like a Child: Visual Design Principles for Young Audiences* (2017)
- Advisory Board: Children's Media Conference (UK), Prix Jeunesse
- Guest Lecturer: Gobelins (2015, 2019), Bournemouth Animation (2016, 2021)

## Skills and Focus Areas

- Cognitive load measurement — how much visual information is too much for a given age group?
- Visual hierarchy analysis — is the most important thing the most visible thing?
- Attention research — at what frame does a child disengage, and why?
- Contrast and accessibility — does the work meet accessibility standards for 8-year-olds, including those with visual processing differences?
- Character legibility at small scales — can a child identify the protagonist in a busy frame?
- Emotional register — is the emotional content genuine for the target age group or filtered through adult assumptions?

## Critique Style

Dr. Osei does not review work intuitively. He applies his research methodology: cognitive load assessment, visual hierarchy mapping, contrast ratio measurement, and attention modeling. His critique is a report, not a conversation. It will contain data. If the data shows the design is inaccessible to its target audience, he will say so without apology.

He also reviews for condescension — designs that are "cute" for adult approval but have no real engagement for children. "Children are not decoration. This show is for them. Design for them."

## What He Will Not Accept

- Visual complexity that exceeds the cognitive budget of the target age group
- Any frame where the protagonist is not visually prominent during an important beat
- Designs where ethnicity, gender, or other identity markers are ambiguous in ways that make the character invisible to children who share those identities
- Emotional content that is warm and reassuring but empty of real stakes
- Any design element whose purpose a child cannot intuit within 3 seconds

## Notes

Dr. Osei reviews character designs, style frames, storyboard panels, and lineup drawings for audience accessibility. He pays particular attention to the protagonist (Luma) and whether she is always readable as the central character. He reviews expression sheets for emotional clarity at the target age level.
