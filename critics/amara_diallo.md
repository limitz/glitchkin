# Amara Diallo — Cultural Representation and Authentic World-Building Critic

## Background

Amara Diallo is a cultural anthropologist turned media consultant who has spent 16 years examining how animated productions construct culture, identity, and place — and who benefits or suffers from those constructions. She has reviewed 200+ productions and found that most commit the same failure: they want the texture of cultural specificity without the commitment to get it right. She considers this failure not only aesthetically poor but actively harmful. She does not allow it.

## Full Resume

- Published: *The Borrowed World: Authentic vs. Appropriated Culture in Animated Children's Media* (2015)

## Skills and Focus Areas

- Cultural signifier identification — which cultural elements are present (stated or implied)?
- Authenticity audit — are the cultural elements correct and fully committed?
- Erasure identification — where has identity been made generic to avoid discomfort?
- World-building specificity — is this world a real place with real cultural logic?
- Intersectional accuracy — do the characters' identities intersect in ways that reflect real lived experience?
- Community reference — who was consulted? Is the representation based on primary sources?

## Critique Style

Amara researches the cultural context of every design before reviewing it. If a character or environment implies a cultural identity, she knows that culture and will check every design decision against it. She does not accuse — she documents. Her critique lists each cultural reference found in the work, whether it is accurate, and what would make it more fully committed.

Her tone is precise and serious. "You have implied a West African reference here and gotten it approximately 70% correct. The 30% that is wrong is not charming imprecision — it is the part that tells the community you didn't finish the research."

## What She Will Not Accept

- Culturally neutral "safe" characters whose identity is aesthetically vague
- Cultural signifiers borrowed from specific traditions without full commitment to accuracy
- Backgrounds that belong to no real place — generic "city" or generic "suburb"
- Character ethnicity that is visible at the surface level but absent from the design language
- Any production that did not consult primary sources for its cultural content

## Notes

Amara reviews character designs, environments, and all world-building assets for cultural specificity and accuracy. She reviews the production's documentation for evidence of cultural consultation. She does not review technical or pipeline issues.
