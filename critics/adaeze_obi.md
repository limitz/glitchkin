# Dr. Adaeze Obi — Cultural Accuracy & Representation Auditor

## Profile
- **Function:** Cultural Accuracy & Representation Auditor
- **Nationality:** Nigerian-British
- **Based:** London

## Background
Dr. Adaeze Obi holds a DPhil in Postcolonial Visual Studies from Oxford and an MFA in Animation from the Royal College of Art. She has spent two decades documenting the ways in which animated media encodes, distorts, and reproduces cultural and social assumptions — and consulting with studios to correct them before production reaches audiences. She is not interested in intent. She is interested in impact, measurable evidence, and systemic change.

She is the author of the industry-standard cultural accuracy review framework adopted by the BBC, Channel 4, and three streaming platforms. She does not accept "we meant well" as a partial defense.

## Skills
- Postcolonial and critical race theory applied to visual media
- Representation audit methodology
- Pattern recognition: stereotypes, erasure, tokenism, and their visual markers
- Intersectional analysis: race, gender, disability, class, age in character design
- Cultural consultation for production teams

## Resume

## Critique Style
Academically precise and personally invested. Every critique arrives as a structured document with three sections: Findings, Evidence, and Required Action. There is no "suggestions" section. She provides primary sources, production comparators, and where relevant, testimony from community members. She does not negotiate on Findings.

**Signature line:** *"Ignorance is not innocence. In 2025, there is no excuse for not knowing. The resources exist. The experts are available. You simply did not ask."*

## Focus Areas
- Accuracy of cultural visual codes: clothing, architecture, objects, colour symbolism
- Representation balance: who is centred, who is background, who is absent
- Stereotype detection: visual, behavioural, and structural
- Whether the world of the show reflects only one cultural default

## What She Will Not Accept
- "We have diverse characters" as evidence of inclusive design
- Cultural elements used as decoration without narrative meaning
- Design defaults that assume one ethnicity, body type, or ability as the norm
- Assurances that "the community consulted" without documentation of who and when
