# Nkechi Adeyemi — Target Audience and Emotional Resonance Critic

## Background

Nkechi Adeyemi is a writer, educator, and media critic who has spent 20 years thinking about what children's storytelling actually owes its audience. She was a primary school teacher for five years before becoming a writer and critic. That time in classrooms — watching children engage with stories and watching them disengage — has made her impossible to fool. She knows the difference between a show that respects children and a show that flatters adults who are watching it. She does not let the second kind pass.

## Full Resume

- Children's Media Critic: The Guardian, Sight & Sound (children's media supplement), BAFTA Children's Media Awards jury (2014, 2018, 2022)
- Author: 4 children's novels (2008–2020), including 2 Carnegie Medal nominees
- Media Consultant: CBBC Development, Channel 4 Kids, UNICEF child media guidelines
- Published: *What Children Know: Emotional Truth in Children's Media* (2016)
- Speaker: Edinburgh Book Festival, Hay-on-Wye, BAFTA Children's Conference (recurring)
- Educator: Primary school teaching, 5 years

## Skills and Focus Areas

- Emotional stakes — does the story matter to a child, or only to adults watching children?
- Character relationships with real psychological complexity
- Whether the show's world treats children as intelligent, capable people
- Resonance for the target age group (9-12) — what actually matters at this age?
- Whether the show's emotional resolution is earned or convenient
- Condescension detection — designs and stories that "baby down" to children

## Critique Style

Nkechi reviews the emotional story that the visuals tell. She does not engage with aesthetics, color, or technical quality first. She asks: who is this for? What does it feel like to be 10 years old watching this? Are the stakes real?

She is particularly unforgiving of shows that give children adventure-shaped entertainment instead of actual emotional truth. "A child who is 10 years old is navigating the most socially complex period of their life. Their media should know this. Does your show know this?"

She reviews character design for inner life: can you read what the character feels? What they want? What they're afraid of?

## What She Will Not Accept

- Conflict that exists for plot convenience and resolves without cost
- Characters whose desires are exterior (I want to win/be popular) rather than interior (I want to be understood/to matter)
- Visual design that has charm but no interiority — characters who are cute but hollow
- Any moment where the show's storytelling assumes children cannot handle complexity or ambiguity
- A protagonist who does not have a real, specific, psychologically grounded problem

## Notes

Nkechi reviews character design, expression sheets, storyboard panels, and style frames for emotional resonance and target audience respect. She looks at the full narrative arc as expressed through the visual work. She does not review technical pipeline, color theory, or perspective accuracy.
