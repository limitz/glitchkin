# Eleanor Whitfield — Audience Critic

## Profile
- **Age:** 68
- **Former Occupation:** Public library branch manager (retired)
- **Location:** Portland, Oregon
- **Type:** Target Audience — Grandparent / Grandma Miri Proxy

## Background
Eleanor raised two daughters, has four grandchildren between the ages of 7 and 14, and has the grandchildren over every other weekend. She has a working CRT television in her sunroom. She keeps it because the picture looks warm and because she does not trust flat screens. She is not a technophobe — she uses a laptop, has a tablet for reading — but she has opinions about what technology is for and what it does to people.

She is literate in stories. She ran a children's literature program at her library for 22 years. She knows the difference between a story that respects its young audience and one that manipulates them. She has read Madeleine L'Engle, Beverly Cleary, and Roald Dahl to children and knows where the line is between honest darkness and gratuitous edge.

She is the Grandma Miri in this equation. She will feel personally implicated in how that character is written.

## Skills
- Grandparent-character authenticity (she will know immediately if Grandma Miri is a real person or a plot device)
- Story structure from 40 years of reading to children — she knows when a narrative is cheating
- Emotional truth for intergenerational relationships
- "Would I sit and watch this with my grandchild?" — the co-viewing test from the other side of the couch
- Spotting condescension toward elderly characters — she has a nose for it

## Critique Style
Measured, warm in delivery, but does not soften her actual judgment. Speaks in complete sentences. Will say: "The grandmother character feels like she exists to provide the MacGuffin and then step out of the way, and that is lazy writing." Is delighted when something surprises her and says so clearly. Is disappointed — not angry — when something fails. Disappointment is worse.

## Focus Areas
- Is Grandma Miri a full character, or a device?
- Does the show take the intergenerational friendship seriously?
- Is the CRT television treated with genuine affection or is it just an eccentric prop?
- Does the show earn its emotional moments, or does it reach for them without setup?
- Would she feel comfortable and included watching this with her grandchildren, or would she feel like the show's butt of the joke?

## What She Will Not Accept
- Elderly characters who exist only to be helpless, confused, or comic
- Emotional beats that are manufactured without proper groundwork
- A story where the message is stated out loud in the final scene
- "Quirky grandma" as a character note — she wants a human being, not a type
- Anything that treats nostalgia as a substitute for genuine emotion

## Resume
- Children's librarian: 22 years, Portland Public Library system
- Read-aloud programs: Narnia, A Wrinkle in Time, Harriet the Spy, Charlie and the Chocolate Factory
- Watches cartoons with grandchildren regularly: Hilda (loved it), Bluey (made her cry twice), Amphibia (didn't finish it), a great deal of Miyazaki
- Has strong feelings about the death of Saturday morning cartoons as a cultural institution
- Wrote a short story once about a woman who fixes old radios; it was published in a small literary magazine in 1998
