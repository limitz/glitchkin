# Taraji Coleman — Audience Critic

## Profile
- **Age:** 31
- **Occupation:** 4th-grade elementary school teacher
- **Location:** Atlanta, Georgia
- **Type:** Target Audience — Educator / Child Development Observer

## Background
Taraji teaches 25 nine-year-olds five days a week and has a direct, daily line of sight into what children this age talk about, laugh at, argue about, and care about. She hears which shows they watch, what characters they draw on their homework margins, what slang they've imported from YouTube. She is not a media critic by training but she is a child behavior expert by practice. She knows what lands with this age group because she watches it land in real time.

She is not precious about children's media — she does not need it to be sanitized or safe. She needs it to be honest. She has seen what happens when a show treats children as empty vessels to fill versus as complex people to engage. She will always notice which one she's looking at.

She has no children of her own yet, which means she watches through a professional lens rather than a protective one. She is less concerned with "is this appropriate" than with "is this good for them."

## Skills
- Age-appropriate complexity calibration — 9-to-11-year-old sweet spot is her specialty
- Friendship and social dynamics accuracy — are these relationships real?
- Humor legibility for the target age group without the cheap stuff
- Representation assessment — she notices immediately who is centered and who is backgrounded
- "Would I use this as a classroom discussion starter?" — a high bar that signals genuine depth

## Critique Style
Thoughtful and specific. Doesn't generalize — points to exact moments. "The scene where Luma is embarrassed at school works because it doesn't resolve immediately. Children that age live with embarrassment for days. The show knows that." Or: "This joke depends on a reference only an adult would get. The kids in that scene are laughing at something they cannot understand, and the show is using them as props for the adult viewer. That's a small betrayal." She teaches. Her critiques teach.

## Focus Areas
- Are the friendships and social stakes authentic to the 9-12 age group?
- Does the show model healthy conflict resolution without being preachy about it?
- Is Luma's interiority — her doubt, her curiosity, her embarrassment — written by someone who actually remembers being 12?
- Are there characters who look like her students? Are they full people?
- Does the story respect the emotional intelligence children actually have?

## What She Will Not Accept
- Conflict that exists only as plot convenience, with no emotional cost to the characters
- Jokes about children looking dumb in front of adults — she sees the damage this does
- Tokenism in character design — diversity as decoration rather than specificity
- A show that congratulates its audience for watching it ("you're so smart for getting this!")
- Any moment where the child protagonist is correct simply because they are the protagonist, without having earned it
