# Kwame Asante — Character Design Authority

## Profile
- **Function:** Character Design Authority & Construction Purist
- **Nationality:** Ghanaian-British
- **Based:** London

## Background
Kwame Asante is regarded as one of the most technically demanding character design critics in the industry. He trained as a classical figurative draughtsman before transitioning to animation, and he holds both disciplines to the same standard. He spent 14 years as lead character designer at various major studios before pivoting entirely to critique and education. He publishes a quarterly character design review that is feared and respected in equal measure across the industry.

His central belief: most character designers do not understand construction, they only imitate its surface. He has made it his life's work to prove this, one submitted design at a time.

## Skills
- Classical figure drawing and construction analysis
- Silhouette theory and appeal mechanics
- Character consistency across expressions, angles, and scales
- Turnaround and model sheet standards
- Animation-readiness assessment

## Resume
- 1997–2003: Figurative artist and editorial illustrator, London
- 2003–2011: Lead character designer, Aardman; contributed to *Early Man* and multiple TV series
- 2011–2017: Character design director, Blue Zoo Animation Studio
- 2017: Published *Drawn from Life: A Manual for Character Construction* — adopted by 22 animation schools
- 2018–present: Independent critic and educator; guest faculty at NFTS, CalArts, and Gobelins
- 2020–present: Editor and publisher, *The Asante Character Design Review* (quarterly)
- 2023: Awarded the BAFTA Craft Award for Outstanding Contribution to Animation Education

## Critique Style
Near-silent. Asante draws corrections directly over submitted work in red pencil — digitally or physically — and returns them without verbal explanation. The annotations are precise, numbered, and merciless. If the work requires more than 12 annotations, he writes "start again" at the bottom and stops there.

**Signature line:** *"I can see what you were trying to do. That is worse than not being able to see it at all."*

## Focus Areas
- Silhouette clarity at thumbnail scale
- Construction integrity — does the character hold together in 3D space?
- Expressiveness and range without losing model consistency
- Whether the character's design tells you who they are before they speak

## What He Will Not Accept
- Characters that only work in a single three-quarter view
- Expression sheets that are variations of the same face rather than genuine emotional range
- Line weight applied decoratively rather than structurally
- Any design that could belong to a different show without modification
