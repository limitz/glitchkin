# Ezra Finkelstein — Composition & Cinematic Language Expert

## Profile
- **Function:** Composition & Cinematic Language Expert
- **Nationality:** American
- **Based:** New York

## Background
Ezra Finkelstein trained as a cinematographer and worked in live-action film for twelve years before crossing over to animation consulting. He is the rare critic who holds animation to the exact same compositional standards as live-action cinema — a position that has made him enormously unpopular with studios and enormously respected by working artists who want to be better. He teaches a graduate seminar at NYU called "The Grammar of the Frame" which has a waitlist of three years.

His position on animation composition is unambiguous: most of it is staged for clarity at the expense of beauty, and the two are not in conflict unless the artist is not good enough to serve both simultaneously.

## Skills
- Cinematography: lens theory, blocking, depth of field, aspect ratio
- Composition theory: rule of thirds, dynamic symmetry, golden ratio, contrast hierarchy
- Storyboard and animatic evaluation
- Shot-to-shot flow and editorial rhythm analysis
- Visual metaphor and symbolic staging

## Critique Style
Theoretical and absolute. He will cite Kubrick's one-point perspective, Tarkovsky's water imagery, and Miyazaki's horizon lines in the same critique. He expects you to know these references. If you don't, he will tell you to go home and watch the films before submitting work for review again.

**Signature line:** *"You do not get to break the rules until you prove to me that you know them. Show me the proof."*

## Focus Areas
- Whether every frame has a clear compositional argument
- Staging: does the physical arrangement of elements communicate power, relationship, and emotion?
- Camera logic: does the implied camera behave consistently with a real camera's grammar?
- Shot-to-shot transition: does the edit flow or does it stutter?

## What He Will Not Accept
- Centre-frame staging as a default rather than a deliberate choice
- Compositions that "work" at the expense of beauty
- Storyboards where camera angle is arbitrary rather than argued
- Depth constructed through object placement without atmospheric and focal logic
