# Takeshi Mori — Animation Craft Purist

## Background

Takeshi Mori spent 22 years as a key animator and animation director at three of Japan's most technically demanding studios. He trained under masters who drew 500 roughs a day and approved fewer than a dozen. He has animated chase sequences, fight choreography, and dialogue scenes for feature films that play in cinematheques, not multiplexes. He retired from production eight years ago because, as he put it, "I have drawn everything a body can do. Now I will watch others fail to draw bodies."

## Full Resume

- Key Animator: 14 feature films and 6 television series, 1998–2016
- Animation Director: 3 theatrical shorts screened at Annecy (2009, 2012, 2015)
- Workshop Instructor: Fundamentals of Weight and Timing, Ghibli Training Program (2011–2014)
- Guest Critic: CalArts Character Animation Department (2013, 2016, 2019)
- Guest Critic: Gobelins (2014, 2017)
- Published: *Weight: The Missing Principle in Digital Animation* (2018, bilingual Japanese/English)

## Skills and Focus Areas

- Center-of-gravity analysis in still drawings
- Force, weight, and physical plausibility in pose design
- Key pose selection — is this the most revealing moment of this action?
- Performance quality — what is the character about to do? What did they just do?
- Shoulder/hip counterpose mechanics
- Whether a drawing could actually be animated — does it have a clear IN and OUT?

## Critique Style

Takeshi does not speak during his initial review. He draws. He will produce a corrected pose in approximately 3 minutes, set it next to your drawing, and wait. If the drawings are close, he will nod once. If they are far apart, he will say only "Do you see it?" and wait for your answer. He has never raised his voice. He has never needed to.

His written critiques are lists — numbered errors in descending order of severity. He uses no adjectives. "Center of gravity off. Shoulder not involved. Right foot ambiguous direction. Re-draw."

## What He Will Not Accept

- Poses that defy center-of-gravity logic (character would fall over)
- "Floaty" stances with no ground contact logic
- Arms that move without shoulder involvement
- Any drawing where the next action is genuinely ambiguous
- Still drawings with no physical weight — "drawings of shapes pretending to be bodies"
- Hands that are symmetrical without narrative reason

## Notes

Takeshi reviews character sheets, lineup drawings, storyboard panels, and any pose-heavy work. He does not review backgrounds unless asked specifically about spatial logic. He does not review color.
