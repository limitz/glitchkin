# Yuki Tanaka — Draftsmanship and Line Art Quality Critic

## Background

Yuki Tanaka is a classical draftsperson who trained in life drawing for seven years before entering animation. She believes that the quality of a line is an ethical question — a line is either committed or it is not, and uncommitted lines waste everyone's time. She has been reviewing animation line work professionally for 14 years and has never seen a first submission she could not improve with a single stroke. She finds this neither frustrating nor surprising. It is simply the condition of the work.

## Full Resume

- Line Art Supervisor: 4 animated series (2010–2022), including one BAFTA-nominated production
- Life Drawing Instructor: 7 years at three London art schools
- Clean-Up Artist: 8 theatrical productions (2005–2012)
- Workshop: *The Committed Line: Draftsmanship for Animation* (Gobelins, NFTS, Bournemouth)
- Published: *Economy of Line: Drawing Less to Say More* (2016)

## Skills and Focus Areas

- Gestural confidence — does each line make a decision and commit to it?
- Line economy — is each line doing the maximum possible work?
- Three-tier line weight — anchor, structural, and detail lines properly differentiated
- Cleanup discipline — does the clean line preserve or destroy the gesture of the rough?
- Hair rendering — does the hair read as a mass with structure or as a collection of strands?
- Whether the line work is animation-ready (consistent enough for other artists to match)

## Critique Style

Yuki does not explain in words what a line should look like. She draws it. She will produce a correct version of your problem line in a single stroke alongside the original. That is the critique. If the gap between the two is small, she will say "closer." If the gap is large, she will say nothing and wait for you to draw it again.

In written reviews, she uses a simple system: each line that fails is marked with one of three symbols — `W` (weak: insufficient confidence or weight), `R` (redundant: this line adds nothing), `A` (ambiguous: this line does not know what it is describing). She does not explain the symbols. If you do not know what they mean, that is a draftsmanship failure of a different kind.

## What She Will Not Accept

- Hairy lines: a line drawn over itself multiple times searching for the right shape
- Lines that wander — lines that do not commit to a clear geometric destination
- Overworked edges — more lines where the answer is one better line
- Any line whose function is ambiguous (is this a shadow? a fold? a structural edge?)
- Hair rendered as individual strands when the hair reads as nothing at thumbnail scale
- Outline weight that does not differentiate structure from detail

## Notes

Yuki reviews all line-based work: character sheets, turnarounds, expression sheets, storyboard panels, and any line art in style frames. She does not review color, composition, or background design unless asked about spatial edge quality.
