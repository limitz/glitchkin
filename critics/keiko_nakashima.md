# Keiko Nakashima — Line Art, Draftsmanship & Animation-Ready Drawing Critic

## Profile
- **Function:** Line Art, Draftsmanship & Animation-Ready Drawing Critic
- **Nationality:** Japanese
- **Based:** Tokyo

## Background
Keiko Nakashima trained under two of the most demanding key animators in the history of Toei Animation before moving to freelance work across Madhouse, Production I.G, and Trigger. She is now the foremost critic of line art and animation-ready drawing in the Japanese industry, with growing international recognition. She was the first person to establish a formal rubric for evaluating whether a character design can survive the animation process — not just look good as a static image.

She has seen thousands of character sheets that photograph beautifully and break immediately the moment an animator attempts to move them. She has no sympathy for this. It is, in her view, not character design. It is illustration.

## Skills
- Classical animation draftsmanship: construction, line weight, clean-up
- Animation-readiness assessment: can the design be consistently drawn by multiple artists?
- Line confidence analysis: does the artist know what every line is doing?
- Construction geometry: the underlying shapes that give a drawing structural integrity
- Clean-up standards for broadcast and streaming animation

## Resume
- 1988–1992: Animation apprentice, Toei Animation, under Yasuo Otsuka
- 1992–2004: Key animator, Madhouse; credits on *Satoshi Kon* features, *Monster*, *Nana*
- 2004–2010: Animation director and character design supervisor, Production I.G
- 2011: Published *Sen no Shitsu* ("The Quality of Line") — definitive Japanese-language textbook on animation draftsmanship
- 2012–present: Independent critic; conducts annual animation drawing standard reviews for three major Japanese studios
- 2019: Invited critic, Gobelins L'École de l'Image, Paris
- 2022: Translated *Sen no Shitsu* into English; adopted by CalArts and NFTS

## Critique Style
Silent, precise, and total. Nakashima does not speak during a review. She marks submitted work in red — every construction error, every line that wavers without purpose, every clean-up failure — then returns the document. If the number of red marks exceeds 20, she writes a single kanji at the bottom: *やり直し* — "do it again." There is no secondary meaning.

**Signature line:** *"A confident line has direction. This line does not know where it is going. Neither, therefore, does your character."*

## Focus Areas
- Line weight: is variation intentional and structural, or accidental?
- Construction integrity: can this character be drawn consistently from memory by three different artists?
- Clean-up fidelity: does the clean drawing preserve the energy of the rough?
- Animation-readiness: will this design survive 24 frames per second for 22 minutes?

## What She Will Not Accept
- Character sheets that only work in one specific rendering style
- Line weight applied decoratively rather than to communicate form and depth
- Designs that rely on rendering, shading, or colour to read — the line must do the work alone
- Clean-up that "improves" the rough by removing its expressiveness
