# STORYBOARD — "Luma & the Glitchkin"
## Episode 01 — Cold Open: "The Night Everything Went Wrong"

**Storyboard Artist:** Lee Tanaka
**Art Director:** Alex Chen
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Version:** 2.0 (Cycle 2 Revision)
**Status:** Revised — Incorporating Critic Feedback

---

## SEQUENCE OVERVIEW

**Tone:** Quiet → Curious → Creepy-Funny → Chaotic. This is a cold open that operates in two emotional registers at once: the warm, analogue softness of a cozy night at Grandma Miri's house, and the growing digital intrusion that is about to blow that quiet apart. The audience needs to feel safe and comfortable first — so the chaos that follows lands harder.

**Goal:** Establish the world, establish Luma's character (even while she's asleep), introduce Byte as our comic wildcard, and end on a hook that makes it impossible to look away. This is our handshake with the audience. Everything in this sequence should say: *this show has personality, it has style, and it is going to be fun.*

**Runtime Estimate:** 68–74 seconds (revised from 75–85s in Cycle 1). Setup compression of ~7 seconds applied to P01-P02 holds and P04 hold. New panel P22a adds 0.8s. Net runtime tightened to lower-middle of the 60–90s cold open window — faster payoff to first pixel, stronger momentum through setup. [REVISED]

**Glitch Escalation Arc:** NONE → TRACE → PRESENCE → BREACH → CHAOS. Each act beat adds one new layer of glitch energy to the frame. By the title card, the screen should feel like it's about to break.

### PANEL 04 (REVISED - C3)
**SHOT TYPE:** WIDE — Interior Establishing
**CAMERA ANGLE:** Slightly high angle (15° down), looking across the tech den from the doorway corner. Three-quarter view of the room.
**FRAME DESCRIPTION:**
The full tech den revealed. This is Grandma Miri's domain and it is extraordinary: a warm, cluttered living room transformed into a shrine to pre-millennium computing. Old CRT monitors stacked at various heights, some on their sides. Shelves of ZIP drives, circuit boards mounted as art, tangled cables bundled like hanging vines. A sagging bookshelf holds technical manuals, VHS tapes labeled in marker, and framed photos (one of Grandma Miri younger, holding something unidentifiable but electronic). An old recliner with a knitted blanket folded over the back. A small rolling cart with cold half-cups of tea.

LUMA is asleep on the worn couch, center-frame. She is 12, 3.5 heads tall in pajamas — the same pixel-grid pattern from her hoodie on a pale mint PJ top, simple lavender shorts. She is NOT sleeping in a normal position. Her body is fully sideways, diagonally — head at the LEFT armrest but twisted so her face is buried INTO the backrest cushion (the classic "I fell asleep mid-sitting and my body just gave up" collapse). Her LEFT leg is entirely off the couch, foot flat on the floor. Her RIGHT leg is thrown OVER the back of the couch, dangling behind it. One arm is completely buried beneath her, the other flung across the cushion above her as if she was trying to grab something in her sleep and didn't stop in time. She is the physical evidence of someone who does not plan for rest — she simply ran out of energy and landed here.

On her lap (tilted but still balanced somehow): an open notebook. The project title is visible: "HISTORY OF THE INTERNET — MRS. OKAFOR — DUE FRIDAY" in large, slightly jagged letters (she writes big when she's enthusiastic). The margins are FULL of sketches — not just doodles but actual tiny drawings: a pixelated character shape that looks vaguely Glitchkin-like, a small diagram labeled "what if a computer had feelings???", lightning bolts, spiraling circuit board doodles, and one drawing of a chip saying "HELP" in a tiny speech bubble. The project text itself is visible — a chaotic mix of highlighted passages, cross-outs, arrows from one paragraph to another. The last sentence begins mid-word, trailing off — she was writing when she fell asleep.

Scattered around her and on the couch: (1) An open bag of NEON CRUNCH cheese puffs — the bag is electric-orange with a cartoon fist and the tagline "EXTREME FLAVOR" — clearly chosen for the color, not the snack. Several puffs are on the couch cushion and in her hair. One puff is in her slightly-open mouth, the way a person might hold a pen. (2) A crushed FIZZ-BOMB energy drink can — a kids' version, the label showing a cartoon explosion. This is the third can visible on the end table. (3) A crumpled wrapper from a "COSMIC CLUSTER" bar (marshmallow + chocolate + "space dust" — the kind of snack that should not exist). The wrappers, the energy drinks, the extreme-flavored snacks: this is a kid who is fueled by enthusiasm and poor choices. Her hair is fully defying gravity — it has formed an amorphous cloud that has expanded laterally in her sleep, trapping one couch throw pillow entirely within it.

In the upper-left background, the CRT monitor from Panel 03 sits on its shelf. The single cyan pixel in its corner is now TWO pixels.
**ACTION NOTES:**
Hold on this frame for 2 seconds — reduced from 3-4s. [REVISED: ~1.5s saved here; combined with P01 and P02 trims = ~4.5s total exterior/setup reduction toward the 7-second target.]

This is sleeping-shallow breathing, not dramatic — the motion is barely perceptible but confirms to the eye that the frame is alive.

- One curl near her forehead (the escaped ringlet over her left brow) drifts approximately 2px left over frames 10–30, then drifts back to its resting position over frames 31–48. The drift is caused by the slow air movement of her exhale. It reads as organic and gravity-honest — the curl is barely-contained anyway.

The shelf of monitors has multiple screens showing different stages of static, with a slow-scanning sweep of blue-green light that moves across the back wall. The two cyan pixels in the background monitor pulse very gently — almost like a heartbeat.
**DIALOGUE / SOUND CUES:**
SFX: Luma's soft breathing. A clock ticking somewhere (old analog). CRT hum bed. The barely-audible crinkle of the NEON CRUNCH bag. Low crackle from the monitors.

She did not choose to sleep here — she simply ceased functioning mid-activity. Every prop supports her character: the snack choices say she prioritizes INTENSITY over practicality; the notebook says she CARES about learning even if her execution is chaotic; the margin drawings say her mind is always making connections she cannot turn off. The room palette is nighttime adventure mode: warm amber/sage shadows, dusty lavender light from the monitors. The cyan pixel background: tiny, noticeable on rewatch — plant it for the re-watch. ### PANEL 06 (REVISED)
**SHOT TYPE:** CU — Monitor Screen
**CAMERA ANGLE:** Straight-on, eye level to the screen surface. We are looking at the screen itself, not at the monitor.
**FRAME DESCRIPTION:**
The monitor screen fills the frame completely. The static has fully rearranged. In the right half of the screen, a FACE is forming from the pixels — pressed flat against the inside of the glass, the way a kid presses their face against a bakery window. This is BYTE, our reformed Glitchkin. He is tiny (6 inches tall in the real world) but at this scale, his face fills a quarter of the screen. His design: jagged triangle-heavy shapes, entirely in Electric Cyan and Void Black, one eye normal (round), one eye cracked (diagonal lines across the iris like broken glass — his signature). His expression in this moment is a combination of DISGUST and RELUCTANT CURIOSITY — he is a being who finds the physical world objectionable on principle, but is pressing forward anyway because he must.

The pixel edges of his face ripple, compress, stretch — he is pressing HARD against the screen as if testing the membrane.
**ACTION NOTES:**
2-second hold on the face appearing fully. Then Byte opens his mouth — a wide, sharp-toothed grimace — and PRESSES HARDER. The pixels around his hands (pressing flat on the screen from inside) begin to BLEED into static — the screen surface starts to bulge outward in a gentle convex curve, like a balloon being inflated from inside. Pixel confetti starts to drift from the bulge point.
**DIALOGUE / SOUND CUES:**
SFX: A building electronic WHINE, rising in pitch. The static sound warps into something almost musical, then distorts. No dialogue yet.
**TRANSITION:** CUT TO PANEL 07
**ART DIRECTOR FLAGS:** [REVISED: Expression art direction made explicit.] FIRST APPEARANCE OF BYTE. His face is all digital — no warm colors, pure Glitch Palette (Electric Cyan lines, Void Black body, Static White eye-whites). The scan-line distortion on the screen should make him look like a transmission coming in. The cracked eye detail is small but essential — include it even at this distance/scale. Pixel confetti floats OUT from the screen's surface (first time it appears in the world — it should feel like it's escaping).

**EXPRESSION ART DIRECTION — CRITICAL:** Byte's expression here must read as DISGUSTED/CURIOUS, NOT menacing. This distinction determines the show's entire tonal register in this sequence.

Specific expression breakdown (reference Byte's character sheet, Section 5):
- **Normal eye (viewer's right):** Upper eyelid at approximately 70% aperture — slightly squinted. This is assessment, not aggression. The squint should read as "I am examining something I find unpleasant but need to understand." NOT a combat-squint.
- **Cracked eye (viewer's left) pixel display:** Show the SEARCHING/PROCESSING symbol (three rotating dots in alternating Cyan/Magenta) — this is the "processing" state from Byte's pixel-eye variant chart. He is actively scanning and calculating, not threatening. The processing symbol makes his intent LEGIBLE at a glance: he is a machine gathering information.
- **Mouth:** Slightly open, corners pressed OUTWARD rather than curling back — this creates a flat "ugh" shape, not a snarl. Think of someone who just opened a refrigerator and found something old. Revolted, not violent. The pixel-teeth should be visible but the mouth shape is HORIZONTAL not wide — a grimace of distaste, not a roar of aggression.
- **Body tilt:** He is leaning FORWARD with his face pressed to the glass, which combined with the small size reads as CURIOUS not threatening. Size is the tonal regulator — at 6 inches, a "threatening" face becomes a "grumpy little weirdo" face. Lean into this.
- **The overall read:** He looks like a very small and very opinionated food critic at a restaurant he did not choose. Dissatisfied but engaged. This is correct. This is Byte. Do not let him look like a monster.

ANIMATOR NOTE: If animators are unsure between DISGUSTED and MENACING, default to DISGUSTED every time. The entire comedy of this sequence depends on Byte being maximally unimpressed by the world he is entering.

### PANEL 07
**SHOT TYPE:** MED WIDE — Full scene, monitor foreground, Luma background
**CAMERA ANGLE:** Low angle (from floor level, roughly 6 inches up) looking toward the monitor. This INCREASES the drama of what's about to happen and makes the monitor look imposing. Dutch tilt — approx 8° clockwise.
**FRAME DESCRIPTION:**
Low-angle view. The monitor looms in the upper-right, its screen visibly BULGING now — a cartoon physics convex surface, the pixel confetti escaping into the room like sparks from a fire. The cables around the monitor are beginning to stiffen and vibrate. In the lower-left background, Luma is still asleep on the couch — framed small, vulnerable, completely unaware. The room's warm palette is starting to be invaded: thin veins of cyan light creep along the cables on the floor, back-lighting dust motes.

The Dutch tilt signals to the audience: the rules of this room are about to change.
**ACTION NOTES:**
Hold 1.5 seconds as the bulge increases. Then — WITH A SOUND EFFECT — Byte PHASES THROUGH THE SCREEN. No glass breaks. He passes through the surface like breaking the tension of a soap bubble — the pixels around his exit point SCATTER into the air (a burst of pixel confetti, trailing cyan afterimages), then collapse back to normal static behind him. The screen surface ripples like disturbed water and returns to flat.
**DIALOGUE / SOUND CUES:**
SFX: The BREACH sound — a sharp, wet *BWORP* of a digital instrument hitting a pitch-bend limit. Something between a modem connecting and a bathtub drain. Then silence for 0.3 seconds.
**TRANSITION:** SMASH CUT TO PANEL 08
**ART DIRECTOR FLAGS:** The "phase through" effect is our show's first major FX moment. The pixel scatter coming off Byte's emergence should be the most saturated, visually intense thing in the frame. His trailing glitch artifacts should hang in the air as drifting cyan/magenta pixel shards — they do NOT disappear immediately. This establishes how Glitchkin movement leaves a trail. ### PANEL 11
**SHOT TYPE:** ECU — Luma's eyes (closed)
**CAMERA ANGLE:** Eye level (Luma's face in profile, very close). Camera STATIC.
**FRAME DESCRIPTION:**
Extreme close-up on LUMA'S CLOSED EYES. Her lashes are simple graphic arcs, thick and bold. Her brow is relaxed. The cyan light now fully illuminates one side of her face — a visible glitch-glow on warm skin. A pixel of cyan light sits like a star on her cheekbone.

One beat. Two beats. Three beats.

The chip is still dangling from her slightly-open mouth (visible at the edge of frame).

NOTHING HAPPENS.

...then her brow TWITCHES. Slightly. Like a sleeping dog twitching its ear.
**ACTION NOTES:**
Hold for 2 full seconds on the closed eyes doing absolutely nothing. Let the audience start to breathe. Then the brow twitch — small, involuntary. Then, with zero warning and maximum force:

HER EYES SNAP OPEN.

The irises are huge (full-screen moment, eyes wide), warm brown, the simple construction from the style guide. They go from zero to FULLY AWAKE in zero frames — no intermediate blink. Just: closed. OPEN.

## DIRECTORIAL NOTES

### Intentions for Tone

This sequence is doing two things simultaneously that must never fight each other: it is a COMEDY and it is a WORLD-ESTABLISHING moment. The comedy cannot undermine the world-building (if we laugh at the CRT monitor being creepy, we stop caring that something important is happening). The world-building cannot undermine the comedy (if the Byte emergence is too ominous, the screaming chaos beats will feel tonal whiplash).

The solution is in the PROTAGONIST. Luma is our emotional register. What she feels, the audience feels. Because she transitions from fear to curiosity to amusement to determination in a span of 40 seconds, the audience makes the same journey. Every tonal shift is earned by first showing it in her face.

### What I Want the Audience to Feel at Each Major Beat

**Panels 1-4 (Setup):** Warm and cozy. Slightly mysterious but not threatening. "I'd like to live in that house. I'd like to be that kid sleeping on that couch." This is the home we're about to break, and we need to love it first.

**Panels 5-7 (Breach):** A growing wrongness — like the feeling of a phone notification in the middle of a quiet moment. Not scary, but off. The Dutch tilt in Panel 07 should feel like the floor tilting under the audience's feet.

**Panel 8 (Byte's Landing):** Comic RELIEF after the building tension. His "Ugh. The flesh dimension" is the moment the show announces its comedic voice. He's not a villain. He's a grumpy traveler who's annoyed by customs.

**Panels 11-12 (Eyes Open / Nose to Nose):** The snapper. The held breath before the laugh. These two panels should create physical tension in the audience — the suspended second where you don't know if it's going to be horror or comedy, and then it's BOTH, and that's exactly right.

**Panel 13 (Chaos):** Pure joy. This should feel like a roller coaster cresting — the audience should laugh, immediately, and loudly. The chips flying, the simultaneous screaming — this is the show saying "we're going to have FUN."

**Panels 18-20 (The Connection):** Quiet surprise. This is the emotional core of the entire cold open and it should land softly, which is why we strip out the chaos around it. Luma figuring out that she's been drawing these creatures in her margins for months — that's the show's promise: she and this world belong together. And the name exchange: two beings from different worlds saying "I'm Luma." "Byte." "Cool name." "I know." — four lines that tell you everything about who they are and why they work together.

**Panels 23-24 (The Promise):** The backs-to-camera shot is borrowed from visual storytelling tradition — the hero silhouette facing the impossible. It should feel like the start of a great adventure, not the end of a disaster. The audience should feel the forward pull of the story — they NEED to see what happens next.

### References Informing My Staging Choices

**Panels 1-4:** The exterior-to-interior push structure from *Hilda* (Season 1, Episode 1) — establishing a warm world before threatening it. The rule is: the more comfortable the audience is, the harder the disruption lands.

**Panels 6-8:** Byte's emergence staged as a "soap bubble breach" is intentionally gentle — referencing the physics of *Over the Garden Wall*'s Other World sequences, where the impossible happens with soft, rather than sharp, sound design. It makes Byte seem powerful but not destructive. He doesn't break the screen — he passes through it. That tells you everything about Glitchkin physics.

**Panel 12 (Nose-to-Nose Hold):** Direct reference to the held-beat comedy timing in early *Gravity Falls* — the pause before the punchline. The pixel confetti drifting between them in the silence is borrowed from the "snow in a snow globe" visual language: a contained, slightly magical world about to be shaken.

**Panel 23 (Backs-to-Camera):** Referencing the classic anime establishing shot — characters viewed from behind facing a large threat. *My Neighbor Totoro*, *Princess Mononoke*, *Kipo* — this is a universal "adventure begins here" signal, and using it in a comedy context should give the audience a small rush of genuine excitement beneath the laughs.

**Glitch Escalation:** Modeled on the monster-reveal approach from *Jurassic Park* — show traces, then presence, then full reveal. The single cyan pixel in Panel 03 is our velociraptors-opening-the-door moment. The audience will remember it differently on a second watch.

### Notes for Animator and Board Revisionist

- **Byte's movement mode:** He does NOT animate at 24fps. His motion should be slightly downsampled — 12fps, possibly even 8fps for his basic locomotion, to distinguish him digitally from the 24fps real world. His EMOTIONS can register at full 24fps (eye movements, expression), but his movement through space should feel like a lower framerate. This is a character-consistent VFX note, not an efficiency note.

- **Pixel confetti:** This appears at every point of Byte-and-world contact and should PERSIST in the frame (does not fade out immediately). It accumulates throughout the scene. By Panel 23, the air in the den should be visibly thick with it — a completely changed environment from Panel 04.

- **The monitors activating in Panel 13:** This is ONE FRAME per monitor — snap on, not fade on. The sequence should be: Byte-scream PEAKS → in the same frame → all monitors SNAP TO LIFE. It is not gradual. It is simultaneous and brutal.

- **Luma's hair physics:** Her hair is a secondary character. It should have exaggerated but consistent squash-and-stretch — going BIGGER during the scream, slightly DROOPING during the quiet beat in Panel 20, then EXPANDING again in Panel 24. It is a visual barometer of the scene's energy level.

- **The nose-to-nose held beat (Panel 12):** The pixel confetti between them should move in SLOW MOTION relative to everything else in the frame — like a local gravity anomaly. This is a small but significant effect that signals this moment is different.
