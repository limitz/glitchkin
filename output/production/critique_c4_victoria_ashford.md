# Critique — Cycle 4
**Critic:** Victoria Ashford, Animation Director
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Subject:** First Visual Output Review — *Luma & the Glitchkin*

---

## Overall Assessment

Let me be direct: the team has cleared the lowest bar. Images exist. That is the only unambiguous win here. In Cycle 1 I flagged the absence of any actual visuals as a production failure; the team has now corrected that. But clearing the lowest bar is not the same as producing work that advances this project toward a pitch. The documentation has genuine ambition — the style guide cites *Hilda*, *The Owl House*, and *Kipo* as reference points. These are productions with hundreds of thousands of hours of combined craft behind their visual identity. What has been delivered does not yet inhabit the same zip code as those references, let alone the same neighborhood. That said, the structural bones of a production pipeline are visible, and some of the conceptual thinking embedded in the outputs is sound. I will give credit where it is due, and I will not soften what is not working.

---

## What's Working in the Visuals

**Proportion Diagram — Conceptual Validity.** The head-count proportions encoded in the diagram (Luma 3.5, Cosmo 4.0, Miri 3.2, Byte 0.7) are internally consistent and align correctly with the style guide specifications. The choice to show Byte at roughly 20% of Luma's height communicates scale difference effectively. The use of a baseline and axis marks shows someone is thinking about this as a production reference document rather than casual concept art. That is the right instinct.

**Silhouette Sheet — The Right Test Is Being Run.** The decision to produce a squint-test silhouette sheet demonstrates actual production literacy. Most student teams skip this step entirely. The four silhouettes — Luma, Cosmo, Byte, and Miri — are distinguishable from one another at a glance. Luma's dome-heavy head reads differently from Cosmo's narrow rectangle-body, and Byte's small blocky mass is instantly distinct from the taller characters. This is the single most encouraging piece of visual evidence in the entire output set.

**Storyboard Color Palette — Correct Instinct.** The contact sheet and individual panels demonstrate that someone has internalized the warm-analog-versus-cool-digital palette principle from the style guide. The use of black/dark-warm backgrounds for the Millbrook exterior, contrasted with electric cyan for Byte's glow in P01, and the hot pink/cyan split in P13, shows color is being used structurally rather than decoratively. The title card (P25) correctly deploys cyan/magenta as glitch-signal colors and the chromatic aberration offset on "THE GLITCHKIN" is the most sophisticated visual idea in the entire output set — it directly reflects the show's thematic language.

**Storyboard Panel Labeling.** Shot codes (EWS, ECU, CU, MED WID), panel numbers, and action descriptions are present. This is professional board formatting practice and should be maintained.

---

## Critical Gaps Still Remaining

**The characters are geometric placeholders, not characters.** I need to say this clearly: a circle on a rectangle is not Luma. A taller rectangle with a square head is not Cosmo. What exists in the proportion diagram and silhouette sheet are shape-language studies — useful as a developmental step, entirely insufficient as character design. There are no facial features, no costume details, no hands, no expressive line quality, no personality. The style guide specifies Luma's pixel-patterned hoodie, Cosmo's too-big glasses, and Byte's cracked eye as essential visual hooks. None of these appear anywhere in the visual output. A network executive or potential crew member cannot evaluate character appeal from a colored rectangle.

**Shape language is being ignored in the storyboard panels.** The style guide explicitly assigns shape families to characters: circles and rounded rectangles for Luma, triangles and jagged polygons for Byte and the Glitchkin. In panel P13, Luma is rendered as a stick figure — a literal stick figure with no shape language whatsoever. This contradicts the show's core design principle. Cosmo in the same panel is a near-identical stick figure. They are visually indistinguishable as characters. This is a significant regression from what the proportion diagram is trying to establish.

**Byte is a square.** In both the proportion diagram and the storyboard panels, Byte is rendered as a cyan square or cube. The style guide specifies Glitchkin use triangles and jagged polygons — unpredictable, sharp energy. A perfect cube communicates the opposite: order, rigidity, machine precision. If Byte is a "grumpy reformed Glitchkin," his design should carry residual chaos — asymmetry, a fractured edge, something that reads as corrupted data made physical. A clean square does not do that.

**Miri's shape language is incorrect.** The style guide assigns "large rounded squares" to Authority/Mentor archetypes, with Grandma Miri as the explicit example. In both the proportion diagram and silhouette sheet, Miri is rendered with a perfect circle head — identical in shape language to Luma. This erases a fundamental visual distinction between the 12-year-old protagonist and her grandmother. Miri should read bigger, softer, more grounded. She currently reads as Luma's shorter twin.

**No faces exist anywhere.** Not in any asset. The single most important appeal element in cartoon character design — the face — is entirely absent. Eyes are described in the style guide as the "primary expression vehicle," large and rounded, with simple iris construction. We have zero evidence that any team member has attempted to draw these characters' faces. This is the most urgent gap in the entire output.

**Storyboard staging is elementary.** P11 (the ECU of Byte's face) is the strongest individual panel composition — the concentric circles work as a tension/focus device and the shot framing is defensible. P01 (the EWS of Millbrook) has reasonable atmospheric staging. But P13 collapses into confusion: three parallel colored rectangles with stick figures in front of them is not storyboard staging — it is a diagram of a storyboard. The action description says "Simultaneous scream. Symmetrical. Monitors activate at the sound." There is no sense of motion, reaction, or comedic timing in the image. The energy spike lines radiating from the figures are a reasonable impulse, but they are rendered as scattered straight strokes with no directional logic.

**No environment/background design exists.** The show is set in Millbrook, with Grandma's house as a primary location and the CRT monitor as the portal to the digital world. There is no background design, no interior layout, no environmental style guide. P01 hints at an exterior but it is a dark silhouette — no architectural character, no warmth that would define the "analog world" half of the show's visual identity.

---

## Image Quality Assessment

I will not soften this: these images are not at a standard that could be shown to a network. They are not at a standard that could be shown to potential crew to recruit talent. What they represent is closer to a proof-of-concept for the production pipeline — evidence that the team can generate labeled, organized visual documents using available tools.

The silhouette sheet could, with significant caveats, be included in an internal development document to show early shape-language thinking. That is its ceiling.

The proportion diagram reads as a technical reference chart, which is appropriate for internal use only.

The storyboard panels, as rendered, would not be accepted in a pre-production package at any studio I have worked at. The P25 title card is the strongest single image — it has graphic confidence and thematic coherence — but a title card without any character design to support it is a logo treatment, not a production asset.

To be fair to the team: these tools (Python/Pillow) are generative coding tools, not illustration software. The team is working within a real technical constraint. The question is whether the team is using these tools as a bridge toward getting actual character design on paper, or whether they are treating programmatically generated geometry as a substitute for illustration. Right now it reads like the latter, and that is a problem.

These images are at **pre-development stage**. The industry equivalent would be very rough internal sketches shared over Slack during a development meeting — not assets, not pitch materials.

---

## What Must Be Produced Next

The following is the critical path to a real pitch package, prioritized in order of blocking dependencies:

1. **Character face studies — Luma first.** A single correctly designed face for the lead character unlocks every downstream asset. Eyes, mouth construction, expression range (minimum: neutral, happy, surprised, scared, determined). This is job one. Nothing else in this list matters as much.

2. **Full character turnaround — Luma.** Front, 3/4, side, back. Costume detail (the pixel-patterned hoodie must appear). Hands. Feet. This is the document that tells potential crew what they're animating.

3. **Character design — Byte.** Redesign from the current square using correct shape language (triangular, jagged, asymmetric). The cracked eye must appear. This character is the most visually distinctive element of the show's premise and currently has no design.

4. **Expression sheets — Luma and Byte.** A minimum of 6–8 expressions each. This is what sells character appeal to a network.

5. **Key background — Grandma's living room interior.** This is the primary recurring location. It must establish the warm/analog visual language and show where the CRT portal lives in the space. One fully realized background will do more for the pitch than ten more proportion diagrams.

6. **Storyboard revision — P13 and surrounding panels.** Re-board the scream sequence with actual character forms (even loose sketch quality) and proper comedic staging. This is the cold open's emotional peak — it must be legible and funny on the page.

7. **Color script — 3–4 panels.** A color script showing the emotional arc of the cold open (dark/mysterious → warm/domestic → digital chaos → smash cut) demonstrates visual storytelling literacy to a network audience.

8. **One fully painted key frame.** A single finished image — not a storyboard panel, not a reference chart — showing a moment from the show with characters, environment, and color at a level of finish that says "this is what the show looks like." This image is what goes on the pitch deck cover page.

---

## Verdict

The team has moved from zero to something. That matters, and I will not pretend otherwise. The silhouette work shows production thinking. The color palette instincts in the storyboards are directionally correct. The title card shows graphic confidence.

But the show's entire emotional and commercial argument rests on character appeal, and there are currently no characters — only shapes standing in their places. You cannot pitch a show about a girl and her chaotic digital companion when neither of them has a face. That is the singular, overriding problem with this output set.

The team needs an illustrator actively drawing, not a script generating geometry. If the available tools can produce character illustration rather than placeholder shapes — even at rough quality — that must happen in the next cycle. If they cannot, the production pipeline needs to be reconsidered.

The documentation surrounding these images remains strong. The style guide is coherent and genuinely thoughtful. The storyboard action lines have voice and comedic instinct. The concept has legs. But concept and documentation are not a pitch. Images are a pitch, and right now the images are not carrying their weight.

One more cycle of placeholder shapes would be a serious problem for this project's credibility.

**Rating: Conditional Progress. Cycle 5 must deliver faces.**
