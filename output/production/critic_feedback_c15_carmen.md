NEAR-MISS. Okay — that is a classroom panel I am not seeing in this contact sheet, but the label tells me it should read as "almost connects, doesn't." Fine. FAILURE — the glitch static screendeath. BLOCKED — the hazard placeholder.

Here is my problem: reading left to right, top to bottom, the arc does not BUILD. It meanders. The jump from SKEPTICAL (A2-03, static medium shot) to INVESTIGATING (A2-04, busy 2x2 montage grid) to DETERMINED (A2-05b, calm MCU) feels like three different directors made three different decisions about what this show IS. I should be able to hold this sheet at arm's length and feel escalation. What I feel is: incident, pause, burst, pause, BANG. That is not escalation. That is a hiccup.

The good news: the bookend logic works. VULNERABLE (Byte MCU with cracked eye) at the emotional start, and FAILURE (the screendeath panel) as the emotional collapse — that is a real throughline. Someone understood the shape of Act Two. But the middle panels between those poles are not doing enough to carry that shape.

The two-row layout is the right choice. Seven panels is the right count for contact sheet legibility. The label typography (arc emotion label above each thumbnail) is an excellent production discipline — it forces the artist to name the beat, not just draw a scene. Keep that system. Make the panels earn the labels.

**What needs to improve:** The contact sheet must read as a rising emotional spine, not a series of isolated moments. At thumbnail scale — I mean REALLY thumbnail, hold the phone at arm's length — you should see brightness/chaos increasing left to right. You do not see that yet.

## Individual Panel Reviews

### A2-02 [B+]

Okay. This is better than the OTS version I killed in Cycle 12, so I will acknowledge the improvement before I bury it.

Byte is centered. Cracked eye is prominent. The dead-pixel glyph is rendered — complex, deliberate, not random noise. Luma is pushed to background-right at approximately 20% frame weight, out of focus. The warm amber tone on the desk surface grounds the scene in the tech den interior. The composition follows the brief.

Here is what is still not working: that light. The cyan spotlight pool beneath Byte is doing compositional work — it isolates him, it says "this creature is the subject" — but it is also flattening him. Byte is floating in a glowing disc. Where is the desk surface? Where are his feet relative to the table? The ground relationship is ambiguous, and that matters because THIS MOMENT — Byte revealing vulnerability for the first time — is the moment we need to feel his physical smallness and his emotional weight simultaneously. If he floats, he reads as abstract. He needs to sit.

The "BYTE MCU / expression: resigned / vulnerable" annotation is a staging note IN the frame, which is production sketch practice. Fine for boards. But the annotation box is competing visually with the cracked eye for center-frame real estate. An animator looking at this needs the cracked eye to be the undisputed focal point. Right now their eye flicks between the annotation and the glyph. That is a composition failure even if it is just a board.

Luma at 20% background weight is correct. Do not touch her. She is doing her job.

**What needs to improve:** Ground Byte physically on the surface. The floating issue makes him read as illustration, not staging. Remove or relocate the annotation box — it should not compete with the emotional center. Add one shadow below Byte's body to anchor him.

### A2-03 [C]

I am going to be blunt. This panel is not staging. This is a diagram of staging.

I have Cosmo. He is a rectangle with a face. Arms are crossed — I can read that. The label "SKEPTICAL" is floating over his torso. There is a whiteboard with a plan on it, partially obscured by the right edge, with Luma's arm visible in background-right. There is something on the left that reads as a glitch artifact or a dark rectangle — a monitor? A screen? I cannot tell.

Here is what I need to know as an animator and cannot answer from this panel: Where is the camera? I can infer it is roughly eye-level with Cosmo, slightly low — maybe. But is it? Or is this the default "place character in center, note the emotion" non-decision that beginners make when they run out of ideas?

The brief says MED-WIDE. Cosmo SKEPTICAL, arms crossed, asymmetric brow — yes, I see that. But where is LUMA in relation to COSMO in three-dimensional space? She is a blob behind him at upper-right. I do not know if she is across the room, three feet away, or standing on a chair. The spatial relationship between these two characters is this scene's emotional information, and it is not in the drawing.

Also: the whiteboard plan in the background. This is the scene's visual thesis — the elaborately doomed plan, the portrait of overconfidence. The brief says "visually beautiful — color-coded, organized." In this panel it is a white rectangle with partial text. It needs to be PRESENT. It is as important as Cosmo's expression. An animator cannot work from this.

The glasses at 9° is correct. That detail I see and appreciate. Good rule-following on the production note. But a character rule applied correctly inside bad staging is still bad staging.

**What needs to improve:** This panel needs a clear camera spec. Place the camera. Establish the distance between Cosmo and Luma in 3D space. Make the whiteboard a visual character — color-coded, readable, elaborately futile. Right now A2-03 is a placeholder that describes a scene rather than stages one.

### A2-04 [B]

Now we're talking. I stood up from my chair for this one too, but for different reasons.

The 2x2 investigation montage is the right structural decision for this beat. Four sub-actions — TV search, under furniture, desk examination, clue found — gridded and labeled. The energy is correct. LIGHT AND ENERGETIC, the brief says. The panel delivers that.

What works: the bottom-right "CLUE FOUND" quadrant does something the other three do not — it breaks the character's body language into a clear emotional shift. Arms up, "AHA!" visible. That is a beat landing inside a montage. That is real storyboard craft.

What does not work: the top-left and top-right quadrants are essentially identical spatial decisions. Luma crouching near an object, searching. The under-furniture shot (top-right, character horizontal) is more dynamic than the TV search (top-left) but neither tells me anything about WHERE IN THE ROOM the camera is. If an animator has to board these four shots into actual animation, they have four labels and four mood sketches, but no positional information about the space.

The desk quadrant (bottom-left) is the weakest. It is ambiguous — is Luma standing, kneeling, leaning? The object she is examining reads as a stick, or a tool, or possibly a flashlight. Make a choice and commit to it.

Also: WHERE IS BYTE in this montage? The plan says he is on the mantelpiece, refusing to participate. Is that information visually present? No. Byte's absence from an investigation scene is character information. It tells us something about his relationship to this plan. Put him in at least one quadrant — even small, even just a silhouette on the mantelpiece — and the montage goes from "Luma investigates" to "Luma investigates alone while Byte watches skeptically." That is a richer story.

**What needs to improve:** Differentiate the spatial logic of the four quadrants — camera position should shift between panels in a montage, not stay static. Include Byte as a visible (non-participating) presence. Clarify the desk quadrant action. These are revision-level fixes, not a redraw.

## Additional Note: A2-06 INSERT Panel

I reviewed `LTG_SB_act2_panel_a206_v001.png` — the phone screen failure INSERT — and I need to address this separately.

This panel is interesting and also problematic. The full-frame glitch static, "APP TERMINATED," the horizontal scan lines and chromatic aberration — this is a punchy visual idea. As an INSERT, a POV shot of a phone screen dying, it earns its existence. It communicates Cosmo's failure with force.

But here is my concern: this is NOT the emotional action of the scene. The brief specifies TWO panels for A2-06: the MED TWO-SHOT of Cosmo and Luma, and then the INSERT. Where is the MED two-shot? What we have delivered is ONLY the insert. The physical space of the failure — Cosmo's face, Luma's hand on his shoulder, the street behind them, the flickering streetlight that was supposed to matter — none of that is present.

You cannot skip the establishing shot and go straight to the punctuation. That INSERT is only effective if the audience already cares about the two people who just failed. If you only show the screen, you are telling the audience what failed. You are not showing them who failed.

The INSERT panel itself: B+. The missing MED two-shot: unexcused absence.

## Priority Fixes (Top 3)

1. **A2-03 requires a full restage with explicit camera spec.** The panel as delivered tells an animator approximately nothing about the 3D spatial relationships between Cosmo, Luma, and the whiteboard. The whiteboard itself — the scene's visual thesis — is functionally invisible. The question is not "did Cosmo look skeptical" (he did). The question is "can someone animate this scene from this panel" (they cannot). Redraw with camera position named, character distance established, and whiteboard rendered as a full visual presence.

2. **A2-06 is missing its MED two-shot.** The INSERT panel (phone screen failure) exists and works. The establishing MED two-shot — Cosmo and Luma on the street, aftermath of the failed app, the moment before she puts her hand on his shoulder — does not exist. An INSERT without its establishing shot is a reaction with no stimulus. Draw the two-shot. The INSERT becomes twice as powerful when it follows something.

3. **A2-04 montage needs Byte placed as a visible non-participant.** This is a single-revision fix that changes the meaning of the scene. Right now the investigation montage is "Luma searches." With Byte visible on the mantelpiece — arms crossed, refusing, watching — it becomes "Luma searches while the one being who understands this problem refuses to help." That is Act Two's central tension made visual. It takes ten minutes to add. Do it.
