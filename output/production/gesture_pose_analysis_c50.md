## Part 1: Gesture Line Analysis of Current Characters

### What a Gesture Line Is

The gesture line (line of action) is a single continuous curve that flows through the entire body from head to feet. It is the spine of the pose — the first thing an animator draws before any shape. A strong gesture line tells you the character's emotional state, physical action, and energy direction before a single detail is added.

**A straight vertical gesture line = a mannequin. A curved gesture line = a character.**

### Reference Analysis (from reference/shows/)

Studying the reference images reveals consistent gesture line principles across professional shows:

**Hilda (scene_hilda.jpg):** Walking through woods. Gesture line curves forward from head through torso, continues through forward-planted foot. The body leans into the walk — her weight is ahead of her center. Even in a simple walk, the gesture line is a gentle S-curve, not a vertical pole. Head tilts slightly UP (curious), body leans slightly FORWARD (moving) — these two directions create the S.

**Owl House — Luz turnaround (Foj_n_iXEAUaUIv.jpg):** Even in the "neutral standing" turnaround pose (front view), Luz's gesture line has a subtle curve. Her hips shift slightly left, her shoulders compensate slightly right, her head tilts the opposite direction from her hip shift. The 3/4 view makes this clearest — the gesture line has a visible C-curve from head through planted foot.

**Owl House — heartwarming scene:** Two characters leaning toward each other. Each has a strong diagonal gesture line converging at the point of connection. The lines of action tell you the relationship before you read any expression.

**Kipo poster (unnamed-768x1152.jpg):** Kipo's pose is a massive S-curve — hips pushed back, torso driving forward, head tilted down with intent. Weight on the front foot. The gesture line practically leaps off the poster. Every supporting character has a distinct gesture line too — the slouching friend, the wide-stance creatures. No two silhouettes are alike because no two gesture lines are alike.

**Kipo model sheet (KIP100_WolfB_CLR_v003_KT.webp):** Even the turnaround's neutral standing pose has weight on one leg, hip shift, slight shoulder drop. The model sheet does NOT show a symmetric T-pose — it shows a living pose with an internal gesture line.

**Expression sheet reference (using-an-expression-sheet.png):** Every expression in the grid has a BODY response, not just a face change. Excited = torso lean forward + shoulders up. Sad = shoulders dropped + head tilted down. Angry = forward lean + chin down. The body IS the expression — the face confirms it.

## Part 2: Weight Distribution Audit

### What Weight Looks Like

A character standing on the ground has weight when:
1. **One leg bears more weight than the other** — the hip shifts toward the weight-bearing leg
2. **The shoulder line tilts opposite to the hip line** — contrapposto/counterpose
3. **The head compensates** — tilts or shifts to keep the center of gravity over the feet
4. **The feet respond** — the weight-bearing foot is flat, the free foot may angle or lift

A character WITHOUT weight:
- Both legs are symmetric columns
- Hips are level
- Shoulders are level
- Head is centered
- Both feet are identical ovals at the same Y position

### Current Weight Audit

**LUMA:** FLOATING. Both legs are identical columns directly below the torso center. Hips are not represented as a separate structure — the torso rectangle sits directly on the legs with no pelvic tilt. Feet are symmetric ovals at the same baseline. She reads as a paper doll pinned to the background, not a child standing on a floor.

**COSMO:** FLOATING. Same problem. His wider, more rectangular build should make weight distribution MORE obvious (wider hip shift, more visible shoulder compensation), but instead the rectangular torso sits centered and level on symmetric legs.

**MIRI:** FLOATING. An elderly woman should have the most distinct weight read of any character — slight forward lean from age, weight favoring one hip (decades of habit), potentially one hand on hip or holding something for balance. Instead: identical symmetric stance to the children.

**LINEUP:** ALL FLOATING. Five characters, five identical weight distributions (none). The two-tier ground plane from C42 helps with spatial depth, but the characters themselves have no relationship to the ground they stand on. The warm/cool shadow bands read as "there IS a ground here" but the characters above them read as "we are not touching it."

### Specific Failures

| Character | Issue | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| ALL | Legs are identical symmetric columns | Weight-bearing leg should be straighter, free leg should angle or bend |
| ALL | Hip line is level (not represented) | Add pelvic wedge shape; tilt toward weight-bearing leg |
| ALL | Shoulder line is level | Tilt shoulders OPPOSITE to hip tilt (contrapposto) |
| ALL | Feet at identical Y position | Weight-bearing foot flat on ground line; free foot slightly above or angled |
| ALL | Head centered over torso center | Head shifts toward free leg side to balance center of gravity |
| MIRI | Identical stance to children | Needs distinct elderly weight pattern (see Part 4) |

## Part 3: Gesture Specification — Luma's 6 Expressions

This is Maya's construction target. Each expression specifies: gesture line shape, weight distribution, counterpose, and the emotional logic connecting body to face.

### Expression 1: CURIOUS

**Emotional logic:** Something caught her attention. She's leaning in to see it. Her body asks the question before her face does.

**Gesture line:** Forward-leaning C-curve. Head forward and slightly down (looking at something), torso angled 8-10 degrees forward from vertical, hips shifted back as counterbalance, weight on the balls of her feet.

**Weight:** 60/40 favoring the FRONT foot. Front leg slightly bent (ready to move closer). Back leg straighter, planted. Hip line tilts 3-4 degrees: higher on the back-leg side.

**Counterpose:** Shoulders tilt opposite to hips — forward shoulder drops slightly (the side reaching toward the interesting thing). Head tilts 5-8 degrees toward the subject (ear toward shoulder on the leaning side).

**Arms:** Asymmetric. One arm slightly forward (reaching or pointing, but NOT the C34 "pointing finger" — a slightly extended hand, palm-down, as if about to touch something). Other arm closer to body, slightly behind (trailing the lean). This arm could be touching chin (callback to THE NOTICING).

**Feet:** Front foot turned slightly inward (pigeon-toed lean). Back foot flat, angled outward for stability.

**Silhouette test target:** The forward lean should make this silhouette NARROWER top-to-bottom and DEEPER front-to-back compared to any other pose. RPD vs DETERMINED should be <=75%.

### Expression 2: DETERMINED

**Emotional logic:** She has decided. She is going to do the thing. Her body commits before her mouth announces it.

**Gesture line:** Slight backward S-curve (power pose). Head up and slightly back (chin lifted), chest pushed forward, hips centered, weight planted and wide.

**Weight:** 55/45 even split — both feet grounded. Stance wider than neutral (feet at 1.2x shoulder width). This is the WIDEST stance on the sheet. Hip line nearly level but tilted 2 degrees: slight drop on the dominant side (right for Luma).

**Counterpose:** Shoulders pulled BACK and DOWN (the opposite of worried-up). One shoulder slightly higher than the other (asymmetry from dominant arm position). Head tilts up 3-5 degrees (chin lift = determination).

**Arms:** Asymmetric. Dominant arm (right) in a fist at hip level, elbow bent, arm pulled back slightly (ready to act). Non-dominant arm (left) crosses body slightly or hangs with a forward angle (about to gesture). NOT both arms straight down, NOT both arms overhead.

**Feet:** Wide stance, both flat, slightly turned outward (stable triangle base).

**Silhouette test target:** Widest stance silhouette. Should read as "planted" and "wide" compared to all others. RPD vs CURIOUS should be <=75%.

### Expression 3: SURPRISED

**Emotional logic:** Something unexpected just happened. Her body reacts before her brain processes — a physical startle reflex.

**Gesture line:** Backward-leaning C-curve. Head snaps back, torso tilts 10-15 degrees backward from vertical, center of gravity shifts behind the feet (she's about to stumble or catch herself).

**Weight:** 70/30 on the BACK foot. Front foot may lift slightly off the ground (startle reflex). The body's center of gravity is behind the feet — she is OFF-BALANCE. Hip line tilts sharply (5-6 degrees) toward the back foot.

**Counterpose:** Shoulders rise sharply (startle = protective shoulder hunch). One shoulder higher than the other (asymmetric startle). Head tilts BACK and slightly to one side (the direction away from the surprise source).

**Arms:** Asymmetric, wide. One arm up near face level (defensive/shield instinct, bent elbow, palm outward). Other arm flung to the side and slightly behind (counterbalance for the backward lean). The arm positions must be DIFFERENT heights and DIFFERENT distances from the body — maximum asymmetry.

**Feet:** Back foot planted flat (catching weight). Front foot lifted or on toe tip (just lost contact). The gap between foot positions creates the "caught mid-stumble" read.

**Silhouette test target:** Most asymmetric silhouette. The backward lean + one arm up + one arm out should create a completely unique outline. RPD vs any other pose should be <=70%.

### Expression 4: WORRIED

**Emotional logic:** Something might go wrong. She's pulling inward, protecting herself, but her eyes are still looking outward (she doesn't fully retreat — she's Luma).

**Gesture line:** Compressed S-curve. Head dips forward and down, shoulders round forward, torso slightly hunched, hips tuck under (the body curling inward).

**Weight:** 60/40 favoring one side (she has unconsciously shifted to her comfort side — LEFT for Luma). Weight-bearing leg is straight. Free leg is bent at the knee, foot turned inward (self-comforting pigeon-toe).

**Counterpose:** Shoulders round FORWARD and UP (protective hunch — the opposite of DETERMINED which pulls them back and down). This is the pose with the most shoulder involvement — the hoodie bunches visibly at the neck. Head tilts down 5-8 degrees but eyes look UP (worried look = face-down, eyes-up).

**Arms:** Both arms close to body but NOT symmetric. One hand gripping the opposite elbow (self-hold). The other arm crosses the body, hand near the gripping arm's wrist. This creates a compressed, wrapped silhouette that is uniquely narrow.

**Feet:** Close together (protective stance). One foot slightly behind the other. Weight-bearing foot flat, free foot on toes (ready to flee).

**Silhouette test target:** NARROWEST silhouette. Compressed inward. RPD vs SURPRISED should be <=65% (maximum contrast between most-open and most-closed poses).

### Expression 5: DELIGHTED

**Emotional logic:** Something wonderful happened. She can't contain the energy — it erupts upward through her whole body.

**Gesture line:** Upward-exploding S-curve. Head thrown back, chest opens, whole body extends upward. This is the TALLEST pose (she's on her toes, arms up, head back — maximum vertical extension).

**Weight:** On the balls of both feet (nearly on tiptoe). Heels lifted 3-5px off the ground line. The body feels LIGHTER than gravity allows — joy as anti-gravity. Hip line tilts 4-5 degrees (one hip pops to the side as the body celebrates asymmetrically).

**Counterpose:** Shoulders rise but UNEVENLY — one shoulder higher than the other because one arm is higher. The hip-pop creates a strong S-curve down the body. Head tilts back AND to one side (the "throwing your head back laughing" gesture).

**Arms:** Asymmetric Y-shape. Both arms up but at DIFFERENT angles and heights. One arm fully extended (fingers spread), other arm bent at the elbow with a fist (celebration pump). NOT identical twin-arm mirror raises. The asymmetry is the joy — symmetric arms read as "surrender," asymmetric arms read as "celebration."

**Feet:** One foot flat (but on the ball, heel up). Other foot lifted slightly (mid-bounce). The feet tell you she's about to jump.

**Silhouette test target:** TALLEST silhouette. The arm-up-Y + tiptoe + head-back should make this the maximum vertical extent. RPD vs WORRIED should be <=65%.

### Expression 6: FRUSTRATED

**Emotional logic:** The plan failed. She's angry at the situation (not at a person). The energy goes outward in a burst, then gets partially contained — she's mid-transition between explosion and regrouping.

**Gesture line:** Aggressive diagonal. Torso leans 10-12 degrees to one side (the "throwing a tantrum" lean). Head tilts opposite to torso lean (looking at the thing that went wrong). Weight shifts dramatically to one side.

**Weight:** 80/20 on one leg. The weight-bearing leg is bent (frustrated stomp). The free leg is barely touching — she just stamped or is about to. Hip line tilts dramatically (6-8 degrees) — the most extreme hip tilt on the sheet.

**Counterpose:** Shoulders twist slightly relative to hips (the torso has a rotational energy that a front-view reveals as one shoulder forward, one back — think of someone about to throw something). Head angle opposes torso angle.

**Arms:** Maximum asymmetry. One arm flung out to the side and DOWN (as if she just threw something or gestured in exasperation). Other arm bent sharply at the elbow, hand near head or pulling at hair (self-directed frustration). These must be at COMPLETELY different positions — one low-wide, one high-tight.

**Feet:** Asymmetric stance. Weight-bearing foot angled sharply outward. Free foot barely touching, turned inward. The stance looks unstable — frustration is an unstable emotional state, and the body shows it.

**Silhouette test target:** Most DIAGONAL silhouette. The side-lean + asymmetric arms + dramatic weight shift should make this completely unique. RPD vs any other pose should be <=70%.

## Part 4: Counterpose Study — Rules for Our Characters

### What Counterpose Is

Counterpose (contrapposto) is the principle that when one part of the body moves, another part compensates in the opposite direction to maintain balance and create visual rhythm. Even "neutral" standing in professional animation has counterpose:

- Hips tilt one way → shoulders tilt the other
- Head tilts toward the dropped shoulder (or away for attitude)
- Weight-bearing leg is straight → free leg bends
- The torso creates an S-curve, never a straight pole

### Why Our Characters Lack It

Our PIL generators build characters from the ground up as symmetric assemblies:
1. Draw legs at `(cx - leg_offset, ground_y)` and `(cx + leg_offset, ground_y)` — symmetric
2. Draw torso centered at `cx` — centered
3. Draw head at `cx` — centered
4. Draw arms at symmetric offsets from torso

Every element is pinned to `cx` (center-x). There is no hip offset, no shoulder offset, no head offset. The character is architecturally incapable of counterpose because every body part shares the same vertical axis.

### Counterpose Rules by Character

#### LUMA (12-year-old, energetic, round shapes)

**Standing neutral:** Subtle but present. Hip shift 3-5% of body width toward weight leg. Shoulder drop 2-3px on the same side as the raised hip. Head tilts 3-5 degrees toward the dropped shoulder. One foot slightly ahead of the other (never perfectly side-by-side).

**General rule:** Luma's counterpose is ROUNDER — her S-curves are gentle, smooth arcs. She doesn't have angular breaks. Her energy is bouncy, not sharp. Think of how a beach ball sits in a bowl versus how a box sits on a shelf — the ball has a natural, organic tilt.

**Per-expression counterpose angles:**

| Expression | Hip Tilt | Shoulder Tilt | Head Tilt | Overall Arc |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CURIOUS | 3° R | 3° L | 5° L (toward subject) | Forward C |
| DETERMINED | 2° R | 2° L | 3° UP | Backward power S |
| SURPRISED | 5° back-leg side | 6° opposite | 5° away from surprise | Backward C |
| WORRIED | 4° comfort-side | 5° opposite (hunched) | 8° down | Inward compressed S |
| DELIGHTED | 5° pop-side | 4° opposite | 8° back + side | Upward erupting S |
| FRUSTRATED | 8° weight-side | 6° opposite + twist | 5° opposite to torso | Diagonal slash |

#### COSMO (older teen, angular/rectangular shapes)

**Standing neutral:** More angular counterpose than Luma. His rectangular body means his hip shift creates a visible ANGULAR break at the waist (not a smooth curve). Shoulder compensation is sharp — one corner of the rectangle torso rises distinctly. His glasses tilt with his head.

**General rule:** Cosmo's counterpose is ANGULAR — his S-curves have distinct angle changes at the hips, waist, and shoulders. Where Luma flows, Cosmo bends at joints. His geometry already supports this (rectangular torso, rectangular head) — the counterpose just needs to ACTIVATE the angles instead of keeping them parallel.

**Specific Cosmo rules:**
- Glasses tilt 2-3 degrees with head tilt (NOT level when head tilts — they are ON his face)
- Arms default to asymmetric (one hand in pocket, one at side) — never both hanging identically
- Stance is wider than Luma's neutral (his base of support is broader — he's taller and more cautious)
- SKEPTICAL is his signature counterpose: hip shifted, one arm crossed, weight dramatically on one leg, head tilted with one brow raised. The whole body says "I'm not buying it" before you read the expression.

#### GRANDMA MIRI (elderly, warm/rounded shapes)

**Standing neutral:** The MOST counterpose of any character. An elderly woman has decades of habitual posture. Her neutral is someone else's expressive pose.

**General rule:** Miri's counterpose communicates AGE and WARMTH, not just balance. Her forward lean is slight but permanent (3-5 degrees — not a hunch, but a lifetime of leaning toward the people she loves). Her hip favors one side (LEFT — she has carried children, stirred pots, held phones on this hip for decades). Her shoulders are slightly rounded forward (not weak — gentle).

**Specific Miri rules:**
- Forward lean is PERMANENT across all expressions (3-5 degrees). She never leans backward — she leans INTO the world.
- Hip favors LEFT in all poses — this is a character signature, like Luma's pigeon-toed lean
- Head tilts are LARGER than the other characters (8-12 degrees in expressive poses) — she is physically demonstrative, she uses her whole body to listen and respond
- Hands are never idle — one hand is always doing something (holding a spoon, adjusting a towel, touching a cheek, resting on a hip). An empty-handed Miri is a Miri with no stage business.
- Cardigan line follows shoulder tilt asymmetrically — it hangs LONGER on the dropped-shoulder side. This is free visual storytelling at zero rendering cost.

#### BYTE (digital character, floating)

**Counterpose exception:** Byte has no ground contact and thus no weight-based counterpose. His "counterpose" is tilt-based: when one arm extends, his body tilts the opposite direction (like a helicopter compensating for rotor torque). His tilt-counterpose should be MORE exaggerated than the human characters because he has no ground friction — a small impulse creates a large tilt.

**Specific Byte rules:**
- Arm extension → body tilts opposite (Newton's third law of floating)
- Emotional lean → glow direction leads the lean (glow commits before body does — C38 rule)
- Cracked eye divergence is his version of facial asymmetry (C41 rule)
- Confetti responds to HIS physics even when he doesn't respond to the world's physics

#### GLITCH (non-humanoid)

**Counterpose exception:** Glitch's body is deliberately unstable — destabilized geometry IS the character. Counterpose rules do not apply. However, Glitch's bilateral eye system (C33) already provides expressive asymmetry: genuine feeling = both eyes stable, performance = right eye destabilized.

## Implementation Notes for Maya Santos

### The Core Code Change

The fix is architectural, not cosmetic. Every character generator currently uses a single `cx` (center-x) for all body parts. The fix:

```
# BEFORE (all parts share one axis):
torso_cx = cx
head_cx = cx
hip_cx = cx

# AFTER (offset chain creates gesture line):
hip_cx = cx + hip_shift_px    # hip shifts toward weight leg
torso_cx = hip_cx - shoulder_offset_px  # shoulder compensates
head_cx = torso_cx + head_offset_px     # head balances
```

Each expression defines `hip_shift_px`, `shoulder_offset_px`, and `head_offset_px` as signed values. The gesture line emerges from these three offsets. The arm positions then derive from `torso_cx` + arm-specific offsets (not from the original `cx`).

### The Weight Change

```
# BEFORE (symmetric legs):
left_leg_x = cx - leg_offset
right_leg_x = cx + leg_offset
left_foot_y = ground_y
right_foot_y = ground_y

# AFTER (weight-bearing vs free leg):
weight_leg_x = cx + weight_shift_px     # shifted toward weight side
free_leg_x = cx - weight_shift_px * 0.6  # less offset, allows bend
weight_foot_y = ground_y                  # flat on ground
free_foot_y = ground_y - free_foot_lift   # 0 = flat, 2-5px = light contact
```

### Testing

Run `LTG_TOOL_expression_silhouette.py` on each rebuilt expression sheet. The RPD (relative pixel difference) between any two expressions should be <=82% (current threshold) — but with proper gesture lines, most pairs should achieve <=75%.

Run `LTG_TOOL_character_face_test.py` to confirm face legibility is preserved after body changes.
