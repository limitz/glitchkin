# CRITIQUE — "Luma & the Glitchkin" Visual Development Package
## Critic: Victoria Ashford, Animation Director
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Materials Reviewed:** Style Guide v1.0, Production Bible v1.0, Character Lineup v1.0, Storyboard EP01 Cold Open v1.0, Style Frames 01-03 v1.0

---

## Overall Assessment

This package has genuine bones. That is not faint praise coming from me — most first-pass visual development packages I review are glorified mood boards with a premise stapled to the front. "Luma & the Glitchkin" is something more intentional than that. The core conceit is visually exploitable, the color system is structurally sound, and the character design philosophy is coherent. I can see how this could become a real show.

I can also see, very clearly, where it will collapse if the team does not fix it now.

The foundational problem is that this package is almost entirely written in text. It describes a show — sometimes with real intelligence and occasionally with genuine artistry — but at no point does it produce a show. We have a style guide without a visual style sheet. We have character design principles without a single actual character design. We have color specifications composed entirely of hex codes. We have storyboard panels narrated in prose instead of drawn. We have "style frames" that are color lighting scripts, not frames.

This is a development document that has confused itself with a visual development package. A pitchable package for a children's animated series requires actual images. What I am reviewing is a very polished pre-visualization document. Those are different things, and the difference matters enormously.

---

## What's Working

**The color architecture is genuinely excellent.** The warm-analog versus cool-digital split is not a novel concept, but the team has executed it with discipline. The decision to give Luma's hoodie cyan accent detail — tying her visually to Byte before they ever meet — is smart visual storytelling. The three-light breakdown in Style Frame 01 (cyan key, warm gold rim, lavender ambient) is the work of someone who understands how color communicates narrative. The rule that distant elements in the Glitch Layer recede to UV Purple (inverse of Real World atmospheric perspective) is a strong, specific world-building decision that will pay dividends in every Glitch Layer sequence.

**Byte is the strongest character concept in this package.** The cracked eye as an emotional tell, the two eyes pointing in literally opposite directions in Frame 03 (cyan toward Luma, magenta toward the void), the detail that his pixel confetti hesitates before landing in the real world — these are the kinds of specific, loaded visual details that make a character memorable. He has more personality in his design philosophy than most characters get in a full pilot.

**The cold open storyboard shows real dramatic instinct.** Panels 01 through 12 demonstrate genuine understanding of visual pacing — the controlled escalation from quiet to chaos, the single cyan pixel in a field of static, the silence before the simultaneity scream, the chip crumb on Luma's cheek. These beats understand what animation does that other media cannot. The comedy of the two-character mutual scream is correctly set up and correctly executed in the writing.

**The shape language system is coherent.** Circles/rectangles/triangles mapping to personality archetypes is standard practice, but it is executed with clarity here, and the ensemble three-way contrast (Luma horizontal-grounded, Cosmo vertical-stable, Byte angular-electric) is a functional compositional system.

## Critical Issues

### 1. THERE ARE NO ACTUAL IMAGES — SEVERITY: FATAL

This is the only issue that needs to be at the top of the list because it makes all other critique partially academic.

Style Guide: No visual reference sheets. No character sketches. No color swatch artwork. No example frame showing what "flat-graphic contemporary with glitch twist" actually looks like for *this* show specifically.

Character Lineup: No lineup image. A document describing a lineup in prose is not a character lineup. Every proportion note, every shape language decision, every color relationship described in that document lives or dies in the actual drawing. Saying "Luma's hair adds 0.4 heads of visual height" tells me nothing I can evaluate without seeing the drawing.

Style Frames 01-03: These are color lighting scripts. They are detailed, technically sophisticated color lighting scripts. They are not style frames. A style frame is a finished, fully rendered image showing the audience exactly what the show will look like. These documents describe an image with extraordinary precision — some of the color mixing analysis (orange hoodie under cyan key in Frame 01, lavender jacket going teal under storm light in Frame 02) is frankly impressive work — but I cannot see it. The studio executive cannot see it. The network cannot greenlight an image they cannot see.

The storyboard is 18+ panels of prose. Where are the panels?

The team has produced an elaborate and thorough written description of a show. They have not produced a show. This needs to be rectified before anything else.

### 2. GRANDMA MIRI IS UNDERSERVED — SEVERITY: HIGH

The production bible and the storyboard both position Grandma Miri as structurally central: she is the keeper of secrets, the long-game of Season 1, Luma's emotional anchor. But she receives approximately one paragraph of design description across the entire package, and that paragraph mostly consists of shape language that was already specified in the style guide.

We do not know what she looks like beyond "large rounded square shapes, silver-streaked hair, vintage tech motifs in clothing, usually has tea." For a character described as the long game of the season arc, she has been treated like a background prop. If she is as important as the bible claims, she needs a full character design treatment comparable to Luma, Cosmo, and Byte. The fact that she does not have one suggests either that she is not actually as important as claimed, or that the team has not done the work.

Neither answer is acceptable at this stage.

### 3. THE CORRUPTION IS UNDEFINED — SEVERITY: HIGH

The production bible introduces "the Corruption" as a distinct threat — "a deeper, more organized darkness within the Layer that the Glitchkin themselves fear." It appears in the villain shape language section of the style guide (inverted triangles and fragmented shapes). And then it completely disappears. There is no visual description. No color specification. No indication of how it is designed differently from regular Glitchkin chaos.

This matters because the Corruption is presumably the season-level antagonist. You cannot build a visual development package for a show without specifying what the antagonist looks like. When the team designed the glitch aesthetic, they designed the world-building texture. They have not designed the villain. A world without a visible villain face has a fundamental dramatic gap.

### 4. THE GLITCH LAYER AS A SPACE IS DESCRIBED BUT NOT DESIGNED — SEVERITY: HIGH

Style Frame 03 ("The Other Side") contains the most sophisticated color specification in the entire package, and it earns credit for that. But one style frame specification of one composition tells me almost nothing about how the Glitch Layer functions as a production environment.

Where are the environment designs? Where is the background artist's reference for what a Glitch Layer establishing shot looks like? How does the pixel-art flora actually render? What does "impossible geometry" mean for an animator drawing it on frame 2 of a 24fps sequence? The team has written beautiful prose about the Layer but has not cracked it as a production space. The Glitch Layer is half the show. It cannot be one style frame description.

### 5. THE STORYBOARD HAS NO VISUAL DIRECTION FOR ITS MOST COMPLEX FX — SEVERITY: MEDIUM-HIGH

Panel 07's "Byte phases through the screen" moment is described as "our show's first major FX moment." The panel notes specify that the pixel scatter "coming off Byte's emergence should be the most saturated, visually intense thing in the frame" and that "his trailing glitch artifacts should hang in the air as drifting cyan/magenta pixel shards."

Fine. But how does that work as animation? What is the frame-by-frame structure of the phase-through? Is this a 2D effect, a composited overlay, a background layer? The storyboard document, as written, is a director's intent document, not a production storyboard. That is a legitimate and useful document — but it is missing the technical specification that would let an FX animator actually build this shot. The same gap appears at Panel 13 (every CRT activating simultaneously — extremely complex lighting environment change) and Panel 14 (Byte's multi-bounce ricochet with compound smear trails).

The team has specified these moments beautifully as concepts and has not addressed how they will be executed.

## Concerns & Weaknesses

**The "Hilda / Owl House / Kipo" reference cluster is a trap.** I understand why it is there — it places the show in a commercial context, tells a broadcaster "this is in the lineage of things you already bought." But it also commits the team to a very crowded aesthetic neighborhood. What specifically makes this show's graphic language distinct from any of those three? The glitch aesthetic is the answer — but that answer needs to be argued visually, not just asserted. As written, the style guide's "but with a distinct digital-glitch twist" is doing a lot of work on a thin foundation.

**The pixel confetti signature is strong but may wear out.** The style guide correctly notes to use the glitch aesthetic sparingly. But pixel confetti is specified as appearing "near any glitch activity" and is present in all three style frames and throughout the storyboard. By the end of the cold open — based on the panel descriptions — the whole room is filled with it. If the visual signature is at full saturation in the pilot's first 90 seconds, it has nowhere to escalate to. I would want to see a deliberate escalation plan for confetti density across the first season, with strict early-episode limits.

**Cosmo's design is the weakest of the trio.** The character lineup document spends most of its energy on Luma and Byte, and Cosmo reads as the character the designer is least excited about. "Rectangles and ovals — stable, slightly stiff" is generic. His visual interest is almost entirely carried by the glasses, and relying on a single prop for all character distinctiveness is risky in animation. Glasses slip, get removed, break, change in different lighting — and when they are off, what does Cosmo look like? His "striped shirt (sage green and cream)" is pleasant but not memorable. Compare: Luma has the pixel hoodie, the specific hair physics, the cyan shoelaces, the warm-analog color identity. Byte has the cracked eye, the size, the digital movement physics, the entire glitch palette. Cosmo has glasses and a striped shirt. This needs work.

**The production bible's tone and content guidelines are thorough, but the "What's Out" list is more interesting than the "What's In" list.** Knowing that jump scares and cynical humor and romantic subplots are excluded tells me about editorial discipline, which is good. But "puzzle-solving and improvised problem-solving under pressure" and "slapstick and physical comedy" describe approximately 80% of children's animated series. The "What's In" list needs at least two or three items that are distinctive to *this* show specifically.

**The episode structure's 3-act format is competent but the act breaks are underspecified.** The production bible defines act lengths to the minute, which is useful for production scheduling. But the emotional function of each act break — what state the audience must be in when they hit the commercial — is not defined. "Act Two escalation. The situation gets worse" is not a structural specification; it is a description. What is the specific emotional commitment the Act 2 break must generate to hold a 7-12 year old through the interruption?

**There are no secondary or supporting character designs.** The style guide's directory structure specifies a "supporting" folder and an "extras" folder. These are empty. The cold open storyboard mentions "three to five townspeople visible in the background." What do they look like? The Millbrook visual vocabulary is described in the backgrounds section of the style guide, but has not been translated into a single human figure. If the town is "a character" as the production bible claims, it needs residents with visual personality.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Produce actual visual assets immediately, in this priority order:** (a) Character design sheets for Luma, Cosmo, and Byte — front, side, three-quarter view, plus an expression sheet minimum. (b) One finished background painting for Grandma Miri's tech den — this is the show's primary location and the pilot needs it. (c) Three style frames — actual rendered images, not specifications. (d) Storyboard panels — actual drawn panels, even rough, not prose.

2. **Design Grandma Miri now, not later.** She needs a full character sheet with the same rigor applied to the main trio. Her shape language ("large rounded squares") needs to be tested against the trio in a actual lineup image to confirm she reads as distinct and as authority figure without reading as threat. If she is the long game of Season 1, her visual design needs to carry secrets — there should be something in her appearance that, on rewatch, tells you she knew everything.

3. **Define the Corruption visually before the end of this development cycle.** What does it look like? Is it a character? A force? A swarm? Its visual design must be meaningfully distinct from the Glitchkin (who are chaotic but not evil) while drawing from the same digital vocabulary. Consider: if Glitchkin are corrupted data made visible, the Corruption might be something more fundamental — deleted data, null values, the absence of information. That visual language (absence, erasure, void-within-void) could differentiate it from the rest of the Glitch aesthetic.

4. **Give Cosmo a second visual hook.** The glasses are not enough. Look at what the glasses are doing in the style frames: in the storm frame, they are fully opaque with reflected cyan — his face disappears behind them. In a show built around character expression, a character whose face disappears in the most dramatic moments has a design problem. Consider: a secondary visual tell that is body-based rather than prop-based. His posture, his hands, his relationship to his notebook — one of these should be elevated to a visual signature comparable to Luma's hair or Byte's cracked eye.

5. **Develop a Glitch Layer environment guide.** This is not a single document request — it is a production requirement. The team needs: (a) A lighting reference sheet showing the Glitch Layer's standard ambient conditions. (b) A flora and fauna reference for pixel-art organisms. (c) A structural vocabulary for the floating platforms (what are the standard sizes, materials, circuit patterns?). (d) A data waterfall FX specification — how many columns, what speed, what does the code look like at readable scale versus distance? This work must happen before any Glitch Layer background painting begins.

6. **Build an FX reference sheet for Byte's movement.** His digital locomotion is described with precision: "micro-increments, hovering with a slight vibration, as if each moment of position is a separate frame at a slightly lower framerate." That needs to be animated reference, even as a rough cycle. The "phase through" FX from Panel 07 needs a frame-by-frame breakdown before it goes into production. You cannot ask animators to build what they cannot see.

7. **Rewrite the "What's In" section of the production bible's content guidelines** to include at least three specific creative commitments that are unique to this show. What does this show do with slapstick that Gravity Falls or Kipo or Hilda does not? What is the specific voice of its comedy? What is the emotional question that every episode is secretly asking? These need to be named, not implied.

8. **Establish a confetti density scale.** Define, in writing and with visual reference, what pixel confetti density looks like at Level 1 (trace presence, early show), Level 3 (standard glitch activity), and Level 5 (full chaos event, reserved for climax moments). Then enforce this scale. The visual signature will dilute itself if it is not managed.

---

## Verdict

"Luma & the Glitchkin" has the conceptual foundation to become something genuinely good. The color architecture is sophisticated, the character philosophy is coherent, Byte is a legitimately interesting design problem solved with intelligence, and the cold open storyboard — in the writing of it — demonstrates real dramatic instinct. If this team can execute visually at the level they have theorized in writing, there is a show here worth making.

But this package cannot be shown to a network, a studio executive, or a broadcaster. It is a pre-visualization document mistaken for a visual development package, and that is a critical error at this stage of production. The team has done the harder half of the work — articulating a clear visual philosophy — and has not done the visible half, which is producing the images that demonstrate that philosophy exists in practice. These things are not the same. An animation show is images, and this package does not yet have any.

The gaps in actual character design, background art, and rendered style frames need to be closed before this cycle ends. Until they are, everything else — the excellent color theory, the strong production bible, the impressive style frame specifications — is architecture for a building that has not been built.

Do the work. Make the images. Then bring it back.
