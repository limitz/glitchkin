## ITEM 1 — Act 1 Cold Open: 4-Panel Arc
### `LTG_SB_act1_coldopen_contact_sheet_v001.png`
**Grade: B+**

### Does the arc work as a cold open?

Yes. The four-beat logic — QUIET → CURIOUS → CURIOUS (escalated) → SURPRISED — is a sound cold open skeleton. QUIET establishes the world before the intrusion; CURIOUS carries the investigation across two panels at increasing proximity; SURPRISED lands the hook. You can feel the show's premise click into place at A1-04. That is the whole job of a cold open and this one does it.

### Panel-by-Panel

**A1-01 — ESTABLISHING (QUIET)**

This panel now has a version of Grandma Miri's kitchen that I can read as a *specific* kitchen, not a generic one. The warm palette — cream, amber, wood — does real work. The CRT's cyan glow bleeding through the doorway at frame-right is compositionally correct: it's where the eye exits, and we're *supposed* to exit toward it. That's a legitimate directorial choice now, not an accident. The "ESTABLISHING SHOT" label at the bottom is a crutch I'd kill in final output, but it's appropriate for a working board.

What still fails: Luma is not in this panel and the kitchen reads as static, almost decorative. There is no *person-ness* here — no sign of habitation. A half-eaten breakfast. A jacket on a chair. Something that says a child was recently in this room and left a mark. The world-before-intrusion needs a ghost of the character before she arrives. Right now it looks like a showroom floor.

The high-angle 3/4 perspective is correct for establishing — I can read the room geometry. Two-point perspective is clear and functional. No complaints on the camera choice.

**A1-02 — ARRIVAL (CURIOUS)**

This is the panel where the board earns its first honest camera score. Eye-level, Luma mid-stride, dotted sight-line connecting her gaze to the CRT doorway. The sight-line arrow as a graphic device is a production shorthand I accept — it's a working board, not a final animatic. The body language reads: she's stopped mid-step, head turned, one arm slightly extended. The ALERT expression on Luma's face is legible.

The problem is Luma's body. She's too frontal — she reads as a flat cutout standing in front of the kitchen backdrop rather than *inside* the space. Her foot isn't in mid-stride; she's just standing there with arms at her sides and a head turn. The stated brief says "one foot in the air (mid-stride)" — that is not what I'm seeing. The staging note in the code says mid-stride → frozen, but the image gives me standing-and-looking. Body language and motion state need to match the brief. An animator staging this shot from this panel would put Luma in static attention mode, not in a freeze-frame of movement. That's a different acting choice with a different emotional texture.

**A1-03 — DISCOVERY (CURIOUS escalated)**

Of the four panels, this one hurts me most because the concept is exactly right and the execution is the smallest — it renders at thumbnail scale as a tiny thumbnail of a darker version of A1-02. Luma is barely visible at MCU/profile, TV is barely visible at right. The room is dark and underlit. The pixel shape in the CRT static is present but tiny.

Here's what a director needs from this beat: SCALE SHIFT. We've gone from a wide kitchen (A1-01) to a medium room shot (A1-02) to what should be a medium close-up of Luma-AND-TV that makes both subjects feel *large* and *close together*. The intimacy of discovery — she's right at the screen now, nose almost to glass — should be palpable. I need to feel the screen's glow on her face. I need the TV to fill at least a third of the frame. I need Luma leaning forward so aggressively that her silhouette is off-center.

What I have is a dark medium shot where both subjects are small, the pixel shape is a dot I have to hunt for, and Luma's face gives me nothing I couldn't read from twenty feet away. The CRT glow on her face — which should be the lighting hook of this shot — is not legible. I cannot feel this scene. This panel is the one point in the cold open where a child watching television would stop being engaged, and that is the worst possible moment for that to happen.

**A1-04 — FIRST CONTACT (SURPRISED)**

This is the cold open's strongest panel and the one that justifies the whole sequence. The two-shot — Luma MCU left, Byte on CRT screen right — is spatially clear, emotionally legible, and carries the hook. Byte's INDIGNANT expression reads on-screen (arms crossed, slight squint noted via annotation). Luma's "jaw dropped / eyes MAX" is appropriately cartoonish for a surprised 12-year-old. The concentric rings emanating from Byte's screen presence give the panel energy and imply the scale of the encounter — this is not a small moment, the rings say so.

Two problems worth naming. First: Luma's body is a rectangular blue block. I understand the stylization but at this scale it renders as a flat billboard, not a character in space. Her body has no foreshortening, no lean-back that would complete the "instinctive recoil" described in the brief. She's surprised but her body hasn't processed it yet. Second: Byte is centered on the CRT screen rather than occupying the lower portion — the brief says "2/3 body reveal in the lower screen area," which would have given Byte a ground to stand on within the screen. Floating centered on a glowing square makes him look more like a logo than a character.

Both are fixable. Neither kills the panel. The emotional communication lands.

### Does A1-01→A1-04 create escalating emotional investment?

Yes, with the caveat that A1-03 is the weak link. The progression from QUIET to CURIOUS to FIRST CONTACT has the right shape — it moves toward intimacy at increasing speed. The arc-color coding (amber → cyan-teal → bright cyan) is a smart shorthand on the contact sheet that visually telegraphs the tonal shift. At thumbnail scale the whole arc reads in under three seconds.

The contact sheet itself is well-constructed. Arrow flow connectors between panels are a production improvement I didn't have in Cycle 4. The QUIET → CURIOUS → SURPRISED footer annotation is exactly the kind of context a director needs when reviewing at a glance.

## ITEM 2 — Act 2 Full Deck: NEAR-MISS → RECOGNITION
### `LTG_SB_act2_contact_sheet_v004.png` (11 panels)
**Grade: A-**

### Does the full arc land?

Remarkably, yes. Reading across the contact sheet thumbnail row: NEAR-MISS → ESTABLISHED → VULNERABLE → SKEPTICAL → INVESTIGATING → WALK+TALK → DETERMINED → HOPEFUL → FAILURE → RESIGNED → RECOGNITION. That's a complete dramatic structure with recognizable act-logic — setup, complication, escalation, reversal, climax. The team has boarded a short film.

At thumbnail scale, the arc beats are color-coded and emotionally distinct. The tech den establishing shot gives me the world; Byte's VULNERABLE MCU gives me the emotional stakes; the FAILURE panel gives me the reversal; and RECOGNITION (Miri) gives me the emotional release. That's the whole machine, and it works.

### Staging/Camera Gaps in the Sequence

**Gap 1: A2-01 → A2-02 — No spatial bridge between den and Byte's ECU.**
A2-01 is the wide tech den with Cosmo at his desk (BG) and Luma in the doorway (FG). A2-02 is Byte's MCU in close emotional territory. We have gone from establishing two humans in a room to a close-up of a digital entity with no shot to tell us where Byte is in relation to that room. Is Byte on the monitor? On a device Luma is holding? Floating beside her? The contact sheet gives no answer. The transition is aurally and emotionally abrupt in a way that would confuse a first-time viewer. One bridging shot is needed — even a cutaway to Luma holding up a phone or the CRT monitor showing Byte — before we move into the MCU.

**Gap 2: A2-04 → A2-05 — Location change with no establishing.**
The INVESTIGATING panel (montage) is interior. A2-05 WALK+TALK is clearly Millbrook Main Street exterior (I checked the environment reference). But the contact sheet gives no transition panel. Interior montage → suddenly outdoors. That's a location jump with no camera logic to bridge it. A brief exterior establishing shot — even a 3-second cut of the school exterior or street — is needed to orient the audience spatially before the walk-and-talk medium shot.

**Gap 3: A2-07 → A2-08 — The hardest transition on the board.**
A2-07 is Byte's ECU in a void (the RESIGNED beat, the most intimate shot in the deck). A2-08 is Miri's ECU in the kitchen doorway. Two extreme close-ups back-to-back. Without a pullback shot between them — even a brief medium shot of the room — these two ECUs will read as parallel eyelines that don't share spatial logic. We need to know where Byte and Luma are when Miri walks in. A medium "door opens" shot before the ECU on Miri would solve this and add dramatic value (the door opening is itself a story event).

## ITEM 3 — A2-08: Grandma Miri Returns
### ECU Choice Critique
**Grade: B**

### Does the low-angle ECU on Miri work for this emotional beat?

Partially — and the partial failure is worth examining closely because it reveals a fundamental staging question about what this scene needs to accomplish.

The ECU on Miri is technically correct for an emotional climax. The face fills the frame, the CRT amber-green glow catches on her cheek and forehead, the SURPRISED → KNOWING expression arc is readable from the panel: wide eyes (not scrunched), one corner of the mouth beginning to rise, cheek crinkles suggesting warmth rather than alarm. That is a well-constructed emotional panel. The choice of amber-green CRT glow as the key light is thematically perfect — it's the Glitchkin's light, and it's touching her as she recognizes them. The color is doing narrative work. I respect that.

The low-angle component is where I push back. A low angle — camera below eyeline — makes a character feel larger than life, imposing, powerful. That is typically the right choice for a character making a decision or assertion: "She belongs here" works as a *declaration* at low angle. But the emotional beat here is RECOGNITION, not declaration. This is the moment of *receiving* information, of having the world make sense again after long confusion. Recognition is an internal beat. It lives in the eyes. A low angle externalizes that — it makes Miri a monument rather than a grandmother.

The better choice: **level eye contact**. Camera at Miri's eyeline or *slightly above* (her face tipping down toward the camera, toward Luma's POV). This puts the audience in Luma's shoes looking up into Grandma's face — not because Miri is large and imposing, but because Luma is a child and this is the most important face in her world. The POV logic is more emotionally honest. Low angle is a power shot. Luma-eye-POV is an intimacy shot. This scene needs intimacy, not power.

**Alternative camera option:** If the low angle is kept, lean into it by including Luma in the lower foreground — back-of-head shot of Luma looking up at Miri in the doorway. Two-shot. Child in FG, grandmother in BG/frame-filling CU. That spatial relationship — small child, enormous grandmother filling the frame above her — would make the low angle work dramatically by establishing the power differential as *Luma's subjective experience*, not as a directorial statement about Miri.

The expression work on the panel is genuinely good. The face is specific. The wrinkle detail, the single lifted corner of the mouth, the wide non-fearful eyes — all of it is correct characterization. I'm not asking for a redraw of the face. I'm asking for a reconsideration of the camera angle as a storytelling choice.

## ITEM 4 — A2-01 vs A2-07: Same Story?
### Cosmo's Den (opening) vs Byte RESIGNED (climax)
**Grade: A- for cohesion**

This is the question I always ask on a cold read: do the first and last images of a sequence feel like they belong to each other? Can you hold up the first panel and the last panel and feel the distance traveled?

**A2-01:** Wide tech den. Cosmo at monitor (back to camera, BG), Luma in doorway (FG, DETERMINED, warm tones). The room is warm amber. The monitor screen is teal-blue. The composition has two focal points — Cosmo's desk and Luma's silhouette — in clear spatial tension. It's a scene about two people in different relationships to technology and to each other.

**A2-07:** Byte ECU in void. Single character. Teal body against near-black. The RESIGNED expression — droopy lid, downcast pupil, flat mouth, cracked eye glyph — is the most emotionally specific piece of characterization in the entire Act 2 deck. The circuit traces in the background are subtle and correct. This panel is genuinely excellent work.

The tonal connection between A2-01 and A2-07: both scenes live in that teal-on-dark palette world that belongs to technology, to the digital realm. A2-01 has the warm amber walls fighting with the teal monitor — the real world and the digital world coexisting uneasily. A2-07 has abandoned the warm world entirely. We are inside the digital space now. Byte is alone in it. That tonal shift — from warm real world with digital intrusion, to pure digital void — traces the emotional journey of the act. The palette is doing structural work across the arc.

They belong to the same story. They are two ends of the same throughline about what it costs to trust someone. I feel the distance traveled between them. That's the job.

## Top 3 Priority Fixes

**Priority 1 — A1-03 DISCOVERY: Redraw as true medium close-up.**
This is the single most important fix on the board. The cold open's discovery beat — the moment the audience commits to being curious — is currently a dark, small, compositionally passive thumbnail. Both Luma AND the TV screen need to fill the frame and be in clear visual dialogue. Luma's face must be CRT-lit from screen-right. The pixel shape in the static must be large enough to read as a character presence, not a dead pixel. If I can't feel the pull of that screen from inside this panel, I don't believe the cold open's premise. Make me feel it.

**Priority 2 — A2-07→A2-08 transition: Add one medium shot bridging panel.**
Two back-to-back ECUs in different settings is a spatial logic failure. Before Miri's ECU, I need one shot that establishes the room — where is Byte, where is Luma, and the door opens. Even a very simple wide-to-medium "door opens, silhouette in hallway light" gives the ECU on Miri the spatial foundation it needs to hit properly. Without it, the emotional climax is floating in compositional nowhere.

**Priority 3 — A2-08: Reconsider the camera angle.**
The low-angle ECU on Miri makes her a monument. This scene requires intimacy, not grandeur. Redraft as a level-eye or slight-above camera that places the audience in Luma's POV — looking into the face of the person who knows the secret. Alternatively: restage as a two-shot with Luma's back-of-head in FG, Miri's face framed in the doorway. Either version of intimacy will serve the RECOGNITION beat better than the current low-angle power-shot framing. The expression work is correct. The camera is the problem.

## Final Assessment

Act 1 Cold Open: **B+** — The arc works. A1-04 lands the hook. A1-03 is the one panel that will lose the audience and must be rebuilt.

Act 2 Full Deck: **A-** — The strongest storyboard output this team has produced. The emotional arc across 11 panels is coherent, color-coded, and dramatically structured. Three spatial gaps (A2-01→A2-02, A2-04→A2-05, A2-07→A2-08) need bridging shots. The work between the gaps is largely excellent.

A2-08 Miri ECU: **B** — Correct expression, wrong camera angle for the emotional function of the beat.

A2-01→A2-07 Cohesion: **A-** — These two panels feel like the spine of the same story. The palette carries the throughline even across nine panels of separation. That's mature visual storytelling.

The floor has risen. Match it on A1-03. Fix the spatial gaps. Reconsider Miri's camera angle. Then I want to sit with a real animator and talk about what we'd actually move in these shots.
