# Takeshi Murakami — Critique Cycle 8
**Date:** 2026-03-29

---

## SF03 Other Side Style Frame [B]

The atmospheric inversion is technically present and partially working. Far structures do purple out and darken; the gradient from void black at the top through UV purple haze to the mid-distance band reads correctly in the upper half. That much was done with discipline.

But the frame has a serious spatial fracture that undermines everything the spec promises.

The data waterfall is the problem. It is saturated electric blue — nearly the most luminous element in the entire frame — and it sits dead center of the composition, bisecting the image vertically. It acts as a wall. The eye strikes it and stops. The spec says the vertical axis of this frame is the journey from Luma (lower-left) to the infinite void horizon (upper-right). That journey is blocked. The waterfall is supposed to be a secondary element, a near-midground texture. Instead it reads as the primary architectural event of the image. Scale is wrong, position is wrong, luminance value is wrong for its assigned role.

Luma is small — correctly small — but the enormous cyan oval around her is baffling. That disc shape has no explanation in either spec document. It crowds her, flattens the sense of isolation that the frame's emotional brief demands. She should feel alone on that platform. Instead she is enclosed in a glowing ring that reads as a spotlight, which is the opposite of "grief-adjacent wonder." The spec explicitly states no warm light, but the problem here is not warmth — it is the imposition of a theatrical lighting rig that announces "protagonist here" rather than letting the void swallow her.

The "no warm light" rule is mostly honored. The corrupted Real World fragments carry their amber correctly — this is the best-executed detail in the frame. The terracotta wall fragment reads as genuinely wrong, invasive, sad, which is exactly right. The lamppost with amber corruption spreading from its base is the most emotionally effective element here.

The void does not feel like a void. The right half of the frame from roughly x=0.65 to the right edge is filled with large purple rectangles that are readable as furniture. They are too even in size, too gridded in their distribution. A void should have scale variation across several orders of magnitude. These shapes could be moved five pixels and the composition would feel identical. They have not been placed — they have been arranged.

The platform circuit traces are visible and correctly detailed. The pixel-art plants are charming and appropriately alive. The ring megastructure at barely-visible opacity is the single best atmospheric depth cue in the piece.

What is missing: genuine spatial depth in the right-side void. The frame has foreground and background, but the mid-distance band between x=0.5 and x=0.9 is confused — the large purple rectangles read at the same approximate depth as the data waterfall. The layering dissolves.

## SF02 Glitch Storm Style Frame [C]

This is the most disappointing piece in the set, and I want to be precise about why.

The storm does not feel like a storm. It feels like a celebration. The pixel confetti explosion in the upper-right quadrant reads as festive — bright, multicolored, cheerful squares cascading outward. The spec calls for kinetic terror. What is delivered is a fireworks display. The color range of the confetti is too wide and too even in saturation; cyan, white, pink, teal — all roughly equivalent values, all roughly equivalent sizes. Terror requires hierarchy. A storm requires a dominant threat color with subordinate notes. This confetti has no dominant.

The sky is nearly absent. The upper half of the frame is occupied almost entirely by the confetti explosion and flat dark field. There is no atmospheric depth to the storm — no sense that it extends into the distance, no graduation of value from the near storm-front to a far roiling sky. The spec notes "enormous threat" and "sky-filling." What is here fills roughly a quarter of the frame in one concentrated burst. The remaining sky is a flat gradient with no character.

The ground plane has excellent spatial logic. The sidewalk recession reads cleanly, the building facades create a reasonable near/mid read, and the characters have adequate scale relationship to their environment. This is the frame's strongest region. The warm terracotta and brown palette of the town feels grounded and correctly "Real World."

But the buildings themselves are doing no atmospheric work. They should be threatened — darkening, developing storm shadow, showing pressure from above. Instead they are flatly lit with no response to the storm overhead. The storm and the architecture exist in separate color worlds with no light interaction between them.

The dutch angle is not present. If the spec calls for a 4-degree clockwise tilt for tension, it is either not implemented or too subtle to read at production size. This is a significant omission — that tilt is the frame's tension signal, the formal device that separates "storm scene" from "incident on a sidewalk."

Byte reads as a dark blob. The Corrupted Amber outline exception specified for this frame appears not to be applied. He is invisible against the dark street environment.

The white geometric shape (lower right, appears to be some kind of device or object) is unexplained and spatially unanchored. It floats without context.

## Other Side ENV Background [B+]

Comparing directly to the style frame, removing the characters and confetti clarifies what the background itself is doing — and it holds up better than the style frame suggests.

The atmospheric inversion works more cleanly here. Without the cyan oval of Luma's spotlight dominating the left side, the actual depth layering reads: void black at upper edge, UV purple haze accumulating toward the mid-distance, far structures dissolving into the purple, mid-distance slabs at appropriate value reduction. This is correct procedure.

The far-right block structures are the same issue as in the style frame — too rectangular, too evenly distributed, insufficient scale variation. But without the character competing for attention, they are easier to read as a distance suggestion. They remain weak atmospheric depth work but they function.

The platform is well-constructed. The circuit trace grid is legible, appropriately technical, and the BELOW_VOID abyss beneath the platform edge creates genuine ground-to-void separation. That darkness below is doing real compositional work — it creates a sense that the platform is suspended, which is the whole point.

The pixel-art plants are excellent. Small detail, but they carry significant emotional weight — the Glitch Layer is alive. The acid green triangular forms against the void black platform read immediately as intentional life, not decoration. This is background storytelling.

The data waterfall remains overweighted — same problem as the style frame. At this scale and luminance it will compete with any character placed in front of it. In a character-absent plate it reads more acceptably, but a production background must be designed around character integration. This one was not.

The ring megastructure is correctly subtle. I want to name it as the single best depth cue in either Other Side piece — a structure so vast it barely reads as a shape. This is what the right side of the frame should feel like. The ring understands void scale. The rectangles on the right do not.

The mid-distance band between approximately 40% and 65% frame width is compositionally weak — there is a gap between the populated left side (platform, waterfall, nearby slabs) and the populated far-right (large purple rectangles). This gap reads as empty in a way that is not intentional void — it is simply unaddressed space. The spec calls for this area to have the corrupted Real World fragments (terracotta wall, road surface, lamppost). They are partially present but their presence is uncertain at this scale.

## Classroom Background [C+]

The classroom demonstrates technical competence and artistic confusion simultaneously.

The spatial architecture is fundamentally sound. Perspective recession of the floor is clear. The ceiling tiles recede correctly. The rows of student desks establish near/mid/far with reasonable success — front desks are larger, rear desks smaller, and there is a readable recession. This is the frame's best quality. Someone understood that a classroom must be legible as a space that characters will occupy.

But the lighting is broken in a way that undermines the entire piece.

The light source is unresolved. There appear to be multiple overlapping light cones or window light patches on the left wall and floor — these semi-transparent overlapping shapes create a muddy, indeterminate light quality. Each shape suggests a different reading of where the light comes from. They compete rather than collaborate. The result is a room that appears lit by a glitch rather than a window. For a Real World setting this is a fundamental failure — the Real World's warmth and coherence is its primary visual identity in this show. A classroom should feel safe, navigable, warm. This one feels irradiated.

The whiteboard/smartboard area is the compositional anchor and it is handled adequately — the board is readable as a board with content. But the horizontal striping at the top of the board reads as a user interface artifact rather than content. It does not read as anything a teacher would write or project.

The bulletin board (upper right, ochre/tan) is enormous. It dominates the right half of the upper frame. The colored sticky-notes on it are so large relative to the board that they read as artwork hung in a gallery, not student work pinned in a classroom. Scale is off throughout the right side.

The left wall has a severe compositional problem: the overlapping transparent rectangles create a zone of visual noise that reads as broken rather than architectural. Windows should be structural and confident. These read as compositing errors.

The room lacks soul. There is nothing in this classroom that tells me anything about the students who use it. A Ghibli background of a classroom would have a half-eaten eraser on the floor, a backpack slouched against a desk leg, a plant on the windowsill that someone remembered to water and then forgot. This classroom is a diagram of a classroom. It is correct in structure and empty in meaning. Backgrounds that merely set the scene are failures. This one sets the scene and nothing else.

The near/mid/far reads adequately through desk scale recession but the light mud prevents the room from reading as a whole. The viewer's eye cannot find a resting point. There is no compositional hierarchy. Everything is equally attended to — which means nothing is attended to.

## Priority Fixes (top 3)

1. **SF03 / Other Side ENV: Remove the character spotlight oval and redesign the data waterfall's luminance and scale.** The waterfall must function as a secondary mid-ground element, not the visual center of gravity. Reduce its width by 40-50%, reduce its luminance value, and allow the void behind Luma to breathe. Luma's isolation is the frame's emotional core. She cannot be isolated while enclosed in a glowing ring and standing beside a searchlight column. Fix this first — it breaks both the style frame and the ENV plate.

2. **SF02 Glitch Storm: Redesign the confetti as threat, not celebration.** Limit the confetti palette to a dominant threatening color (cyan or a cold near-white) with minimal subordinate notes. Introduce atmospheric storm depth — the sky must graduate from near turbulence to far roiling mass, not present as a flat field with a confetti burst attached. Apply the 4-degree dutch angle. Apply the Corrupted Amber outline exception to Byte. This frame's brief is kinetic terror and it is delivering kinetic cheerfulness.

3. **Classroom: Resolve the light source and add inhabitant evidence.** The overlapping transparent light patches must be unified into a single coherent window-light logic. One direction, one quality, one color temperature. Then: add three to five specific human details — an object on a desk, something pinned to the bulletin board that reads as a child's work, a detail that implies time has passed in this room and people have lived in it. A background that tells no story is not a background — it is a set dressing, and set dressing does not belong in this production.
