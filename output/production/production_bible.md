## 1. Logline & Elevator Pitch

### Logline
A resourceful 12-year-old girl discovers that the dead pixels on her grandmother's old CRT monitor are actually tiny creatures from a corrupted digital dimension — and she's the only one who can wrangle them before they glitch reality apart.

### Elevator Pitch
"Luma & the Glitchkin" is a comedy-adventure animated series for kids 7–12. Luma, an imaginative and scrappy middle-schooler, discovers that Glitchkin — mischievous beings made of corrupted data — have been leaking out of an ancient monitor in her grandmother's house. With the reluctant help of Byte, a grumpy reformed Glitchkin who rides her shoulder, and her best friend Cosmo, an anxious overthinker with a gift for improvised engineering, Luma must round up the Glitchkin before they scramble the town of Millbrook — and eventually the whole world — into digital chaos.

Think "Hilda" meets "The IT Crowd" meets a Saturday-morning cartoon that still remembers what Saturday morning felt like.

## 2. Target Audience

### Primary Audience
- **Age range:** 7–12 years old (core)
- **Gender:** Broadly inclusive — protagonist-forward story with ensemble that reflects diverse personalities and strengths, not gender roles
- **Viewer profile:** Kids who are curious, creative, slightly nerdy, and drawn to mystery and adventure without wanting genuine horror

### Secondary Audience
- Adults (18–35) who grew up in the CRT-monitor era and feel nostalgia for early internet/computer culture
- Parents watching alongside their children

### Platform Considerations
- **Primary:** Streaming (widescreen 16:9 presentation). Content must hold up on tablets and mobile devices — compositions and line weights must read on small screens.
- **Secondary:** Broadcast television — no content that would be flagged by broadcast standards (no blood, no realistic violence, age-appropriate peril only)
- **Accessibility:** Color choices must be distinguishable for colorblind viewers. Do not rely solely on red/green contrast for critical information. Captions and audio description tracks are planned.

## 3. Tone and Content Guidelines

### Tone
"Luma & the Glitchkin" lives in the space between **warmth and chaos**. The real world is cozy and familiar; the Glitch Layer is electric and unpredictable. The tone of any given episode can shift between these poles, but the show should always feel fundamentally **safe and funny** at its core — even in tense moments, the audience should trust that Luma will find her way through.

**Tonal pillars:**
- **Playful** — the humor comes from character, situation, and absurdity, never at a character's expense
- **Energetic** — pacing is snappy; the show respects children's intelligence and attention
- **Slightly surreal** — the Glitch Layer lets us break physics, geometry, and visual expectations; this is a feature, not a bug
- **Emotionally honest** — underneath the chaos, the show explores real feelings: frustration, loneliness, the fear of not fitting in, and the joy of unexpected friendship

### What's In
- Slapstick and physical comedy (Glitchkin antics, Byte's exasperated reactions)
- Puzzle-solving and improvised problem-solving under pressure
- Genuine friendship dynamics — conflict and reconciliation
- Light mystery (why do the Glitchkin keep appearing? What is the Corruption?)
- Grandma Miri's backstory parceled out as a long-running thread
- Visual humor and sight gags — the show is very aware it's a cartoon

### What's Out
- Realistic violence or injury (glitch effects are abstract, never gory)
- Adult relationship drama or romantic subplots among the main cast
- Cynical humor that punches down at the characters
- Jump scares or horror imagery — peril should feel exciting, not genuinely frightening
- Product placement or in-show advertising
- Stereotyping or tokenism in character representation

## 4. Episode Format

### Runtime
- **Standard episode:** 22 minutes (full half-hour slot with commercial time, or streaming equivalent)
- **Shorts / interstitials:** 3–5 minutes (standalone Glitchkin spotlight shorts to supplement the main series)

### Structure — Standard Episode (3-Act)

| Segment | Approximate Length | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Cold Open | 1–2 min | Hook the audience. Establish the episode's central glitch problem or threat. Often comedic. |
| Act One | 6–8 min | Setup. Luma's "normal world" before escalation. Introduce episode-specific Glitchkin or complication. End on a clear problem. |
| Act Two | 8–10 min | Escalation. The situation gets worse. Cosmo's plan fails. Byte's information proves incomplete. The Glitch Layer intrudes deeper into the real world. Emotional stakes peak. |
| Act Three | 4–6 min | Resolution. Luma finds the creative or emotional solution. Glitchkin contained (or befriended). Brief decompression / tag. |
| Stinger | 30 sec | Post-credits gag, usually a Byte moment or a tease for something to come. |

### Segment Notes
- Each episode should function as a complete story. Series-long arcs are seeded but never required for episode satisfaction.
- The Glitchkin of the week should feel distinct in personality, not just in visual design.
- Cosmo and Byte should each have at least one moment per episode where they carry the scene independently.

## 5. Main Cast Overview

### Luma (Protagonist)
- **Age:** 12
- **Role:** The wrangler, the improviser, the heart of the show
- **Personality:** Energetic, optimistic to a fault, occasionally reckless but never cruel. Luma charges at problems headfirst and figures out the details mid-leap. She has a deep emotional intelligence she doesn't always recognize in herself.
- **Visual hook:** Pixel-grid patterned hoodie (warm terracotta with electric cyan pixel pattern), oversized pockets, sneakers worn down at the heel.
- **Shape language:** Circles and rounded rectangles — approachable, trustworthy, in constant motion.
- **Relationships:** Best friend to Cosmo since second grade. Reluctant partnership with Byte that gradually deepens. Idolizes Grandma Miri even when she doesn't admit it.
- **Arc (season 1):** Learning that solving a problem and understanding it are not the same thing.

### Cosmo (Best Friend / Support)
- **Age:** 12
- **Role:** The planner, the worrier, the voice of reason that nobody listens to at the wrong moment
- **Personality:** Cautious, methodical, deeply loyal. Cosmo processes anxiety by making lists and contingency plans. He is braver than he thinks he is and the show knows it even when he doesn't.
- **Visual hook:** Thick-framed glasses slightly too large for his face, striped shirt (sage green and cream), always carrying a worn notebook.
- **Shape language:** Rectangles and ovals — stable, slightly stiff, grounded.
- **Relationships:** Has known Luma so long he's stopped being surprised by her. Has an intense intellectual curiosity about the Glitchkin that he'd never admit is excitement.
- **Arc (season 1):** Learning to trust instinct when the plan fails.

### Byte (Reluctant Companion)
- **Age:** Unknown (describes himself as "early access")
- **Role:** The insider, the grumpy conscience, the comic foil with hidden depth
- **Personality:** Sarcastic, world-weary, and deeply private about what he actually cares about. Byte is a reformed Glitchkin who no longer fits in with the chaos of his kind. He is not nice. He is useful. He occasionally crosses into something adjacent to kind and immediately denies it.
- **Visual hook:** Approximately 6 inches tall. One eye is cracked (dead pixel effect — it glitches when he's emotional). Oval body — wider than tall, buoyant, floating. Rides on Luma's shoulder.
- **Shape language:** Oval — buoyant and rounded, deceptively approachable. The contrast between his soft silhouette and abrasive personality is intentional. [Updated C38: chamfered-box/triangles design retired C8; oval is canonical.]
- **Relationships:** Technically works with Luma because it is preferable to the alternative. Has a complicated history with the Glitch Layer he has not fully disclosed.
- **Arc (season 1):** Accepting that belonging somewhere is not the same as surrendering who you are.

## 6. World Overview

### The Real World — Millbrook

Millbrook is a small, slightly eccentric town that feels like it was built by someone who had seen a real town described to them but never actually visited one. Buildings lean. Streets curve without reason. Everyone has too many opinions about local history.

**Rules of the real world:**
- Physics apply — mostly. Exaggerated cartoon physics (falls, impacts) are permitted, but the world has weight and consequence.
- Glitch elements are visible only to Luma (at first) and to Byte. Cosmo gains awareness over the course of season 1.
- Glitch contamination is always visible as a visual intrusion: cyan halos around affected objects, pixel-dither patterns on contaminated surfaces, objects that stutter in place.
- Adults are not oblivious — they notice something is wrong — but they consistently misidentify the cause (blaming the internet company, "that new cell tower," etc.).

**Key locations:**
- **Grandma Miri's House** — Luma's home base. Warm amber and sage. Dense with vintage technology. The CRT monitor is in the study.
- **Millbrook Middle School** — Dusty lavender and sage green. Luma and Cosmo's daily reality.
- **Millbrook Town Center** — Terracotta and cream architecture, visible power lines everywhere. The town is unknowingly built on a node of glitch energy.
- **The Junkyard** — On the edge of town. A treasure trove of discarded technology. Significant location for season 1's arc.

### The Glitch Layer

A digital dimension that exists as a parallel overlay on the real world. You cannot go to the Glitch Layer — it comes to you, or you fall through the cracks into it.

**Rules of the Glitch Layer:**
- Physics are suggestions. Geometry is unstable. Directions are not reliable.
- The Layer is structured like a corrupted operating system — there are rules, but they have exceptions, and the exceptions have their own exceptions.
- Glitchkin are indigenous here. They are not evil; they are chaotic. The distinction matters.
- The Corruption is a distinct and separate threat — a deeper, more organized darkness within the Layer that the Glitchkin themselves fear.
- Time moves differently. An hour in the Glitch Layer may be minutes or days in Millbrook. This is used sparingly as a plot device.

**Visual language of the Glitch Layer:**
- Void Black base (#0A0A14) with floating geometric platforms
- Pixel-art flora and fauna — recognizable species rendered in 8-bit aesthetics
- Scrolling code waterfalls — data cascades as visual background texture
- Impossible geometry — staircases that loop, horizons that curve up, shadows cast in the wrong direction
- See style_guide.md for full color specifications

## 7. Recurring Elements

### Grandma Miri
- **Role:** Mentor figure, keeper of secrets, Luma's anchor
- **Visual:** Large rounded square shapes — solid, protective, warm. Silver-streaked hair. Vintage tech motifs in her clothing. Almost always has a cup of tea and a knowing look.
- **Story function:** Grandma Miri knows more about the Glitchkin and the Glitch Layer than she lets on. Her backstory is the long game of season 1. She trusts Luma implicitly and never infantilizes her.

### The Glitchkin as a Species
- Glitchkin are made of corrupted data and manifest with wildly variable forms. Size ranges from marble to car. Limbs may be asymmetric; features may float; body proportions follow no biological logic.
- They are not villains. Each has a distinct personality, a specific glitch-type (visual corruption, audio scrambling, physics inversion, etc.), and a containable "core" that Luma uses to resolve each episode.
- Episodic Glitchkin should feel like characters, not obstacles.
- The Glitchkin have their own social dynamics within the Glitch Layer. Byte is an exile from this society.

### "Pixel Confetti"
The show's visual signature: tiny floating square particles that appear near any glitch activity. They are never explained in-world. They are always present near Luma in the Glitch Layer. This is intentional. It becomes significant in later episodes.

### Millbrook as a Character
The town itself is a recurring presence. Background artists should treat each Millbrook environment as a character with its own personality. The town reacts to glitch events even when residents don't notice — lights flicker, dogs stare at nothing, vending machines dispense wrong items.

## 8. Visual Style Summary

The full visual specification is maintained in the **Style Guide** (`/home/wipkat/team/output/style_guide.md`). New team members must read it in full. Key headlines:

- **Style family:** Flat-graphic contemporary cartoon with digital-glitch twist (reference: *Hilda*, *The Owl House*, *Kipo*)
- **Real world palette:** Warm ambers, terracottas, sage greens, dusty lavenders
- **Glitch palette:** Electric cyan, hot magenta, acid green, UV purple
- **Character line weight:** Medium-thick, Deep Cocoa (#3B2820), never pure black
- **Backgrounds:** No outlines; painterly-flat with texture overlays
- **Shadows:** Flat cel-shadow, one tone, no gradients
- **Signature element:** Pixel confetti particles near all glitch activity

**Shape language summary:**
- Luma: circles/rounded rectangles
- Cosmo: rectangles/ovals
- Byte: oval (buoyant, wider-than-tall) — canonical since C8. Glitchkin vary by type (triangles/jagged polygons for generic Glitchkin species; Byte's oval is the exception that marks his reformed status)
- Grandma Miri: large rounded squares

## 9. Software & Pipeline

**This production uses open source tools exclusively.** Proprietary software (Toon Boom Harmony, Adobe After Effects, Photoshop, etc.) is not permitted. If a required tool does not exist in the open source ecosystem, the team will implement it in Python.

### Primary Animation Software
**OpenToonz** — open source 2D animation tool (used by Studio Ghibli). Primary tool for character animation, rigging, and frame-by-frame work. Runs on Linux, Mac, and Windows.

### Digital Painting & Background Art
**Krita** — open source digital painting application. Used for all background paintings, character design sheets, color keys, and style frames. Native support for 16-bit color depth and PSD-compatible layered files.

### Vector Assets
**Inkscape** — open source vector graphics editor. Used for model sheets, style guides, title cards, graphic UI elements, and any asset that benefits from resolution-independent vector output.

### Compositing
**Natron** — open source compositor (equivalent to Nuke/After Effects). Used for all final compositing, color grading, and output rendering. Blend modes referenced throughout production documents — Screen, Add, Multiply, Overlay, Normal — use **Natron's blend mode terminology**, which follows the industry-standard naming convention consistent across open source compositors.

### Pipeline Automation & Scripting
**Python + Pillow/PIL** — image generation, batch processing, and pipeline automation scripts.
**Python + pycairo / svgwrite** — vector and SVG asset generation.
**ImageMagick** — CLI batch image processing and format conversion.

All pipeline scripts are tracked in `/home/wipkat/team/output/tools/README.md`. If a required tool does not exist, the team must implement it in Python and register it in that index.

### Delivery & Handoff Format
- **From OpenToonz:** PNG sequences (per shot, per layer where required)
- **Final composite:** Assembled in Natron for final color grade and output
- **Deliverable master:** PNG sequence archive; H.264/H.265 for distribution review cuts
- **Asset exports for review:** PNG, flattened, at delivery resolution

### Version Control
- **Binary assets** (PNG, layered source files, OpenToonz scenes): **Git LFS** (Large File Storage)
- **Production documents** (.md, .txt, .csv, scripts): **Standard Git**
- All team members must have Git LFS installed before committing binary files. Committing binaries to standard Git without LFS is a pipeline violation.

### Bit Depth
See Section 11 (Production Standards) and the Naming Conventions document for bit depth standards. Short version: **16-bit PNG for all production assets.**

## 10. Crew Roles

### Continuity Supervisor
**Reports to:** Alex Chen, Art Director

The Continuity Supervisor is responsible for:
- **Pixel Face Continuity Tracking** — maintains the canonical reference document for all pixel-face characters (Byte, Glitchkin, and any other digital-dimensional entities with pixel-grid facial features). Tracks approved expressions, eye states, and transition rules across all episodes.
- **Episode Asset Matrix** — owns and updates the master matrix of which assets appear in which episodes, flagging any asset that needs to be created, updated, or reconciled before an episode can go to production.
- **Cross-Episode Visual Consistency** — reviews all deliverables against established style and continuity references before Art Director sign-off stage. Catches drift in character proportions, color values, and line weight before it compounds.

This role is a prerequisite for the show entering multi-episode production. It must be filled before Episode 2 begins boarding.

## 11. Production Standards

### File Formats

| Asset Type | Delivery Format | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Character design sheets | PNG (transparent bg) + source file | 300 DPI minimum |
| Background paintings | PNG (flattened) + layered source | 300 DPI minimum |
| Color palette references | PNG + ASE (Adobe Swatch Exchange) or equivalent | Include hex codes in filename or embedded metadata |
| Storyboards | PDF (panels at 16:9 aspect) + PNG exports | Label panels clearly |
| Style frames | PNG (flattened) + layered source | Final hero frames at delivery resolution |
| Production documents | Markdown (.md) | Plain text, version-controlled |

### Resolution Standards
- **Delivery resolution:** 1920 x 1080 (1080p HD) — all final assets at this resolution or above
- **Aspect ratio:** 16:9 (widescreen). Safe zones: action safe at 90%, title safe at 80%
- **Working resolution:** Artists may work at 2x (3840 x 2160) and export down; do not work below delivery resolution

### Naming Conventions

**Canonical reference:** `/home/wipkat/team/output/production/naming_conventions.md`

The full unified naming system is maintained in the above document. All team members must read it before creating any asset. The old `LH_INT_` and `GL_` prefix systems used in earlier documents are **retired** — see naming_conventions.md for the reconciliation schedule.

**Summary format:** `LTG_[ENV/CHAR/FX/SB/PROD]_[descriptor]_v[###].[ext]`

**Version numbering:** Always three digits (`v001`, `v002`). Never overwrite — always increment the version number. Current canonical version is always the highest number unless explicitly marked `_FINAL` (see naming_conventions.md for the precise definition of `_FINAL`).

## 12. Production Notes — Critique Findings

### Cycle 9 Critique Findings (2026-03-29)

**CRITICAL SPEC VIOLATION — SF03: Byte Body Fill = Void Black**

During Critique 9, Naomi Bridges identified that the SF03 v002 generator (`LTG_TOOL_style_frame_03_other_side_v002.py`) contained `BYTE_BODY = (10, 10, 20)` — Void Black — instead of the specified `(0, 212, 232)` GL-01b Byte Teal. This caused Byte's entire body to be invisible against the UV Purple ambient background, rendering the character undetectable in the frame. The violation was present since SF03 was first built and went undetected across multiple cycles and critique passes.

**Canonical spec (permanent record):**
- Byte body fill = **GL-01b Byte Teal `(0, 212, 232)` / `#00D4E8`** — always, in all scenes, including Glitch Layer environments
- Exception: SF02 storm-scene ONLY — Void Black body with CORRUPT_AMBER `(#FF8C00)` 3px outline (narrative color statement: Byte consumed by Corruption). This exception is documented in byte.md Section 13A and must never be applied to non-storm frames.
- Void Black `(10, 10, 20)` / `#0A0A14` is a **background environment color**, never a character fill

**Resolution:** Jordan Reed rebuilt SF03 as v003 in Cycle 19, correcting the body fill and fixing eye legibility (eye_r formula multiplier increased for 30px+ diameter; Void Black slash removed from magenta eye). Sam Kowalski performed a color review pass on v003 to confirm compliance.

**Lesson for all team members:** When building any scene generator, always sample the rendered output against the background and verify that each character element is visually distinct. A body fill that matches the background is a P0 failure regardless of whether the spec value looks "correct" in code.
