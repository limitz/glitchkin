## Beat-by-Beat Outline: P03–P24

### P03 — FIRST CONTACT: THE PIXEL ARRIVES
**Beat:** The static has a new element. A single cyan pixel appears in the lower-right corner of the CRT screen face. It is tiny. It is precise. It does not belong.

**Emotional function:** This is the show's visual motif at its most elemental — a single point of difference in a field of noise. The audience doesn't know yet whether this is signal or artifact. Luma has turned toward it. She does not look surprised. *She has been watching.*

**Story note:** The pixel should not appear dramatically. It should appear the way Luma notices things — it was already there when we started looking. No flash, no sound cue. Just: there it is.

**Story gap (between P02 and P03):** We don't see the moment Luma turns. She is already oriented toward the screen when P03 opens. Diego should consider whether P02 holds on her turning — or whether the cut itself does that work. If the cut handles it, no gap. If P02 ends on Luma still facing away from us, P03 needs a transitional beat: her head turn, her decision to close the distance.

---

### P04 — THE ROOM RESPONDS
**Beat:** Wide interior. The cyan bleeds slightly beyond the screen — a directional source. The warm domestic light of the den is still dominant. Something is contesting it.

**Emotional function:** Two temperatures in one frame. This is the visual grammar of the whole show established in miniature: Real World warm, Glitch Layer cool, the boundary between them the CRT screen. The audience may not consciously register this yet. They will feel it.

**Story note:** Luma's posture here is key. She is not alarmed. She is *attentive.* The distinction matters — this is someone who has been here before in other forms (dead pixels, ceiling grid tiles, flickering projector). She knows how to be interested without being afraid yet.

---

### P05 — LOOKING BACK
**Beat:** MCU monitor from inside the shelf, looking out. The pixel cluster is growing: 8–12 cyan pixels. Through the static, Luma's warm form is blurred in the background.

**Emotional function:** The camera has shifted perspective. We are no longer with Luma — we are with whatever is on the other side of the glass. *Something is watching her.* This is the reversal of the pre-credits tag: there, Luma watched a dead pixel. Here, a cluster of dead pixels is watching Luma. The show's argument — that seeing goes both ways — begins here.

**Story gap (between P04 and P05):** The perspective shift is significant. If it happens as a cut, the audience needs a half-beat in P04 (or the end of P04) where something on the screen suggests an interior gaze — the pixel cluster orienting toward Luma, or the static developing a directionality. Without that plant, the P05 reverse-shot can feel like a stylistic choice rather than a story beat. *Flag for Diego: consider whether P04 ends with a subtle gaze vector from the pixel cluster, or whether P05 opens with a beat that establishes the POV before revealing it.*

---

### P06 — BYTE'S FACE
**Beat:** CU of the monitor screen. Byte's face, pressed flat against the glass from inside. His expression: DISGUSTED. Curious. Assessing. He is looking at her.

**Emotional function:** The show's central comic engine introduced in one image. Byte is grumpy. Byte is skeptical. And Byte came through anyway. The face communicates the whole of his character: he is here against his better judgment, which is his constant condition.

**Story note:** This is the first full character introduction of Byte, and it happens entirely through expression. The cracked eye should be visible. The HOT MAGENTA scar diagonal should be legible at this scale. His DISGUSTED expression should read clearly — but it should also contain the curiosity. He is not dismissing her. He is assessing her.

---

### P07 — THE BREACH BEGINS
**Beat:** Med wide, low angle, Dutch tilt 8° CW. The monitor BULGES — the screen bows outward under pressure from inside. Dutch tilt: the room's geometry is no longer reliable. Crack lines at the bezel corner. Cyan distortion rings breaking past the screen boundary.

Byte phases through — lower half still inside, upper body emerging. Not gliding: *pushing.* This costs something.

**Emotional function:** The impossible becoming real. The Dutch tilt tells the audience: something has changed about this room and it will not change back. The straining quality of Byte's emergence is essential — he is not flowing through, he is forcing through. The breach point is not frictionless. This matters for the season's stakes: if going back and forth were easy, the CRT would not be a threat.

**FX reference:** `fx_spec_cold_open.md` FX Moment 1.

**Story note (Byte's expression mid-emergence):** Between P06 and P07, Byte's facial register shifts. In P06 he is assessing. In P07 he is working. There may be a transitional expression — EFFORT, not DETERMINED — that isn't currently in the expression sheet. *Flag for Diego/Maya: is there a mid-emergence Byte expression, or does DISGUSTED carry through the physical effort?*

---

### P08 — BYTE IN THE REAL WORLD
**Beat:** Byte full reveal, standing in the Real World. Six inches tall. Oval body. One eye clean electric teal; the other cracked. HOT MAGENTA scar diagonal. Pixel confetti drifting.

He looks around. He looks at his own body in the Real World. He looks at the room.

**"The flesh dimension."**

He says this like it offends him. Like he has been here before and liked it even less the second time.

**Emotional function:** The comedy landing. The absurdity of this creature in this domestic space. But underneath it: *he has been here before.* "Liked it even less the second time" is a story seed. Byte's history with the Real World is not new. The audience should feel the weight of "second time" without being asked to analyze it.

**Story note:** This is Byte's first spoken line. It establishes his voice register immediately: clipped, declarative, assessing his environment in the most efficient linguistic unit available. He is not performing for an audience. He is managing information.

---

### P09 — THE APPROACH
**Beat:** Med wide. Byte floats up off the floor (18 inches). Cracked eye scanning. He spots Luma on the couch. Normal eye iris shifts LEFT — toward her. He has found what he came through for. The cautious approach begins. He does not know she is already awake.

**Emotional function:** Byte's intent established without dialogue. He is not here by accident. He came through for a reason, and she is it. His caution in the approach is character: someone who has been burned by proximity, moving slowly toward something they need anyway.

**Story gap (between P08 and P09):** Byte's 360 scan of the room. P08 establishes the landing. P09 establishes the orientation. Between them is the moment Byte takes in his environment — the bookshelves, the worn couch, the ambient warmth of the Real World — and makes the choice to proceed. *This beat is currently implicit. Diego should consider whether P08's ending accommodates it, or whether a short bridging beat (Byte's gaze moving across the room, landing on Luma) needs to be explicit.* If skipped, the audience may not feel Byte's agency — he may seem to drift toward Luma rather than choose her.

---

### P10 — THE APPROACH: HIS PERSPECTIVE
**Beat:** OTS (Byte's POV). Looking at Luma's sleeping face. Byte in cool silhouette FG. Luma's warm skin and orange hoodie in the BG. Cyan glow catching her cheek from Byte's proximity. Two temperatures, one frame.

**Emotional function:** The show's visual grammar stated explicitly: FG cool, BG warm. Byte is the cool element approaching the warm one. But the fact that his proximity is beginning to light her face — his cyan glow on her warm skin — is the show's central emotional argument in miniature. He is already changing the light around her.

---

### P11 — THE EYES OPEN
**Beat:** ECU Luma's face. Closed eyes. Cyan glow on her face from Byte hovering nearby. Long hold. Nothing happening. Then — brow twitch. Eyes SNAP open.

**Emotional function:** The reversal of P05. In P05, we discovered something was watching her from inside the screen. In P11, we discover she was never as unaware as Byte believed. This is Luma's character in miniature: she doesn't announce attention. She was already attending.

**Story note:** The brow twitch before the snap should be distinct — not a slow waking, but the beat before a decision. She was not asleep. She was waiting. Her eyes open because she is ready, not because she was startled.

---

### P12 — NOSE TO NOSE
**Beat:** CU two-shot. Luma and Byte. Equal frame weight. Negative space between them. CRT visible camera-right background. Both of them very still. Both of them deciding.

**Emotional function:** The show's premise in one composition: the girl and the Glitchkin, equidistant, assessing each other. Neither is running. Neither is sure of the other. This is the hinge — everything before this was the approach, everything after this is the show.

**Staging spec:** Per PANEL_MAP.md P12 notes — equal presence ~30% each, ~40% gap, Byte at Luma's eye level.

---

### P13 — THE THRESHOLD
**Beat:** Mirror composition. Byte camera-right. Luma camera-left. The show's moral argument in one frame: the organic eye (open, wondering) facing center; the cracked eye (processing, not ready) facing outward.

**TITLE CARD: LUMA & THE GLITCHKIN**

**Emotional function:** The title card earns its position because we have seen what the show is: two beings who perceive things others miss, looking at each other across a gap, deciding whether to trust. The asymmetry of the eyes — one wondering, one processing — is the season's entire arc in a still image.

## ACT 1 ENTRY — P14 through P24 (approximately)

*Note: The panel numbering beyond P13 is extrapolated from existing references (P14, P15 confirmed; P16, P17 flagged by Diego). PANEL_MAP.md is the authoritative source. Diego should confirm panel assignment against the map before committing.*

---

### P14 — THE ERUPTION: BYTE RICOCHETS
**Beat:** (From `pilot_episode_outline_v001.md` A1-03) Byte phases through the CRT screen — this time fully, no half-emergence. He ricochets off the bookshelf.

**Emotional function:** The gear shift. The cold open was quiet, watchful, delicate. Act 1 opens in chaos. The contrast is the comedy — we went from a numinous moment of mutual recognition to a six-inch creature ricocheting off furniture at speed.

**Story note:** Byte's ricochet trajectory should feel physical and have apparent logic — he bounces off hard surfaces at predictable angles, he cannot pass through Real World objects (only through CRT glass). This establishes the rules of his Real World presence: he can impact, he cannot phase through, the laws of cartoon physics apply to him here.

---

### P15 — LUMA HITS THE FLOOR / GLITCH HAIR CIRCLE
**Beat:** Luma's attempt to intercept results in her hitting the floor. The Glitch hair circle (pixel confetti around Luma's hair from Byte's proximity) appears.

**Emotional function:** Luma's first failure at containing the chaos she invited. The Glitch hair circle is significant — it is the show's visual signal that Luma and the Glitch Layer have a special proximity. The confetti isn't Byte's; it's drawn to Luma. This should not be explained. It should just be.

**Story gap (between P14 and P15):** Luma's three attempts before hitting the floor:
1. The laundry basket (she had to get it — it was already in the den)
2. The throw pillow (improvised, immediately)
3. Reasoning (calling to Byte — he ignores her)

*These three beats exist in the outline as description but may not be assigned to storyboard panels. Diego: do these three attempts read as a montage within P14, or do they warrant discrete panels? The comedy value is in the escalation — each attempt less prepared than the last. If they collapse into a single beat, the escalation is lost. Consider whether P14 covers only the first ricochet + laundry basket, and P15–P16 cover the remaining attempts before Luma hits the floor.*

---

### P16 — FLAGGED BY DIEGO (next priority)
**Expected beat:** Based on sequence logic, P16 is likely the moment immediately before or at Byte landing on top of the CRT. This is the beat where the physical chaos resolves: Byte comes to rest, and the comedy beat transitions to the story beat.

**Story note:** If P16 is the landing, the emotional register needs to shift mid-panel. The chase ends not with a capture but with Byte choosing to stop. He lands on top of the CRT not because Luma caught him but because he decided to be caught. The difference is character: Byte is never out of control. The ricochet is him being difficult, not being lost.

**Story gap:** The shift from chaos to stillness. If P15 ends with Luma on the floor and Byte still in motion, P16 needs a clear beat that marks the transition. Options: (1) Byte stops in mid-air and descends deliberately, (2) Byte lands with a thud and the room goes quiet, (3) Luma's stillness on the floor reads to Byte as sufficient — he stops because she stopped chasing. *Flag for Diego: which of these reads correctly? Option 3 is most character-consistent — Byte responds to Luma's non-pursuit — but requires Luma's floor position to read as intentional, not accidental.*

---

### P17 — FLAGGED BY DIEGO (next priority)
**Expected beat:** The long beat. Byte on the CRT. Luma on the floor. Both assessing. This is likely the beat that maps to the outline's "A long beat. They are both assessing each other very carefully."

**Emotional function:** The show's second threshold. P12 was nose-to-nose in the cold open — exploratory. P17 is after the chaos — post-test. Byte has now seen how Luma responds to surprise (she doesn't run, she tries to solve it), and Luma has now seen how Byte moves through the Real World (purposeful, despite the apparent chaos). The mutual assessment here is between two people who have new information about each other.

**Story gap:** The transition from mutual assessment to Byte's line. Byte says: **"You can actually see me."** This is not a question. But it should not arrive immediately after P17 opens. There needs to be a held beat — both parties deciding whether to speak — before Byte commits to the sentence. *The held beat is the emotional weight. If it is not held, the line reads as information delivery. If it is held, the line reads as the beginning of something.* Flag for Diego: P17 may need to be two sub-beats — the assessment (silence, evaluation) and the commitment (the line). If they fit in one panel, the staging needs to accommodate both.

---

### P18 — "YOU CAN ACTUALLY SEE ME"
**Beat:** Byte's line delivered. The Act One turn.

**Emotional function:** Not a question. Not a statement of surprise. An acknowledgment — weighted, reluctant, and enormous. Byte has been invisible his entire existence in the Real World. He did not expect this to be different. The line carries the full history of all the times he went unseen.

**Luma's response:** She should not answer immediately. This is one of Luma's rare pauses. The line requires it.

**Story gap (between P17 and P18):** Luma's non-immediate response. The outline does not specify what Luma does in the beat before answering. Suggestions for Diego:
- Her eyes track to Byte's cracked eye specifically — she noticed the crack before anything else
- She sits up (she was still on the floor from P15) — small physical claim of presence
- She says nothing for two seconds, then: some version of "yeah" or a single-syllable confirmation that does not match the weight of what Byte has said

*Whatever Luma does, it should not be wonder-faced and speechless. That is not her register. She might say "yeah" with the studied casualness of someone who has been waiting their entire life to say it.*

---

### P19–P24 — ACT 1 SCENE A1-04: "THAT IS CURRENTLY SPREADING"
**Expected beats:** Byte's minimum-information explanation. The contamination established visually (cyan halos, pixel-dither on the refrigerator door). Luma's three questions (Byte answers one). The rate-of-spread line. Luma opens a new notebook page.

**Emotional function:** The show's working relationship begins. This sequence is all economy — Byte is managing bandwidth, Luma is taking notes. The comedy is in the efficiency: they have moved from a standoff to a briefing in under thirty seconds, because both of them are pragmatic.

**Story note for the contamination reveal:** The refrigerator pixel-dither should be visible before Byte mentions it. Luma has likely already seen it — she notices everything. The contamination becoming visible to the audience before Byte names it gives Luma's attention to it a silent beat: she saw it first. Byte's confirmation ("that is currently spreading") comes as verification of something she already suspected, not a revelation.

**Story gaps to identify (P19–P24):**

1. **Byte's mid-air position when explaining.** Does Byte stay on the CRT for the briefing or float to be closer to Luma? The choice is character: staying on the CRT is territorial (it's his point of origin, he wants to be near his exit); floating to Luma level is an implicit concession that they are going to have to work together. *Flag for Diego: which staging is correct for Byte's arc in the pilot? He should resist the latter, which makes it more significant if it happens by accident.*

2. **Luma's notebook.** When does she get it? She was on the floor. The notebook implies she retrieved it between P15 and P18. This is fine if the blocking allows it — but if the ricochet-chase sequence covered the whole den, the notebook was somewhere she couldn't access. *Flag for Diego: establish where the notebook is in P02 so its retrieval is coherent.*

3. **The transition to A1-01 (Kitchen, Morning).** The Scene A1-04 briefing is night/late night. Scene A1-01 (kitchen, morning, Miri) follows. The time cut from A1-04 to the next morning has not been assigned a panel. *This cut is a story gap: the audience needs to understand that Luma slept (or didn't sleep), returned to the kitchen in the morning, and the Byte/CRT situation is still happening. A simple smash-cut to the kitchen with morning light is sufficient. No intermediate panel required. But the cut needs to be deliberate — it should feel like morning, not a continuous scene.*

## What the Cold Open Is Doing (Summary for Directors)

The sequence P03–P13 + P14–P24 is building two parallel tracks:

**Track 1 — The Noticing:** Luma's perceptual signature (dead pixel → pixel cluster → Byte's face → Byte in room → Byte's line). Each step is a thing she already half-knew confirmed.

**Track 2 — The Watching:** The reverse perspective (P05 looking back at Luma, P09 Byte's cautious approach, P10 his POV of her). The audience learns before Luma does that she is being watched. This creates dramatic irony that resolves in P11 — she already knew.

These two tracks landing simultaneously in P12 (the nose-to-nose) is why the scene works. The resolution is not surprising. It is satisfying. We have been building toward both characters knowing the other is there.

Act 1 (P14+) breaks that register deliberately. The comedy of the ricochet sequence works *because* it follows the quiet of P12/P13. The contrast is the joke. Directors and animatic artists should resist the temptation to add energy to P12/P13 in anticipation of the Act 1 explosion. The stillness earns the chaos.
