## 1. LOGLINE

Twelve-year-old Luma discovers that the dead pixels on her grandmother's ancient CRT television are actually living creatures from a corrupted digital dimension — and the only way to save both worlds is to trust a grumpy, six-inch rogue named Byte, who would rather not be saved at all.

*A story about the people who see what everyone else scrolls past.*

---

## 2. SERIES CONCEPT

"Luma & the Glitchkin" is a 22-minute comedy-adventure animated series for children 8–12. Luma is the kid who notices everything — cracks that look like rivers, ceiling tiles with perfect grids, the flicker at the edge of a screen — and who stopped trying to show people because nobody ever looked. The Glitch Layer finds her because she is the only one capable of finding it back.

What makes this show irreplaceable is its emotional architecture: underneath the Glitchkin mayhem, this is a story about the specific loneliness of perceiving the world differently, and what happens when the universe finally confirms that your way of seeing is not a flaw — it is the mechanism. The comedy is fast and physical. The feeling underneath it lands slowly, the way all important things do. The show earns its laughter by earning its tears first.

The CRT television at the center of the story is not a MacGuffin. It is a heirloom — a machine that has passed through at least two generations of women who pay attention to things other people overlook. That lineage matters. The show is, at its core, about what grandmothers pass to granddaughters when they don't even know they're doing it.

## 3. WORLD RULES

### Three Worlds

**The Real World — Millbrook**

A small, slightly eccentric American town that feels like it was assembled by someone who had seen a real town described but never visited one. Buildings lean. Streets curve without apparent reason. The whole town sits, unknowingly, on a concentrated node of glitch energy — which is why strange things have been happening in Millbrook for as long as anyone remembers, and why residents have elaborate wrong explanations for all of it.

*What can happen:*
- Cartoon physics apply: exaggerated falls, elastic reactions, impact smears — but consequences are real. If something breaks, it stays broken.
- Glitch contamination intrudes visually: cyan halos on affected objects, pixel-dither patterns on contaminated surfaces, objects that stutter in place.
- Adults perceive glitch effects but misidentify them ("the cell tower," "the internet company," "something in the water").
- Cosmo gains awareness of the Glitchkin gradually across season 1. Other humans remain unaware unless directly affected.

*What cannot happen:*
- Glitch creatures existing in the Real World without a CRT or screen as an anchor point. They need a breach.
- Luma perceiving the Glitch Layer directly without either Byte nearby or a CRT screen active. She needs a window.
- Glitch effects persisting after a Glitchkin is contained. When they leave, the contamination heals (except for "the Corruption" — see below).

**The Glitch Layer**

A corrupted digital dimension that exists as a parallel overlay on the Real World. It does not have a fixed geography — it is structured like a failing operating system, with internal logic that has been patched so many times that the patches contradict each other.

*What can happen:*
- Physics are suggestions. Directions are not reliable. Time moves differently (an hour in the Glitch Layer may be minutes or days in Millbrook — used sparingly).
- Impossible geometry: staircases that loop, horizons that curve upward, shadows cast in the wrong direction.
- Glitchkin are native here. They are chaotic but not evil. The distinction is fundamental.
- Luma can bring objects from the Real World in with her (within the limits of what she can physically carry). They don't always come back the same way.

*What cannot happen:*
- Luma surviving in the Glitch Layer without Byte. She needs him as a guide and translator. Without him, the Layer's geometry becomes actively hostile.
- Permanent exits from the Glitch Layer other than through a CRT screen. The breach is always the CRT. Always Grandma Miri's CRT, specifically, in season 1.
- Cosmo entering the Glitch Layer in season 1. He is aware of it by the end, but he doesn't cross over until the season 1 finale — and only barely.

**The Other Side (The Corruption's domain)**

The deep interior of the Glitch Layer where the Corruption has taken hold. A place of ordered silence rather than chaotic noise — which makes it more frightening than the Layer proper, not less. Where the Layer is broken and alive, the Other Side is precise and dead. It is what the Glitch Layer would look like if something started fixing it — ruthlessly, without asking the residents what they wanted.

*What can happen:*
- Rules that applied in the Glitch Layer no longer apply here. Geometry is stable — which is the problem. Nothing should be this stable in the Layer.
- The Corruption can extend reaches into the Glitch Layer from here, corrupting specific Glitchkin, altering terrain.
- Time is frozen. Things that should be decaying are preserved perfectly, which is uncanny.

*What cannot happen:*
- Luma and Byte visiting the Other Side in casual episodes. This is late-season territory. The first time they go is a major story event.
- The Corruption acting through anything other than its established visual signature (organized geometry, amber-on-black palette, precision where there should be chaos) until it is ready to reveal itself.

### The CRT Television

Grandma Miri's CRT television is not ordinary hardware. It predates Millbrook's founding. No one knows exactly where it came from, including Grandma Miri (though she knows more than she says). The screen has never displayed a normal broadcast in living memory — it shows static, which is actually a compressed view of the Glitch Layer's surface. The dead pixels are breach points where Glitchkin have pushed through. Every time Luma powers it on, she opens a window. Every time she powers it off, the window closes — but what has already come through stays.

The CRT has its own rules:
- It can be used to call Byte (he is the only Glitchkin with a stable enough signal to be summoned rather than stumbled upon).
- It shows the immediate vicinity of the Glitch Layer corresponding to Millbrook's position — it is not a general-purpose portal.
- It is physically fragile. If it breaks, the breach point closes. This is the season 1 finale's central threat.

### The Glitchkin as a Species

Glitchkin are living data — entities that emerged from corrupted digital broadcasts decades ago and have developed their own culture, hierarchy, and mythology inside the Glitch Layer. They are not programs running tasks. They are citizens of a broken world trying to make it work.

- Size ranges from marble to car, with no reliable correlation to age or status.
- Each Glitchkin has a specific glitch-type: visual corruption, audio scrambling, physics inversion, memory distortion, etc. Their behavior is an expression of their type.
- They are not evil. They are chaotic in the way a flooded room is chaotic — not malicious, but indifferent to what they damage.
- Byte is an exception. He is a Glitchkin who developed awareness of the Real World and chose to try to navigate it. This made him an outcast in the Layer. He has not been back by choice.
- Episodic Glitchkin should be introduced as characters with personality and motivation, not as monsters with a health bar.

## 4. TONE GUIDE

### Five words that ARE the show:
**Warm. Electric. Reckless. Specific. Earned.**

### Five words that are NOT the show:
**Cynical. Gross. Frantic. Vague. Safe.**

### Expanded notes:

*Warm:* The show loves its characters, including the difficult ones. It never laughs at Cosmo's anxiety or Byte's grumpiness — it laughs *with* them because it understands why they are the way they are.

*Electric:* Pacing is snappy. The Glitch Layer crackles. Luma's energy is contagious. Even quiet scenes have a current running underneath them.

*Reckless:* Luma does not plan. Neither does the show. The best jokes come from situations that escalate faster than anyone intended, and the best emotional beats come from characters saying things they didn't mean to say yet.

*Specific:* Millbrook is not "a town." Grandma Miri's kitchen smells like crossword puzzles and burned toast. Byte's cracked eye glitches specifically when he's pretending not to care. Cosmo keeps records of every time anyone is right about anything. Specificity is how the show earns its emotion.

*Earned:* Every laugh lands because the character work is real. Every tearful moment lands because the show didn't cheat to get there. Nothing is manipulative. Nothing is cheap.

## 5. CHARACTERS

---

### LUMA

**Age:** 12
**Role:** Protagonist, wrangler, heart of the show

**Want:** To find the thing she's been looking for without knowing what it was. (Adventure. Proof that the way she sees the world matters.)

**Need:** To learn that solving a problem and understanding it are not the same thing — and that some problems require patience, not speed.

**Obstacle:** Her own recklessness. She charges through situations that require stillness. The Glitch Layer rewards her perception but punishes her impatience.

**Cost:** If she never slows down, she will always be alone in the most important moments — because the people who love her can't keep up when she's running.

**Internal Arc — Doubt in Certainty [v002 addition]:**
Luma's deepest structural flaw is not that she acts without thinking. It's that she trusts the world to confirm her instincts *before* she has to trust them herself. She charges in so fast partly because she's afraid of the pause — afraid that if she stops, the doubt will catch up, and she'll talk herself out of the thing she already knows is true.

The pilot's emotional architecture must build to a moment where she is most correct — where her perception is *exactly right* — and is also the most certain she has ever been that she is wrong. Not a moment of theatrical doubt after a loss. A moment of correct-and-doubting simultaneously: the breach is real, the fix is possible, and Luma is standing at the edge of the CRT with Byte on her shoulder genuinely uncertain whether she is a girl who sees things or a girl who *thinks* she sees things. She reaches in anyway. But we feel the weight of the second possibility in the half-beat before she does. That half-beat is the show's emotional signature.

**Appearance:** 12-year-old girl, 3.2 heads tall. Circular shape language — rounded, approachable, in constant motion. Pixel-patterned hoodie (warm terracotta with electric cyan pixel-grid — the hoodie visually bridges her world and the Glitch Layer). Wide reckless eyes, arched brows, asymmetric hair cloud that defies physics. Sneakers worn down at the heel.

**Voice Guide:**

*Three things Luma says:*
- "Okay but what if we just — " (interrupted by her own action)
- Describing what she noticed before anyone else did: "Did you see how it flickered right before it — no, never mind, it's fine"
- Naming things with too much confidence: "I'm calling him Jelly. He is Jelly now."

*Three things Luma would NEVER say:*
- "I don't know, let's wait and see." (She always has a theory. She always acts on it.)
- "Maybe this isn't our problem." (Everything is her problem if she can see it.)
- "That's weird, I must have imagined it." (She stopped doubting her own perception the moment Byte looked back at her through the static — but the doubt lives under that sentence, always.)

**Voice rhythm:** Fast, improvisational, full of mid-sentence pivots. She starts sentences she finishes differently than she planned. She uses present tense for everything, even the past. She does not do dramatic pauses — she fills them. Occasionally she fills them with the wrong thing, which is how you know she's scared.

**Social world — Millbrook Middle:** [v002 addition — see also Section 6]

At school, Luma occupies a specific social position: she is not unpopular, she is *uninvested*. Other kids know she's interesting; they just can't keep track of her. She is not a loner by rejection — she is a loner by distraction. The social cost of this is specific: she misses the maintenance of friendship. She forgets to text back. She disappears into fixations. Her closest relationships (Cosmo, Grandma Miri) survive because those people have learned to follow the signal, not the schedule.

Two named background students are canonical in the Millbrook Middle social world:

- **Dev Patel-Huang** — 12, in Luma and Cosmo's class. Loud, socially fluent, compulsively funny. He is the person who knows everything that's happening in every social circle simultaneously and narrates it like a sports commentator. He likes Luma in an abstract way and would defend her without hesitation but is perpetually confused by her. Useful narrative function: his social awareness provides the audience an external read on how others see Luma.

- **Preethi Okafor** — 13, one grade above. Science team, serious, terse. She once saw Luma staring at a ceiling tile for four minutes and when she asked what was wrong Luma said "the grid is slightly off-axis, see?" and pointed, and Preethi looked and said "oh" and they sat in silence for another three minutes. Not exactly friends. Not exactly not. Useful narrative function: she's the character who almost gets it — who is close enough to Luma's perception to make the audience briefly wonder if she'll also find her way into the Glitchkin world, which she won't (in season 1).

### BYTE

**Age:** Unknown. Describes himself as "early access."
**Role:** Reluctant companion, grumpy conscience, the heart of the story's emotional core

**Want:** To not need anyone. (Specifically, to not need Luma. To solve the Corruption on his terms, alone, and then go back to... wherever he'd go.)

**Need:** To accept that belonging somewhere is not the same as surrendering who you are.

**Obstacle:** His own history. Byte was cast out of the Glitch Layer's social order when he developed a preference for the Real World. He interprets connection as danger because the last time he trusted his community, they rejected him for it.

**Cost:** If he never lets anyone in, he will destroy himself trying to fix the Corruption alone — which is exactly what the Corruption is counting on.

**Appearance:** Approximately 6 inches tall. Oval body — wider than tall, buoyant, floating. One eye clean electric teal; the other cracked (dead pixel glyph — glitches specifically when emotional, which he will deny). Hot magenta scar diagonal across body. Pixel confetti trails from his underside. Rides Luma's left shoulder.

**Voice Guide:**

*Three things Byte says:*
- Clipped, technical, impatient: "That is incorrect. That is not how that works. You are going to do it anyway."
- Reluctant warnings delivered too late: "I should have mentioned this earlier—" (he always should have mentioned it earlier)
- Begrudging acknowledgment: "That was... not entirely wrong." (This is his version of congratulations.)

*Three things Byte would NEVER say:*
- "Thank you." (He will do the equivalent action — he will step between Luma and a threat without being asked — but he will not say the words.)
- "I was wrong." (He will act differently the next time. He will not narrate the change.)
- "Fine, I'll tell you everything." (Information from Byte is always parceled. He reveals things when he's ready, which is always later than Luma needs them.)

**Voice rhythm:** Short. Declarative. Clipped, as if he's managing bandwidth. He does not do sentiment out loud, though his actions frequently contradict this. His sarcasm is dry and precise — never broad. He sounds like someone who has spent a long time around corrupted data and learned to value accuracy as a form of survival.

### COSMO

**Age:** 12
**Role:** Best friend, planner, the voice of reason that arrives slightly too late

**Want:** To understand the Glitch Layer through documentation and analysis. (He wants to know exactly what is happening so he can predict it and prepare for it.)

**Need:** To trust instinct when the plan fails — and to discover that he has good instincts, he just never gave them a chance to prove it.

**Obstacle:** His own anxiety. Cosmo's need to prepare is also his need to not be surprised. But the Glitch Layer is made of surprises. He can either control his environment or be present in it; he cannot do both.

**Cost:** If he stays on the outside of every situation with his notebook, he will miss the moments when he was the only one who could have helped.

**Appearance:** 12 years old, 4.0 heads tall. Rectangle/oval shape language — organized, slightly stiff, always upright. Thick-framed circular glasses at a permanent 7-degree counterclockwise tilt (never level, even at rest). Striped shirt (sage green and cream). Flat hair with a single obstinate cowlick. Always carrying a worn notebook with a cracked spine.

**Voice Guide:**

*Three things Cosmo says:*
- "Actually, I have some notes on that." (He does. They are on the correct page.)
- Hedging at speed: "I mean, I *think* it should work, I haven't field-tested this specific configuration, but theoretically—" (he then produces the exact tool needed)
- Sincere, slightly formal praise: "That was a genuinely excellent instinct, Luma." (He means it. He keeps records of when people are right.)

*Three things Cosmo would NEVER say:*
- "Let's just wing it." (He knows what winging it looks like. He has watched Luma wing it in real time. He has filed reports.)
- "It's fine, nobody will notice." (He will notice. He always notices. That's the problem and also the gift.)
- "I told you so." (He never actually says this even when he did, in fact, tell them so. He finds it unkind.)

**Voice rhythm:** Precise but warm. Longer sentences than Luma or Byte. He completes his thoughts, sometimes to a fault. Uses qualifiers like "technically," "actually," "to be precise." Speeds up when he's excited, which he tries to hide, which makes him more obviously excited.

### MIRI (GLITCH LAYER REPRESENTATIVE / ELDER)

**Role:** Warm anchor in the Glitch Layer. The Glitchkin who chooses understanding over chaos.

**Want:** For the Glitch Layer community to survive the Corruption intact — and for that community to include Byte again.

**Need:** To accept that she cannot hold things together through warmth alone. Some breaks require confrontation, not comfort.

**Obstacle:** She sees the best in everyone, including the Corruption, which she refuses to name as a true threat until it is almost too late.

**Cost:** Her optimism is her protection. If she gives it up, she doesn't know what she becomes.

**Appearance:** A Glitchkin elder — warm, rounded shape language, the largest stable Glitchkin Luma encounters. Visually welcoming. Glows with warm amber tones that shouldn't exist in the Glitch Layer's cold palette — which is itself a story question.

**Voice Guide:**

*Three things Miri says:*
- "You made it. Good. Sit down. I have things to explain and not much time to explain them."
- "Byte told you about me? That means he trusts you. He won't say that, but that's what it means."
- Describing danger with careful warmth: "It isn't that the Corruption is evil. It's that it doesn't believe anything here is worth preserving."

*Three things Miri would NEVER say:*
- Dismissals: "That's not your problem." (Everything that touches her community is her concern.)
- Comfortable lies: She will avoid topics. She will not falsify them.
- Ultimatums: "It's us or them." (She has lived through enough of those to know they're usually wrong.)

### GRANDMA MIRI (Real World)

**Note on naming:** In the story, Luma's grandmother is named "Miri" and the elder Glitchkin in the Glitch Layer is also named "Miri." This is not a coincidence. The show plants this connection early and pays it off in the season 1 finale. Writers must not treat this as an error — it is a seed.

**Role:** Anchor, mentor, keeper of secrets — and the connective tissue between worlds.

**Want:** For Luma to have exactly the adventure that is coming for her. She has been quietly preparing for this since before Luma was born.

**Need:** To let Luma navigate it herself — to be the grandmother, not the guide. To have given the tools without having to hold the hand.

**Obstacle:** She knows too much. Every instinct she has is to protect. Holding back is the hardest thing she does.

**Cost:** If she tells Luma everything, Luma will try to plan it. And this particular thing cannot be planned.

**Appearance:** Older woman, large rounded-square shape language — solid, protective, warm. Silver-streaked hair. Vintage tech motifs in her clothing. Always has a cup of tea and a look that suggests she already knows how this conversation ends.

**Voice Guide:**

*Three things Grandma Miri says:*
- "Tea?" (Universally. In every situation. Including urgent ones. Especially urgent ones.)
- Planting information as observation: "Funny thing about that old television — it always turned itself on when I needed to think." (She watches to see if Luma files this.)
- The question behind the question: "Did you figure it out?" (She asks this about everything. She is always asking whether Luma trusts her own perception.)

*Three things Grandma Miri would NEVER say:*
- "I don't know anything about that." (She will say "I have a feeling" or "that's interesting" — she will not lie outright.)
- Panic: She does not escalate audibly. She goes very still.
- Condescension: She has never once spoken to Luma like a child. This is a constant in the show.

## 6. PILOT EPISODE CONCEPT — "DEAD PIXELS"

### Premise
Luma arrives at Grandma Miri's house for a two-week summer stay and immediately notices the ancient CRT television flickering in ways that make no sense. When she powers it on, something looks back at her through the static — and then pushes through.

### Act One — The Discovery

Luma arrives at Grandma Miri's house carrying a single backpack that is clearly too small for two weeks. Grandma Miri greets her with tea and the kind of look that says she has been expecting this visit to be different from the others.

Luma notices the CRT television immediately. Grandma Miri says she's had it since before Luma's mother was born. Luma asks why she still has it. Grandma Miri says it's reliable. Luma asks if it works. Grandma Miri says: "Define works."

That night, Luma can't sleep. She comes downstairs. The CRT is on. Nobody turned it on. On the screen, the static has a pattern — and the pattern has a face. Luma leans in. Something in the screen leans back.

She powers it off. Goes back upstairs. Lies in bed with her eyes open.

She goes back downstairs. Powers it on. "Hello," she says.

The screen erupts. Byte phases through — a six-inch oval creature that ricochets around the kitchen for three minutes, breaking absolutely nothing, but making it clear that everything is about to break.

**Act One End:** Byte, cornered on the counter, looks at Luma. Luma looks at Byte. Byte says, with great reluctance: "You can actually see me." It is not a question. It is the beginning of something neither of them is ready for.

---

### Act Two — The Complication

Byte is not a stray. He came through because something chased him — a glitch-pulse that has contaminated Grandma Miri's kitchen and is spreading, slowly, through the house's electronics.

Luma calls Cosmo. Cosmo arrives with a notebook, a collection of measuring tools, and the expression of a person who has accepted that his life is like this now. Cosmo cannot see Byte (yet), but he can see the visual evidence of contamination — the cyan-halos, the pixel-dither on the refrigerator door.

Byte, furious at being visible to a human, explains the minimum possible: there is a breach in the Glitch Layer, it's leaking, and if Luma doesn't help close it, the contamination will spread to every electronic device in the house and eventually the block. He specifies that he needs Luma, not Cosmo, because only Luma can see it. He specifies this multiple times. He is very pointed about it. Cosmo writes it all down in his notebook with quiet dignity.

**[v002 addition — Social Cost Scene]**

Earlier that day, before Byte appeared, Luma had a half-formed plan with Dev Patel-Huang to meet at the community pool — the kind of plan that forms in the last week of school and feels solid and then evaporates. Dev texted three times. Luma did not text back. She was watching the CRT static.

This is not played for drama. It is played for specificity. The scene — brief, maybe fifteen seconds — is Luma's phone lighting up on the kitchen table, Dev's name visible, while Luma stares at the CRT screen with her face pressed close. She is not choosing the Glitchkin over Dev. She doesn't even register the choice. She just... doesn't see it.

Cosmo, when he arrives, says with careful lightness: "Dev asked me if I knew where you were." It's the only mention of it. No resolution. It is a cost that Luma has been paying for so long she doesn't notice it anymore.

The contamination reaches the house's internet router. Every screen in the house activates simultaneously. Luma looks at the CRT. The breach is there — visible through the screen, a torn place in the Layer leaking cyan light.

She reaches in.

---

### Act Three — Resolution

Inside the Glitch Layer (just barely — Luma is leaning through the CRT screen, not fully inside), Luma can see the breach: a small tear in the Layer's fabric, cycling through corrupt data. It is not large. It is spreading.

Byte, on her shoulder, talks her through it in short, impatient sentences. The breach needs a signal — a clean data packet to act as a patch. Luma does not have a clean data packet. She has her phone.

**[v002 revision — Doubt in Certainty]**

This is the moment where Luma goes still. Not action-movie still. Actually still. She is leaning through a CRT screen into a dimension she has no scientific framework for, holding a phone, with a six-inch oval creature telling her to photograph the kitchen floor. And for one half-beat, she hears the version of herself that has been told her whole life that she notices things that aren't there. The pixel pattern on the kitchen floor is just a floor. The static was just static. She has done this her whole life — seen patterns where there are none — and she is about to act on it with both arms inside a television and a possible rupture in the fabric of reality depending on whether she is right.

She holds the half-beat. Her eyes go to the floor. She sees the grid.

It is exactly where she knew it would be.

She uses her phone's camera — pointed at the grid pattern on the floor of Grandma Miri's kitchen (the one Luma noticed the first day and traced with her finger) — to generate a clean geometric input. Cosmo, from the kitchen, holds the phone at exactly the right angle (guided by Luma's voice through the CRT screen, who got the angle from Byte, who got it from a 40-second rapid-fire knowledge dump he won't repeat).

The breach closes. The house goes quiet. Every screen goes dark.

Luma pulls back into the kitchen. Byte is on her shoulder. Cosmo is looking at the exact place where he calculated Byte should be, based on Luma's description, and is saying nothing.

Grandma Miri is in the doorway with tea. She looks at the three of them. She looks at the spot on Luma's shoulder.

"I thought that might be you," she says to Byte.

Byte says nothing. His cracked eye glitches once.

---

### Tag

Grandma Miri goes to make more tea. Cosmo opens his notebook to a fresh page. Luma looks at Byte.

"You knew her," Luma says.

Byte says: "Drink your tea."

He floats off her shoulder and disappears behind the CRT television.

Cosmo writes: *"Known to Grandma Miri. Prior relationship. Investigate."*

The CRT screen pulses once — a dead pixel winking.

## 7. SERIES ARC — SEASON 1

### The Overarching Question
Why does the Corruption exist — and what does it want with Millbrook specifically?

### The Season's Shape

**Episodes 1–4 (Establishment):** Luma, Byte, and Cosmo establish their working relationship. Each episode introduces a new Glitchkin causing a specific type of chaos, which Luma resolves through perception and improvisation. The Corruption is visible only as a background detail — slightly too-organized geometry at the edges of Glitch Layer scenes. Grandma Miri's knowledge is parceled: she confirms what Luma already figured out, never more.

Dev and Preethi appear in background school scenes — Dev as social traffic reporter, Preethi as distant satellite. Neither is a confidant. Both are witnesses to who Luma is before the Glitch Layer changes her.

**Episodes 5–8 (Complication):** The episodic Glitchkin begin acting differently — more organized, less chaotic. Something is influencing them. Byte starts giving Luma real information (in pieces, reluctantly). Cosmo begins to perceive the Glitch Layer directly — first as data anomalies he can measure, then as something he can almost see. The Corruption starts sending organized advance geometry into the Real World. The dead pixel on the CRT screen multiplies.

The social cost continues: in Episode 6, Dev corners Luma in the hallway and says, plainly, "You keep vanishing." Luma doesn't know what to say to this because she doesn't have a reason she can give. This scene does not resolve. It's the first time Luma's secret has a named consequence in the social world.

**Episodes 9–11 (Escalation):** Luma and Byte enter the deep Glitch Layer for the first time. They find the Other Side. The Corruption is not a force — it is a decision. Something in the Layer chose order over chaos, and it is enforcing that choice. Miri (Glitch Layer elder) confirms what she has been withholding: the Corruption is connected to the CRT television. It was always going to be Millbrook.

**Episodes 12–13 (Finale):** The Corruption targets the CRT directly — if it can corrupt the breach point, it can use it as a controlled portal into the Real World. Cosmo crosses into the Glitch Layer (barely, and only once). Grandma Miri's backstory pays off: she and Byte have history that predates Luma's birth. The season ends with the breach closed, the CRT physically intact but changed.

**[v002 revision — Byte's finale: non-verbal commitment]**

Byte does not stay by choosing out loud. The original v001 beat — "Byte choosing to stay in the Real World (reluctantly, out loud, using words, which costs him everything)" — is revised.

The revised beat: after the breach closes, the CRT screen goes dark for the first time in memory. Every other Glitchkin has returned to the Layer. The breach is sealed. There is nothing technically keeping Byte in the Real World. Luma is on the floor of Grandma Miri's kitchen, exhausted, Cosmo next to her.

She looks at the spot on her left shoulder. Empty.

She doesn't say anything. She doesn't look for him. She knows that this is the cost — that she was always going to lose something to close this.

A pause. Then: something small, approximately six inches, oval, settles on her left shoulder. It is warm in a way the kitchen is not.

Byte does not say anything either. His cracked eye is still.

*He just stays.* The camera holds on this for a breath too long — long enough that the audience understands: for someone who has never voluntarily stayed anywhere, this is the loudest thing he has ever said.

A final scene plants the existence of something beyond the Corruption — something older, further in.

### What Changes by the End of Season 1

- **Luma:** Has learned to hold the half-beat. Not always. Not easily. But she knows the difference now between a problem that needs speed and a problem that needs her to *look first.* She still charges in. But she knows what she's doing when she does.
- **Byte:** Is staying. He did not say so. He is there anyway.
- **Cosmo:** Has seen the Glitch Layer. Has not fully processed this. Is filling the notebook at three times normal speed.
- **Grandma Miri:** Has told Luma the thing she was saving for when Luma was ready. (We don't hear it. We see Luma's face when she does.)
- **The CRT:** Still on. Still showing static. One new dead pixel in the corner.
- **Dev:** Got a text back. Eventually.
