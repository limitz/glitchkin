## THE GAP THIS FRAME CLOSES

Two critics independently noted that despite the show's stated intergenerational visual language — "she came from here," the CRT as matrilineal heirloom, technology of seeing passed between women — no rendered pitch asset places Miri and Luma in the same frame. This relationship is the show's emotional spine. The pitch needs one frame that proves it exists.

---

## 1. THE SPECIFIC MOMENT

**Scene A1-01 — Grandma Miri's Kitchen, Morning. Pilot, Act One.**

Luma has been awake all night watching the CRT. She comes into the kitchen. Grandma Miri is already there, making tea, moving through the kitchen like someone who owns time.

Luma says: "Your TV is broken."
Miri says, without looking up from the kettle: "Define broken."
Luma says: "The static moves."
Miri says: "The static has always moved."
Miri hands Luma tea. That is the end of the conversation.

**The beat to capture:** the moment Miri hands Luma the mug. Both of them are in the frame. Miri is not looking at Luma — she is looking at the kettle, or at her own hands. Luma is looking at Miri. The mug is passing between them.

This is the right moment because:
- It is a pure two-character frame — no Byte, no Cosmo, no CRT in the foreground.
- The CRT can be a light source in the background — audible as a hum, visible as a faint cool glow through the den doorway.
- The exchange has already happened. We are in the beat after — the mug is in transit, Miri is already thinking about something else, Luma is trying to figure out what Miri knows.
- It is domestic and specific. It is not a dramatic moment. That is the point.

---

## 2. STAGING INTENT

**Miri:** Left of frame, turned three-quarters toward the counter. Her hands around the kettle or releasing the mug. She is not performing — she is just doing what she was doing. Her silver-streaked bun is visible (see Note below on accessory). Her rounded-square shape language is warm and substantial; she fills her side of the frame without competing for it.

**Luma:** Right of frame, closer to camera. She is slightly lower in frame than Miri — they are not level; Miri has a few inches on her, and the kitchen geometry (Luma leaning on the counter vs. Miri standing) should reinforce this. Luma is looking at Miri. Her expression: not alarmed, not asking — reading. This is Luma doing what Luma does: looking at something until she understands it.

**The mug:** Center of the frame. Not necessarily perfectly centered — it can be slightly off. It is the thing connecting them. If it lands in the warm light at the center of the two-shot, the frame reads in one glance: she is giving her something.

**Sightline:** Miri's sightline goes to the counter or kettle — not to Luma. Luma's sightline goes to Miri. They are not making eye contact. This is intentional. The relationship is asymmetric in the way that intergenerational relationships are: Luma is working hard to read something that Miri is not hiding but is also not explaining.

**Camera position:** Slightly low angle, as if a seated observer at the kitchen table. The kitchen should feel like it belongs to Miri — the camera is a guest. Miri's shape dominates the left; the cooler, slightly chaotic energy of Luma's side of the frame sits right.

---

## 3. EMOTIONAL REGISTER

Not "warm" in the generic sense. More specific than that.

**What the audience feels:** This woman has been here before. Whatever is happening with the CRT, whatever Luma stayed up all night watching — Miri has a relationship with it that predates Luma's birth. And she is not going to say so. She is going to hand her granddaughter tea.

The frame should hold both the warmth (the mug, the kitchen, the morning light, the domestic rhythm) and the question underneath it (why does she know). It is not a mystery frame — it is not sinister. It is the feeling of standing in a room where the walls have more history than you can read yet.

**The word:** Knowing. Miri's posture and sightline should communicate that she is not unaware. She is aware and she is not worried. That's the asymmetry — she's already at peace with this. Luma is still catching up.

---

## 4. WHAT THE CRT IS DOING IN THE FRAME

The CRT television is **a background light source**, visible through the den doorway at the rear of the kitchen. It is on. It is in static.

Its function in this frame:
- It provides a cool blue-cyan cast that enters the kitchen from the far doorway, competing softly with the warm morning sunlight coming through the kitchen window.
- It should not be bright — it is far away and the doorway frames it. It is an ambient presence, not a key light. Think: a faint digital halo leaking into the edge of an otherwise warm room.
- It is in the background, behind Miri's left shoulder if the staging holds as described. This places the CRT — and its cool digital world — literally behind the person who has history with it.
- Luma's position in the frame puts her slightly facing the doorway. She is on the side of the frame closest to the CRT's sightline. This is not coincidence in story terms — she is the one who was watching it all night.

**What the CRT must NOT do:** overpower the warmth of the kitchen. The kitchen is Real World. Morning sunlight is the key light. The CRT's cool glow is a note, not a voice. If it reads as a third character competing for attention, it is too prominent.

**Color temperature architecture for this frame:**
- Dominant: SUNLIT_AMBER — morning light from the kitchen window, left or upper-left source.
- Secondary: the kitchen's own warm domestic palette (Miri's objects, the tile, the worn surfaces).
- Accent: ELEC_CYAN / CRT_GLOW — far background only, through the doorway, soft.

See `output/production/fx_spec_cold_open.md` for canonical CRT glow colors. See `output/production/story/story_bible_v004.md` Section 3 for Millbrook kitchen world-rules.

**"The woman who owns this house hands the girl who inherited her eyes a cup of tea, and neither of them mentions the television."**

That is the frame.

## NOTE ON MIRI'S HAIR ACCESSORY (FLAG 05)

FLAG 05 is OPEN as of C44: the canonical MIRI-A chopstick hair accessory has been identified as a cross-cultural design error (Igbo-Nigerian/Brazilian heritage; chopsticks are East Asian hair culture). The story team recommendation (C43) and Maya Santos design assessment (C44) both support replacing the chopsticks with dark wooden hairpins. Alex Chen's confirmation is pending.

**For this style frame:**
- If Alex has confirmed the replacement before Jordan begins, use **wooden hairpins** (dark stained, slender, same bun anchor geometry as the chopstick pair).
- If Alex has not yet confirmed, check with Alex before committing the accessory detail in this frame. Do not use the chopstick design — it is flagged for replacement.
- The bun geometry and overall silhouette crown are unaffected by the accessory swap. The design job the accessory does (two slender elements anchoring the bun) is identical with either version.

## SUPPLEMENTARY CONTEXT: WHY MIRI'S KITCHEN

The kitchen was built by Hana Okonkwo (generator: `LTG_TOOL_bg_grandma_kitchen.py`, most recent version v006, C43). It includes canonical details: crossword puzzle, tea mug, burned-toast context, fridge magnets, morning sunlight. Jordan should use this generator as the foundation ENV or as reference — Hana's kitchen spec is canonical for this space.

Miri's heritage per story bible v004 is Igbo-Nigerian / Brazilian-descended. The objects in her kitchen — the specific dishes, tools, patterns — are drawn from Igbo and Afro-Brazilian material culture as texture, not as subject matter. This is already embedded in Hana's ENV work and should be carried through to any additions Jordan needs to make for character placement or foreground staging.

**The kitchen should not be redesigned for this frame.** The relationship beat works because the kitchen already belongs to Miri. Jordan is placing characters into an established space, not building a new one.
