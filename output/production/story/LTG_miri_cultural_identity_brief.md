## 2. GRANDMA MIRI'S CULTURAL HERITAGE — PRODUCTION DECISION

### 2a. The question

Miriam "Miri" Okonkwo-Santos carries a surname that signals Igbo Nigerian (Okonkwo) and Portuguese-Brazilian (Santos) heritage. Was this arbitrary, or is it intended to carry story weight?

### 2b. Story recommendation: the heritage is meaningful and should be made explicit

The surname should not be arbitrary. An arbitrary multicultural name in a cartoon pitch reads as demographic tokenism — it signals diversity without delivering it. Amara Diallo named this correctly. The right path is to commit: Miri's background is Igbo-Nigerian and Brazilian-descended, and this should be felt in the show.

**What this means for the story:**

Miriam Okonkwo-Santos was born to a family with Nigerian and Brazilian roots in an era when engineers with her background navigating the American tech sector was genuinely exceptional. She is a woman who spent her career being the first or one of very few. This history shapes her: her patience, her precision, her refusal to panic, her way of solving problems from first principles rather than received wisdom. She built her own frameworks because she frequently had to.

Her background also shapes the physical world of the show in ways that can be visually specific without being taxonomically displayed. The artifacts in her kitchen and den — the specific dishes, the patterns on things, the language on things, the tools she keeps — can be drawn from Igbo and Afro-Brazilian material culture without requiring a heritage showcase moment. She is not a character who explains herself. She is a character whose space explains her, if you look.

This is consistent with the show's core principle of **Specific** (the fifth tonal pillar): "Grandma Miri's kitchen smells like crossword puzzles and burned toast" is the right kind of sentence. The right approach to her heritage is that same level of specificity — embedded in objects, routines, and the texture of her home — not in biographical exposition.

**What this does NOT mean:**

- Miri does not explain her heritage to Luma or to the audience.
- No "heritage episode" exists in season 1.
- The show does not treat her background as a lesson or a thematic vehicle. It is simply who she is.
- The world of the show is already built: these additions are texture, not revision.

**Canonical statement for story bible:**

> Miriam "Miri" Okonkwo-Santos grew up between Nigerian and Brazilian communities and came of age as a software engineer during an era when her combination of identity and expertise was structurally exceptional. She does not discuss this. It is present in everything she touches: the way she organizes her workspace, the objects she keeps, the precision of her corrections, the warmth she offers without announcement. She has built her own frameworks for most of her life. She is not surprised that her granddaughter had to build her own too.

## 3. THE CHOPSTICK HAIR ACCESSORY — PRODUCTION DECISION REQUIRED

### 3a. The problem

The canonical MIRI-A design (grandma_miri.md v1.2) includes a "tall bun + chopstick pair" as the primary hair accessory. The design locks this in as the silhouette's crown element ("Bun-chopstick crown passes squint test at thumbnail").

Chopsticks as hair accessories belong to East Asian, specifically Chinese and Japanese, hair culture. Miri's established heritage is Igbo-Nigerian and Brazilian-descended. The chopstick accessory is a cross-cultural design error.

### 3b. Story recommendation: replace the chopstick with a culturally coherent accessory

The design function is clear: the bun needs a pair of slender, perpendicular elements to create the visual crown that passes the silhouette test. The chopsticks are doing a specific design job. The story recommendation is to replace them with something that performs the same design job and is culturally coherent with Miri's background.

**Recommended replacement: hand-carved wooden hair pins**

A pair of smooth, slender hand-carved wooden pins — dark stained, approximately the same length and diameter as chopsticks — passes the silhouette test identically while removing the cultural mismatch. In West African and Afro-Brazilian hair traditions, hairpins and hairsticks made from wood or bone are documented and used. The visual form (thin cylindrical pairs anchoring a bun) is transferable. The pins read from a distance as "wooden accessories in her hair," which is culturally neutral at minimum and culturally coherent at best.

**Alternative: a pair of thin metal pins** (similar to French épingles, common in many cultures) — slightly less visual interest but silhouette-safe.

**This is a design team decision.** The story team's position is that the chopsticks should be replaced. The specific replacement is Maya Santos's call, with Alex Chen signing off. The replacement must pass the thumbnail silhouette test at the same level as the current MIRI-A design.

**Action required: Alex Chen + Maya Santos to confirm replacement and update grandma_miri.md, character lineup, and any tool files that reference the chopstick detail.**

## 4. MILLBROOK — STORY SPECIFICITY DECISION

### 4a. The problem

Amara's lowest score. Millbrook is currently described as "a small, slightly eccentric American town that feels like it was assembled by someone who had seen a real town described but never visited one." This is internally charming, but it is also a dodge — it is a description of the *feel* of the town without locating it anywhere real or specific.

Amara's critique: the show's premise could support genuine historical specificity. It does not currently deliver it.

### 4b. Story recommendation: Millbrook is in the American Midwest, probably Ohio or Indiana

This is the story recommendation, not a locked canon decision. The reasoning:

The show's emotional architecture requires that Millbrook feel like a real place a real person left behind in their career and came back to. Miri was a software engineer — likely working in a coastal or Southern tech hub. She is now back in a small midwestern town where she raised her family. This is a plausible and emotionally resonant life arc. The tech career happened elsewhere. The CRT television predates that career. It was here when she arrived, or she brought it here.

**The Midwest specificity this enables:**

- Millbrook can sit on a Great Lakes watershed or an old industrial river — somewhere that had a modest tech or manufacturing economy in the 70s and 80s and has been a small quiet town since. This gives Miri a plausible context for being there: she may have come back for the quiet, or for a parent, or because the land was cheap when she wanted to stop proving herself.
- A midwestern Black community in a small town is a specific and real thing. There are communities of Igbo-descended families in the American Midwest with deep roots. The town having a modest but real Black community — not foregrounded as a diversity statement, just present, as in a real town — is more honest than Generic American Town.
- The "eccentric" quality of Millbrook (buildings lean, streets curve without reason) can sit alongside this specificity. A real eccentric midwestern town is more interesting than an abstract eccentric cartoon town.

**What we are NOT recommending:**

- A real named town. Millbrook is Millbrook.
- An explicit regional identity that needs to be referenced in dialogue.
- A setting that imposes regional stereotypes.

**What we ARE recommending:**

- The design team treats Millbrook as a midwestern American small town with specific visual reference points: post-war housing stock, old industrial materials, flat geography with a lot of sky, particular tree species (oaks, maples), specific signage styles.
- Hana Okonkwo to note: the classroom and hallway designs should reflect a school built in the 1960s-70s era, typical midwestern public school — linoleum, institutional colors, slightly too-large corridors. The existing classroom design is close. It can lean further into this with specificity.
- The community pool, the town center, any street scene: these should feel like they could exist in a specific corner of Ohio or Indiana. Not labeled. Just real.

## 5. SCHOOL NAME — CANONICAL DECISION

### 5a. Diego's request

Diego Vargas needs a canonical school name for the Millbrook Middle School hallway seal.

### 5b. Recommendation

**Millbrook Middle School — no rename needed.**

"Millbrook Middle School" is functional, consistent with the town name, and already appears in existing assets and story documentation. There is no story reason to rename it.

**The hallway seal should read:** MILLBROOK MIDDLE SCHOOL

Optional addition below the name, if the seal needs a founding year: **EST. 1962** — consistent with a midwestern school built during the postwar population expansion. This detail does not need story confirmation; Diego can use his judgment on whether to include it.

If Diego wants a school mascot/motto for the seal, the story team does not have a canonical answer. **Suggestion for Diego:** a generic midwestern school mascot (Wolves, Eagles, Falcons) that does not conflict with anything established. Not a Glitchkin or anything that echoes the show's supernatural elements. The school should feel like a real school, not a themed one.
