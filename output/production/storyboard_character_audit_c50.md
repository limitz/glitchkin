## Task 1: Panel-by-Panel Character Read Assessment

Evaluated at actual panel display size (800x600px standalone, ~360x220px contact sheet).
Three questions per panel: (1) Can you identify who it is? (2) Can you read their emotion? (3) Does body language communicate the beat?

### Rating Scale
- **PASS** — Character identity, emotion, and beat all read at panel scale
- **WEAK** — Identity reads, but emotion/beat depends on captions or annotations
- **FAIL** — Character is a colored shape; identity/emotion only via context or text

## Task 2: Minimum Character Rendering Requirements for Storyboards

Storyboard panels are NOT expression sheets. Characters appear at 50-200px tall inside a 800x600px panel. The requirements are different from full-size character art.

### What MUST read at storyboard scale (50-200px character height)

#### 1. SILHOUETTE (Priority 1 — most important)
At panel scale, silhouette is the ONLY reliable identification cue. Each character needs a silhouette that is unique and identifiable even as a solid black shape:
- **Luma:** Messy asymmetric hair cloud breaking the head circle + hoodie bulk (wider at shoulders, hanging past hips) + proportions (child, 4-head-tall)
- **Byte:** Inverted teardrop body, distinctly non-human proportions, single-eye bump at top
- **Cosmo:** Rectangular head (wider than tall) + glasses bump + narrow body
- **Miri:** Compact/round body + hair bun circle on top + shorter proportions

**Current failure:** Luma and Cosmo have nearly identical silhouettes (circle on rectangle). Byte works because his shape IS his identity.

#### 2. GESTURE LINE (Priority 2)
Every character pose needs a visible C-curve or S-curve through the body. Even "standing neutral" gets a 3-5 degree lean, one hip slightly higher. This is visible even at 50px tall and communicates the difference between "alive" and "mannequin."

**Current failure:** All human characters have vertical-axis symmetry in every panel. Bodies are centered on a straight vertical line.

#### 3. COLOR BLOCK (Priority 3)
The character's identifying color must read as a distinct, solid shape:
- **Luma:** LUMA_HOODIE orange (#E8703A) — this works already
- **Byte:** ELEC_CYAN (#00D4E8) — this works already
- **Cosmo:** Striped shirt (needs horizontal lines visible even at small scale)
- **Miri:** Cardigan warm tone + distinct from Luma's orange

#### 4. HEAD-TO-BODY RATIO (Priority 4)
At storyboard scale, the head needs to be proportionally LARGER than anatomically correct — 35-40% of body height — because the face is the expression carrier and at small scale a small head becomes an unreadable dot.

**Current failure:** Head is ~25% of body height. At 100px character height, the head is 25px and the face is ~15px wide. Expression is impossible.

#### 5. EYE AS EXPRESSION CARRIER (Priority 5)
At storyboard scale, detailed facial features collapse. The ONLY facial element that can carry expression is the eye region (eyes + brows as a unit). Eyes need to be:
- Large: 30-35% of head width
- Shaped per expression: not just dots in circles, but distinct SHAPES (narrow = angry, wide = surprised, droopy = sad)
- High contrast against skin

**Current failure:** Eyes are ~22% of head, drawn as circles with dots. Indistinguishable between expressions at panel scale.

### What does NOT need to read at storyboard scale
- Clothing wrinkles, fabric texture
- Nose detail, lip shape, ear shape
- Finger articulation (mitten hands are fine)
- Shoe detail
- Blush, subtle skin tone variation

## Task 3: Professional Storyboard Character Rendering — Reference Study

### What professional pitch storyboards do (from Hilda, Owl House, Kipo reference study)

**Key insight: Storyboard characters are rougher than expression sheets, but they still have GESTURE.**

Studying the reference images from `reference/shows/`:

1. **Hilda scene (scene_hilda.jpg):** Character is walking — weight clearly on one foot, body has a lean, arms have swing, hair has bounce. Even at thumbnail size, you can tell she is MOVING and HAPPY. The body communicates before the face does.

2. **Owl House scene (scene.jpg):** Luz at MCU — worried expression reads via: (a) LARGE eyes taking up 40% of face width, (b) distinct brow shape (curved, not straight), (c) asymmetric mouth, (d) body lean backward/away. At half this size, you'd still read "worried kid."

3. **Owl House ECU (scene2.jpg):** Emotional ECU — eyes are ENORMOUS (50%+ of face), distinct teardrop shapes, multiple highlights, tears as separate elements. The expression is unmistakable even at extreme reduction.

4. **Kipo model sheet (KIP100_WolfB):** Production model sheet shows: distinct hand shapes (4 variants), foot/shoe shapes, shadow treatment. Even the neutral standing pose has slight asymmetry and weight distribution.

### The pitch storyboard standard

Professional pitch storyboards are NOT finished art. They are rough. BUT they have:
- **Gesture line in every pose** — the body curves, it does not stand straight
- **Silhouette differentiation** — you can tell characters apart as solid shapes
- **Expression via body** — emotion reads from posture before you see the face
- **Large heads with big eyes** — the face region is exaggerated for readability
- **Hands that DO things** — even simple mitten shapes change per action

Our panels have excellent STAGING (compositions, negative space, camera language, depth temperature) but the characters inside those compositions are geometric primitives that do not act.

### Realistic target for our panels

We do NOT need Hilda-quality finished character art. We need:
1. Gesture line in every character in every panel
2. Silhouette that identifies the character without color
3. Head-to-body ratio of 35-37% (from Maya's prototype)
4. Eyes large enough to carry one broad expression state (wide/narrow/asymmetric)
5. Arms/hands that form a distinct shape per beat (not T-pose, not stuck-to-sides)

This is achievable with our PIL pipeline using the bezier helpers from Maya's construction prototype (`tube_polygon`, `bezier3`, `bezier4`).
