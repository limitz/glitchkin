**Project:** *Luma & the Glitchkin*
**Created by:** Alex Chen, Art Director

**Version:** 1.0

---

## 1. Show Concept

*Luma & the Glitchkin* is a comedy-adventure cartoon about **Luma**, a 12-year-old girl who discovers that the "dead pixels" on her grandmother's ancient CRT monitor are actually tiny creatures called **Glitchkin** — mischievous beings made of corrupted data who have leaked into the real world. Luma must round them up before they scramble reality itself, aided by her best friend **Cosmo** (a cautious, overthinking boy) and a grumpy reformed Glitchkin named **Byte** who rides on her shoulder.

The tone is **playful, energetic, and slightly surreal** — mixing the warmth of slice-of-life with the chaos of digital glitch aesthetics.

---

## 2. Overall Art Style Direction

### Style Family
**Flat-graphic contemporary cartoon** — in the lineage of shows like *Hilda*, *The Owl House*, and *Kipo and the Age of Wonderbeasts*, but with a distinct digital-glitch twist.

### Key Principles
- **Graphic simplicity with rich texture.** Shapes are clean and readable at a distance, but up close there are subtle grain textures, scan-line overlays, and pixel-dither patterns that give the world a tactile, slightly retro-digital feel.
- **Warm analog world vs. cool digital chaos.** The "real" world leans warm (ambers, terracottas, sage greens). Glitch elements pop in electric cyans, magentas, and acid greens.
- **Expressiveness over realism.** Anatomy is stylized for maximum personality. Squash-and-stretch is encouraged. Physics are suggestions, not laws.
- **Readability is king.** Every frame should read clearly as a silhouette. If you squint and can't tell what's happening, simplify.

## 3. Character Design Principles

### Shape Language

| Character Archetype | Primary Shapes | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Hero / Protagonist | **Circles + rounded rectangles** — approachable, trustworthy | Luma |
| Cautious / Cerebral | **Rectangles + ovals** — stable, grounded, slightly stiff | Cosmo |
| Chaotic / Trickster | **Triangles + jagged polygons** — unpredictable, sharp energy | Byte and Glitchkin |
| Authority / Mentor | **Large rounded squares** — solid, protective | Grandma Miri |
| Villain / Threat | **Inverted triangles + fragmented shapes** — unstable, menacing | The Corruption |

### Proportions
- **Children (Luma, Cosmo):** 3.5–4 heads tall. Large heads, small shoulders, expressive hands. Feet slightly oversized for grounding.
- **Adults:** 5.5–6 heads tall. Still stylized — no realistic proportions.
- **Glitchkin:** Wildly variable. Byte is roughly 6 inches tall (2 "heads"). Other Glitchkin range from marble-sized to car-sized. They do NOT follow biological rules — limbs can be asymmetric, features can float.

### Appeal Checklist
- [ ] Does the character read in silhouette?
- [ ] Can you tell their personality from their pose alone?
- [ ] Are the eyes expressive and large enough to carry emotion at distance?
- [ ] Is there a single memorable visual hook (Luma's pixel-patterned hoodie, Cosmo's too-big glasses, Byte's one cracked eye)?
- [ ] Does the design survive simplification for wide shots?

### Face and Expression
- Eyes are the primary expression vehicle. They are **large, rounded, with simple iris construction** (solid color + highlight dot). No detailed irises.
- Mouths are **simple curved lines** that can stretch and deform freely. Teeth shown only for emphasis.
- Eyebrows are **thick, graphic shapes** — not hair-like. They float above the head when needed for extreme expressions.
- Blush is a **simple oval or circle**, never gradient.

### Costume Notes
- Characters have **one iconic outfit** each. Variations are minimal (pajamas version, winter version) and keep the same color identity.
- Clothing folds are minimal — **2-3 key fold lines maximum**. Clothing is graphic, not draped.
- Patterns on clothing are flat and bold (Luma's pixel-grid hoodie, Cosmo's striped shirt).

### Shared Visual DNA — The Byte/Luma Cyan Connection

**Art Director Decision (Cycle 5) — Alex Chen**

The use of cyan across both Byte and Luma is **intentional**. Byte is made of the same glitch energy that powers Luma's hoodie pixel pattern. Electric Cyan (#00F0FF) is the visual DNA that connects them — Byte is, at a conceptual level, living inside the color of Luma's clothing.

**Official color split:**

| Character / Zone | Color | Hex | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Luma — hoodie pixel accents | Electric Cyan | #00F0FF | Pure, unmodified. The source color. |
| Luma — shoe laces | Electric Cyan | #00F0FF | One deliberate glitch-color intrusion into her warm wardrobe. |
| Byte — body FILL | Byte Teal | #00D4E8 | Slightly cooler and deeper than Electric Cyan. Creates figure-ground distinction. |
| Byte — body HIGHLIGHT | Electric Cyan | #00F0FF | Pure color resurfaces at the brightest, most expressive surfaces. |
| Byte — pixel-eye displays | Electric Cyan | #00F0FF | The shared identity is most visible here — Byte's eyes glow the same color as Luma's pixels. |

**Reading the distinction:**
- Byte's fill reads as slightly **deeper and cooler** than Luma's pure pixel accents.
- Luma's pixels read as **pure electric** — the original, unshifted signal.
- In close-up, both colors are visible and clearly related. In medium and wide shots, both characters carry the same visual identity cue — the connection is felt even when it cannot be analytically confirmed.

**Environment rule — cyan-dominant scenes:**
When the background environment is cyan-dominant (e.g., Byte emerging from a cyan monitor, Byte in a Glitch Layer scene with strong cyan ambient), apply a **2px Corrupted Amber (#FF8C00) outline** to Byte's silhouette to prevent figure-ground failure. This outline is the existing exception documented in the master palette under GL-07 (Corrupted Amber). Cross-reference: `/output/color/palettes/master_palette.md` GL-07.

This rule does NOT apply to Luma — her warm skin and orange hoodie provide natural separation from cyan environments.

### Mood Palettes

**Daytime Adventure:** Warm cream sky, sage/terracotta environment, soft gold highlights. Upbeat and safe.

**Nighttime / Suspense:** Dusty lavender sky, deep cocoa shadows, selective electric cyan glitch lighting. Moody but not scary.

**Full Glitch Chaos:** Glitch palette dominates. Real-world colors desaturate. High contrast. Acid green and hot magenta fight for attention. Use sparingly — this is the "volume 11" palette.

**Emotional / Quiet Moments:** Reduced palette. Lean into warm cream, soft gold, and dusty lavender. Minimal glitch colors. Let the character art carry the frame.

### Color Dos and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Use color temperature to guide the eye (warm foreground, cool background — or reverse it for tension).
- Let the glitch palette INTRUDE into the warm palette — it should feel like it's breaking the rules.
- Use limited palettes per scene (5-7 dominant colors max).
- Push saturation on Glitchkin; pull saturation on backgrounds so characters pop.

**DON'T:**
- Use pure black (#000000) anywhere. Darkest value is Deep Cocoa or Void Black.
- Use pure white (#FFFFFF) except for specular pops and screen flashes.
- Give every scene the full glitch treatment — it loses impact. Reserve it for climactic moments.
- Match saturation between characters and backgrounds — characters should always be more saturated.

### The Two-World Logic

Every color on this show belongs to one of two worlds. If a color does not clearly belong, you have the wrong color.

| World | Temperature | Feeling | Governing Colors |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Real World** | Warm | Safety, home, the analog, the known | Warm Cream, Soft Gold, Terracotta, Sage Green, Dusty Lavender |
| **Glitch Layer** | Cold / Neon | Danger, magic, the digital, the unknown | Electric Cyan, Hot Magenta, UV Purple, Acid Green, Void Black |

**The one rule:** Glitch colors intrude into the Real World. Real World colors do not belong in the Glitch Layer — when they appear there, they are corrupted objects, fragments of memory, things from the other side. That visual tension is the show.

| 2 | **Terracotta** `RW-04` | `#C75B39` | You need the Real World to push back. Architecture, warmth, grounding. Placed next to Electric Cyan it creates the maximum warm/cold tension the show runs on. |

### The Corrupted Amber Rule — When It Appears

**Corrupted Amber** (`GL-07`, `#FF8C00`) is the bridge color — Real World amber turned electric and *wrong*.

It appears in exactly four situations:

1. **Byte's silhouette outline** when Electric Cyan + Byte Teal together cover more than **35% of the visible background** around Byte. This is programmatic figure-ground protection — it prevents Byte from disappearing into cyan environments. 3px stroke at 1920×1080. Do NOT apply this outline in warm-dominant scenes (Key 01 interiors) — it would read as a corruption warning in a safe scene, which breaks the visual language.

2. **Real World objects mid-corruption** — a mailbox, bicycle, or sign that is being consumed by glitch energy shimmers amber at its edges. Amber = transformation in progress.

3. **Glitch Layer interface warning** — first-level alert pulse (amber) before escalating to Hot Magenta danger signal. Always in that sequence: Amber (caution) → Magenta (danger).

4. **Near-corruption glow** on a Real World object that is about to be transformed.

**NEVER use Corrupted Amber as:** a warm light color, a skin fill, a safe ambient tone, or a substitute for Soft Gold. It must always carry unease.

### Forbidden Colors — What to NEVER Use in Each World

**Never in the Real World:**
- `#39FF14` Acid Green as a large fill (it is a Glitch Layer organism color)
- `#7B2FBE` UV Purple as a shadow tone (it reads as Glitch Layer intrusion even when none is scripted)
- `#FF2D6B` Hot Magenta in safe scenes (it is an alarm color; casual use destroys its function)
- `#0A0A14` Void Black as a line color in warm scenes (use `#3B2820` Deep Cocoa instead)

**Never in the Glitch Layer:**
- `#C75B39` Terracotta as an architectural fill (it reads as "home" and undercuts the alien quality)
- `#3B2820` Deep Cocoa as line work (invisible against `#0A0A14` backgrounds — Forbidden #6)
- `#E8C95A` Soft Gold as a large ambient fill (it reads as "safe" — reserve Real World ambers for memory fragments and corrupted objects only)

**Never anywhere:**
- `#000000` pure black or `#FFFFFF` pure white (use `#0A0A14` / `#FAF0DC` or `#F0F0F0` instead)
- `#8C5A38` Skin Shadow as a costume or object fill (it reads as flesh — Forbidden #7)
- `#39FF14` Acid Green in storm/danger effects (it is semantically "healthy glitch" — using it in danger contexts contradicts its own meaning — Forbidden #8)
- `#FAF0DC` Warm Cream or `#E8C95A` Soft Gold as 20%+ ambient fill in interior or evening scenes — they fight the mood (Forbidden #10)

### Quick Color Decision Flowchart

```
Is this the Real World? → Warm palette. Ambers, terracottas, sage, lavender shadows.
Is this the Glitch Layer? → Cyan, magenta, UV purple, void black. Organic warm colors only as fragments.
Is Byte near a cyan background? → Add Corrupted Amber outline if cyan > 35% of background.
Is a Real World object being corrupted? → Shift edges toward Corrupted Amber before full glitch takeover.
Is this a shadow in a night/Glitch scene? → Use #7A5A7A (cool shadow) — never warm brown.
Does this color feel "neutral grey"? → Wrong. Every color must be either warm or cold. Interrogate it.
```

### Style
- **Painterly-flat hybrid.** Backgrounds use flat color shapes with soft edges and subtle texture overlays (grain, gentle noise). Think watercolor zones filled with flat color.
- No hard outlines on backgrounds — outlines are reserved for characters and foreground objects to maintain separation.
- Backgrounds should feel like **illustrated picture-book spreads** — inviting, detailed enough to be interesting, simple enough to not compete with character action.

### Level of Detail

| Shot Type | Detail Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Wide / Establishing | High | Full environment painting. Textures, atmospheric perspective, small details. This is where BG artists shine. |
| Medium | Medium | Key landmarks readable. Simplify distant elements. Focus detail around character action zone. |
| Close-up | Low | Soft, blurred, or heavily simplified. Often a single color gradient or texture. Characters own these frames. |

### Perspective Approach
- **Gentle perspective, slightly tilted.** Not rigid vanishing-point perspective — lines converge loosely. The world should feel handmade, not architectural.
- Dutch angles are allowed for tension but should be subtle (5-15 degrees). Save extreme angles for storyboards.
- **No fisheye or extreme wide-angle** unless it's a specific glitch-distortion effect.

### Key Environments

1. **Luma's House** — A cozy, cluttered home full of Grandma Miri's vintage tech collection. Warm ambers and sage greens. CRT monitors, old keyboards, tangled cables as decorative motifs. It should feel like a warm hug that's also a computer graveyard.

2. **Millbrook Town** — A small, quirky town with slightly wonky architecture (buildings lean, roofs are uneven). Terracotta and cream palette. Visible power lines and antennas everywhere — the town is unknowingly an antenna for glitch energy.

3. **The Glitch Layer** — A digital dimension that overlaps with reality. Void black base with floating geometric platforms, pixel-art flora, scrolling code waterfalls, and impossible geometry. This is where the glitch palette goes all-out.

4. **School** — Luma's middle school. Institutional but not grim — dusty lavender and sage green. Cluttered bulletin boards, scuffed floors, personality in the imperfections.

## 6. Line Work Standards

### Character Line Work
- **Consistent weighted line.** Base stroke weight is **medium-thick** with slight taper at endpoints.
- Line color is **Deep Cocoa (#3B2820)** by default — NOT black. For Glitchkin, line color shifts to **Void Black (#0A0A14)** or a dark version of their body color.
- **No hatching or cross-hatching.** Shadow is communicated through color, not line.
- Lines may **break** at points where forms turn away from light — this keeps things airy.
- Internal detail lines are thinner than silhouette lines (roughly 60% weight).

### Background Line Work
- Backgrounds have **NO outlines** in general. Shapes are defined by color edges.
- Exception: foreground objects that characters interact with (doors, chairs, items) get **thin outlines** to tie them to the character layer.

### Glitch Line Work
- Glitchkin and glitch effects use **variable, jittery lines** — as if the stroke is slightly corrupted. This can be achieved with a slight zigzag or displacement.
- Glitch lines may **double or offset** (like a misregistered print) for emphasis.

## 7. Visual Dos and Don'ts — General

### DO
- **Exaggerate poses.** If a character is surprised, their whole body reacts. Push it further than feels natural.
- **Use asymmetry.** Perfectly symmetrical poses feel dead. Tilt heads, shift weight, bend wrists.
- **Layer depth.** Every composition should have a clear foreground, midground, and background layer.
- **Use negative space.** Let frames breathe. Not every inch needs detail.
- **Add "pixel confetti"** (tiny floating square particles) near any glitch activity — it's our visual signature.
- **Keep tangents clean.** No character outlines merging with background edges.
- **Reference the style guide** before starting any new asset. Consistency is everything.

### DON'T
- **Don't use gradients on characters.** Flat fills with cel-shadow only (one shadow tone, one highlight tone max).
- **Don't draw fingers individually** unless the shot demands it. Mitten-hands with a thumb are default. Close-ups can have 4 fingers.
- **Don't center every composition.** Use rule-of-thirds framing as default.
- **Don't mix real-world and glitch palettes 50/50.** One should dominate; the other intrudes.
- **Don't add texture to character fills.** Texture belongs on backgrounds and glitch effects only.
- **Don't use drop shadows or outer glows** — they break the flat-graphic look. Shadows are cast shapes on the ground, painted in the shadow color.

## 9. Animation Style Notes

*Added Cycle 11 — Alex Chen, Art Director. Three cycles overdue per Fiona O'Sullivan.*

### Core Movement Philosophy

This show uses **snappy, readable animation** rooted in classic cartoon timing, distorted by deliberate digital glitch. Every motion should feel intentional — either warmly organic (real world) or abruptly precise (Glitch Layer). There is no middle ground.

### Character Movement Principles

**Luma**
- **Forward lean is permanent in active scenes.** At rest, Luma stands neutral. The moment she is curious, excited, or in motion, her whole body pitches forward 10–16°. This is her visual tell for engagement.
- **Runs with a forward lean and slightly flailing arms** — not a smooth athletic run. She pumps her arms too enthusiastically. It reads as earnest, not clumsy.
- **Anticipation is always exaggerated** — a full wind-up before a run, a full crouch before a jump. No subtle anticipations.
- **Emotional reach:** when Luma reaches toward something, her whole torso follows her arm. She doesn't just extend a hand — she commits her body.

**Byte**
- **Floats and bobs continuously.** Sinusoidal float cycle: 24 frames per cycle, ~8px vertical travel, 4-frame overshoot at apex and nadir. This idle bob is always present — Byte is never perfectly still. It communicates that he is not of this world.
- **Glitch-snap transitions:** when Byte changes position rapidly, he disappears from one position and snaps to the next on a single frame, with a 2-frame motion blur smear as the only transition. This is intentional stylization, not camera blur.
- **Expressions change instantly** — no slow blinks or gradual emotion shifts. Byte's pixel eyes can flip from one expression to another between frames. His body language carries the warmup.
- **Hovering height:** Byte's baseline float is 12–16px above any surface he is "resting" over. He never fully lands on anything.

**Cosmo**
- **Measured, deliberate movement.** Cosmo does not rush. His timing is 20–30% slower than Luma's for equivalent actions.
- **Startle reaction is massive** — the one exception. Arms fling wide, glasses shoot up. Then he immediately returns to composure. The contrast is the joke.
- **Never runs naturally** — his run cycle is too upright, arms too stiff.

**Grandma Miri**
- **Economical movement.** Small, precise motions. Her hands are always busy (soldering, adjusting, working).
- **Authority through stillness** — when she stops moving, everyone notices.

### Glitch Effects Timing Rules

- **Glitch effects are INSTANT, not gradual.** A glitch appears on frame 1 at full intensity. If it fades, it fades OUT in 2–4 frames maximum. No slow glitch build-ups.
- **Pixel confetti spawns in burst clusters**, not continuous streams. A burst = 8–20 particles on the same frame, then drifting 4–8 frames before disappearing.
- **Corruption effects** use right-angle motion only — horizontal or vertical snaps, never diagonal arcs.
- **Byte's emergence from screens:** emergence is sudden (1–2 frames), not a gradual poke-through.

### Scene-Level Timing Reference

| Action | Timing Standard |
|--------|----------------|
| Luma standard run cycle | 8 frames (snappy, 3fps foot contact) |
| Byte float bob cycle | 24 frames |
| Byte snap-teleport | 1 frame appear, 2-frame blur smear |
| Cosmo walk cycle | 14 frames (deliberate) |
| Anticipation wind-up | 6–10 frames |
| Glitch burst duration | 4–8 frames |
| Expression snap (Byte) | 1 frame |
| Expression ease (Luma, Cosmo) | 3–4 frames |

### World-Layer Motion Contrast

The **real world** uses organic ease-in / ease-out on all motion curves. Arcs are rounded. Physics are friendly and slightly exaggerated.

The **Glitch Layer** uses **linear motion** — no easing. Straight-line moves from A to B at constant velocity. Immediate starts, immediate stops. When anything in the Glitch Layer appears to follow organic physics, something has gone wrong (and that wrongness is the point of the scene).

## 10. Glitchkin Construction Rules

*Added Cycle 11 — Alex Chen, Art Director.*

### What Is a Glitchkin?

Glitchkin are beings of corrupted data that have leaked through old CRT monitors into the real world. They are not biological — they are **emergent digital entities** that have acquired just enough physical presence to interact with the world while retaining the visual logic of corrupted file systems.

Byte is a reformed Glitchkin — his design is canonical and represents the "most coherent" end of the Glitchkin spectrum. Other Glitchkin are wilder, less stable, and more clearly wrong.

### Core Construction Rules

1. **Pixel body — no smooth curves.** Glitchkin bodies are made of pixel-art blocks. Every edge should have at least one 90° corner. Even the roundest Glitchkin should feel like it is approximating a circle from square pixels, not actually being round.

2. **No fixed shape.** A Glitchkin's silhouette can change from scene to scene — they are not dimensionally stable. However, their *essential features* (eye configuration, dominant color, key corruption mark) must remain consistent. The silhouette is variable; the identity is not.

3. **Corruption marks are mandatory.** Every Glitchkin must have at least one visible corruption mark — a scar, a cracked feature, a pixel dropout zone, a misregistered color bleed, or a missing limb replaced with a geometric placeholder.

4. **Corruption marks must be angular and sharp.** Even on a soft-looking Glitchkin, corruption marks use sharp angles. The contrast between soft pixel body and sharp corruption detail is the visual signature of the species.

5. **Always smaller than Byte, unless plot-specified.** Byte is 6 inches tall. Standard Glitchkin range from marble-sized to roughly Byte's size. Any Glitchkin significantly larger than Byte is plot-notable and must be scripted, not casual design.

6. **Limbs are optional and asymmetric.** When limbs exist, they are clearly rectangular pixel blocks — no organic limb taper, no elbows unless square.

7. **Eyes are the identity anchor.** No matter how chaotic the rest of the design, the eyes must be readable. Eyes define the Glitchkin's emotional state.

8. **Color logic:** Each Glitchkin has a dominant Glitch Palette color (GL-01 through GL-05) plus a secondary corruption color at scar/crack sites only. Byte's combination (GL-01b Teal body + GL-03 Hot Magenta corruption) must not be duplicated by new Glitchkin designs.

### Glitchkin Size Categories

| Category | Size Range | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Micro | Marble to thumb | Background swarm Glitchkin. No individual designs needed. |
| Standard | 2–6 inches | Named Glitchkin with recurring roles. Full design required. |
| Large | 6 inches to 1 foot | Plot-notable only. Approved by Art Director. |
| Mega | Larger than 1 foot | Single-episode threat. The Corruption only. |

### The Corruption — Special Case

The Corruption is not a Glitchkin. It is the systemic failure state that Glitchkin emerge from. It manifests as an encroaching visual failure: **Stage 1** (pixel dropout patches), **Stage 2** (color inversion zones), **Stage 3** (Byte Cyan vs Corruption Magenta direct confrontation, GL-01b vs GL-03). The Corruption is an environmental condition, not a character with a face.

### Quick Construction Checklist

- [ ] Does the body have at least one visible 90° corner?
- [ ] Does the design have a dominant Glitch Palette color?
- [ ] Is there at least one corruption mark (angular, sharp)?
- [ ] Are the eyes readable at small scale?
- [ ] Is it smaller than Byte (unless scripted otherwise)?
- [ ] Does it look like corrupted data rather than a biological creature?
- [ ] Is the dominant color different from Byte's GL-01b Teal + GL-03 Magenta combination?

## 11. Prop Design Guidelines

*Added Cycle 11 — Alex Chen, Art Director.*

### The Aesthetic Logic of Props

Props in *Luma & the Glitchkin* exist in two distinct design registers depending on which layer of reality they inhabit. A prop's design immediately tells the viewer **where they are** and **what rules apply**.

### Real World Props — Warm Material Logic

Real-world props follow the logic of **old, loved, slightly neglected objects**. Grandma Miri's house is full of vintage technology kept functional through personal care. Nothing is sleek or modern. Everything has a readable history.

**Design principles:**

1. **Warm materials dominate.** Wood, fabric, aged plastic, worn metal. Real-world props lean amber, terracotta, sage, and ochre.

2. **Retro-computing aesthetic is the dominant prop language.** CRT monitors, mechanical keyboards, tangled cables, cassette tapes, floppy disks, oscilloscopes, soldering equipment. These are functional objects, not ironic props.

3. **Evidence of use is mandatory for hero props.** A mug has a ring stain. A keyboard has worn-off letters. A monitor has a crack in the bezel. Props that look factory-new feel wrong. Wear signals a real world with history.

4. **Cables are always present.** Tangled cables are the decorative motif of this show's real world. They appear in foreground, midground, and background. Cable colors follow master_palette.md PROP-04 through PROP-07. Cables activate dead compositional zones.

5. **Scale is relatable but slightly exaggerated.** Keyboards are wide, monitors are chunky, mugs are generous. This is a cartoon world.

6. **No sharp geometric precision.** Real-world props have slightly imperfect symmetry. Screen bezels are not perfectly rectangular. The handmade quality in line work extends to prop geometry.

### Glitch Layer Props — Pure Geometry Logic

**Design principles:**

1. **Pure geometry only.** Glitch Layer props are made of perfect rectangles, exact circles, precise triangles. No organic imperfection. No wear. They were generated by an algorithm.

2. **Color is restricted to the Glitch Palette.** No warm colors unless they represent real-world contamination (a plot point).

3. **Floating, not resting.** Glitch Layer props hover, orbit, or are suspended in void space. Gravity is optional.

4. **Scanlines and pixel-dither are always visible** on every solid surface in the Glitch Layer.

5. **Props can be incomplete.** Structural absence, not structural damage. There is no "broken" — there is only "incomplete."

6. **Scale breaks without warning.** Props do not follow consistent scale rules. This disorientation is intentional.

### Transition Objects — Bridging Both Worlds

Some props exist in both worlds simultaneously — real-world objects partially corrupted by Glitch Layer contact.

**Design rule:** warm colors and real-world materials on intact surfaces; pure Glitch Palette geometry on corrupted surfaces. The boundary between them must be a clean edge, not a gradient. The clean edge is the visual argument that two different laws of physics are fighting over the same object.

Grandma Miri's CRT monitor is the canonical transition object: warm wood case and aging plastic (real world), electric cyan screen interior and pixel confetti escaping from the vents (Glitch Layer bleeding through).
