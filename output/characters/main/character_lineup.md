## 1. THE ENSEMBLE AT A GLANCE

Four characters. Four different answers to the question "what do you do when reality stops following the rules?"

**Luma:** Jump in. Figure it out on the way down.
**Cosmo:** Document the danger. Follow anyway because the alternative is unthinkable.
**Byte:** Refuse to admit you care. Help anyway. Deny everything.
**Grandma Miri:** Make tea. Watch carefully. Know when to step in and when to let it happen.

The show lives in the space between these four perspectives. The character designs must communicate this before anyone speaks. A reader looking at the lineup for the first time should be able to assign personality to each character based on shape, proportion, and color alone.

They succeed at this. Here is why.

## 2. RELATIVE SIZES

### Standard Lineup Reference (left to right: Miri, Cosmo, Luma, Byte)

The convention for the full cast lineup places Miri on the far left (as the eldest and the anchor), Cosmo beside her, Luma in center-right position, and Byte either on Luma's shoulder or standing at her feet. In trio shots (episodes not featuring Miri), the Cosmo-left, Luma-center convention remains.

**Heights in absolute terms (estimated real-world):**
| Character | Approximate Real Height | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Cosmo | 5'1" (approx.) | Slightly above average for a 12-year-old boy — tall/lanky |
| Luma | 4'9" (approx.) | Average-to-below for a 12-year-old girl — compact and grounded |
| Grandma Miri | 5'0" (approx.) | Shorter than Cosmo, slightly taller than Luma in real terms — but reads as a presence |
| Byte | 6 inches | Not a biological height. A size. |

**Heights in "Luma head" units:**
| Character | Height in Luma Heads | Visual Height (including hair/updo) |
|---|---|---|
| Cosmo | ~4.3 Luma heads | 4.3 (hair adds almost nothing) |
| Luma | ~3.5 Luma heads | ~3.9 (hair adds ~0.4 heads visually) |
| Grandma Miri | ~3.2 Luma heads | ~3.45 (updo adds ~0.25 heads) |
| Byte (on ground) | ~0.5 Luma heads | ~0.55 including spike |

### The Lineup Reading
**Trio (Cosmo, Luma, Byte):**
- Cosmo is visibly taller than Luma — but only by one head. It is noticeable but not dramatic.
- Luma reads WIDER and more grounded than Cosmo despite being shorter. Her hoodie mass and hair volume create a horizontal footprint.
- Cosmo reads taller and narrower — a vertical accent to her horizontal presence.
- Byte on the ground beside Luma reads as dramatically small — reaching her upper thigh.
- Byte on Luma's shoulder reads as her extension — a part of her silhouette.

**Full cast (adding Miri):**
- Miri is the shortest character with the widest torso-to-height ratio. She reads as the most grounded form in the lineup — planted where the others lean.
- Her silver hair updo is distinctive against the trio's hair (Luma's dark cloud, Cosmo's dark flat cap) — she is immediately readable as a different generation.
- She is slightly shorter than Luma with visual hair height, but her wider body makes her read as heavier in mass than Luma despite the height similarity.
- The terracotta cardigan creates a warm family echo with Luma's orange hoodie — in the lineup they are the visual warmth anchors on either end (if Miri is on the left and Luma center-right).

**Recommendation for standard model sheet lineup:** Show the trio together (existing convention) and add a second "full cast" lineup with Miri included. Byte on Luma's shoulder in both. In the full cast lineup, Miri flanks the trio on the left, creating a warm bookend with Luma's warm orange in the center.

## 3. SHAPE LANGUAGE CONTRAST

The cast was built around four distinct geometric families. In every composition they share, these shape families should be visible and in dialogue with each other.

### Shape Summary
| Character | Primary Shapes | Secondary Shapes |
|---|---|---|
| Luma | Circles, rounded rectangles | Soft, organic outlines throughout |
| Cosmo | Tall rectangles, ovals (via glasses) | Straight lines, contained curves |
| Byte | Triangles, chamfered polygons | Jagged edges, angular protrusions |
| Grandma Miri | Circles (settled/compressed), weathered rectangles | Soft curves, generous corners, textured surfaces |

### The Shape Hierarchy in a Shared Frame

**In the trio (Luma, Cosmo, Byte):**
1. **Byte's angles** create the highest visual tension — jagged shapes draw the eye immediately. He reads first because his form is the most unusual.
2. **Luma's circles** are the most universally appealing and resolve the tension — the eye finds her form restful after Byte.
3. **Cosmo's rectangles** read as the stable background element — his form grounds the composition.

**In the full cast (adding Miri):**
4. **Miri's settled circles and weathered rectangles** function as the compositional anchor of the entire group. She is the heaviest visual mass in terms of density — widest torso, shortest height, most grounded. She belongs at the base of the composition in height, and at the warm end of the color temperature range.

The addition of Miri to the composition changes how the trio reads: with her present, the trio's energy is contained by her stillness. Cosmo reads less like the "stable background element" and more like the "organized" member of the group when compared to Miri's deeper groundedness.

**Compositional use of the full cast:**
- Byte can appear in any position because his read is immediate and strong at any scale
- Luma should typically be the visual center because the circle is the most stable compositional anchor
- Cosmo works well at frame edges or as the vertical element in a horizontal composition
- Miri grounds whichever edge she occupies — she is a compositional weight, not an accent. Place her where you want the composition to feel anchored.

### Silhouette Contrast Test
Reading all four characters as solid black shapes:

**The full cast silhouette should be immediately parseable as four distinct entities even at thumbnail size:**
- The large hair cloud (Luma) — no other character has this
- The tall thin rectangle with flat head (Cosmo) — no other character has this
- The small angular cube with spike (Byte) — no other character has this
- The compact wide-torso with silver updo and long cardigan (Miri) — no other character has this

If a silhouette reads as ambiguous, the design has failed. These four silhouettes do not fail. They are maximally distinct.

**Miri-specific silhouette challenges:**
- Miri's head shape (compressed circle) is in the same family as Luma's (circle). At thumbnail, they could potentially be confused. The distinguishing factors: Miri's hair (updo mass, small and high) vs. Luma's hair (wide cloud, dominant) — these read completely differently. Miri's wider body and shorter legs also distinguish her at thumbnail.
- When Miri and Luma are in the same frame, the silver updo reads immediately as "different character" from Luma's dark hair cloud. The contrast between silver and near-black creates a strong visual separation.

**The specific silhouette moments to test:**
- Four-shot, waist up: all four characters must read as distinct. Luma's hair must separate from both Cosmo's flat head and Miri's updo. At this scale, the hair shapes are the primary separators.
- Three-shot (trio), waist up: Luma's hair must still read as a separate mass from Cosmo's flat head beside her. This test continues to pass easily.
- Over-the-shoulder shot behind Luma with Cosmo in mid-ground: Byte on Luma's shoulder adds a distinctive angular bump to an otherwise organic silhouette.
- Long shot (all four at 15% of frame height): must read as: one large-head round figure, one tall thin figure, one wide-short-silver-haired figure, one tiny angular figure. They do.
- Long shot (Miri in kitchen/den, trio leaving): Miri in her home context — her wide cardigan silhouette and updo read immediately as "adult in a domestic space," distinct from the trio's running silhouettes.

## 4. COLOR RELATIONSHIPS

### The Core Color Cast
| Character | Primary Color | Secondary Color | Accent/Hook Color |
|---|---|---|---|
| Luma | Warm Orange #E8722A | Dark Espresso Hair | Cyan #00F0FF (hoodie pixels, shoelaces) |
| Cosmo | Cerulean Blue #5B8DB8 | Sage Green #7A9E7E | Warm Espresso #5C3A20 (glasses, shoes, notebook) |
| Byte | Electric Cyan #00F0FF | Hot Magenta #FF2D6B | Acid Green #39FF14 / UV Purple #7B2FBE |
| Grandma Miri | Terracotta Rust #B85C38 | Silver White hair #D8D0C8 | Sage Green #5A7A5A (slippers) / Deep Warm Brown skin #8C5430 |

### Color Temperature Distribution
The full cast creates a deliberate temperature structure:
- **Luma: WARM** — orange, dark warm brown hair, warm caramel skin
- **Miri: WARM-SETTLED** — terracotta rust (the same warm family as Luma, deeper and older), deep brown skin, silver-warm hair
- **Cosmo: NEUTRAL-COOL** — blue and sage green, olive skin, warm earth accessories
- **Byte: COOL/ELECTRIC** — pure glitch palette, no warm tones

**The Warm Family (Luma + Miri):**
The orange family connecting Luma and Miri is the show's clearest visual statement of their relationship. Luma's hoodie (#E8722A) and Miri's cardigan (#B85C38) are the same family of warm orange-reds, at different values and saturations. When they are in frame together, they create a "warm center" that anchors the scene emotionally. Even in scenes where they are not on screen simultaneously, the palette connection functions as a memory: "these two are the same color, which means they are the same kind of person."

This temperature distribution means:
1. In a warm-lit scene (daytime, Luma's house/Miri's den), Luma and Miri both integrate with the environment while Cosmo and Byte stand out against it — with Byte popping most dramatically. Scenes in Miri's den are the warmest visual context in the show.
2. In a digital/glitch scene (the Glitch Layer), Byte integrates while Luma, Cosmo, and Miri all stand out — with Luma's orange and Miri's rust (if present) creating a warm intrusion into the cool digital world.
3. This is a structural storytelling tool: the characters' color identities literally tell us which world they "belong" to. Luma and Miri belong to the warm analog world (same palette family). Byte belongs to the cool digital world. Cosmo belongs to neither. This positions Cosmo as the mediator — not anchored to either world's color temperature.

### The Cyan Connection
There is a deliberate cyan thread connecting Luma and Byte:
- Byte is cyan
- Luma's hoodie pixel pattern prominently features cyan (#00F0FF)
- Luma's shoelaces are cyan
This is the color of their relationship — the digital world has already reached into Luma's life and left its mark in her wardrobe. She and Byte are connected before they even meet. Cosmo has no cyan on him. This is also correct: his relationship to the digital world is purely intellectual, not instinctual.

### Color Do's in Trio Compositions
- **DO** place Luma and Byte together in frame when the warm/cool contrast needs to be maximum
- **DO** use Cosmo's sage green to visually bridge Luma (warm) and Byte (cool) when a calming/mediating narrative moment occurs
- **DO** allow Byte's colors to feel intrusive against a warm background — they should look like they don't belong in the analog world, because they don't
- **DO** keep character saturation higher than backgrounds at all times — this is non-negotiable for the trio especially

### Color Don'ts in Trio Compositions
- **DON'T** dress them in similar hues. Their color identities are three distinct zones. Costumes changes (pajamas, seasonal variants) must maintain the overall temperature identity of each character.
- **DON'T** let Byte's cyan read as "sky blue" — it must always be electric, neon, digital. If he's reading as pastel in a scene, the background values need adjustment.
- **DON'T** let Cosmo's blue drift toward cyan — it needs to stay in the muted, intellectual blue zone, distinct from Byte's electric glitch cyan.

## 5. PERSONALITY CONTRAST — DESIGN AS CHARACTER

### The Four Modes of Facing a Problem

**Luma faces a problem by treating it as an opportunity.**
- Design expression: her body leans FORWARD. Her center of gravity is always ahead of her feet. Even standing still, she seems about to move. Her hair lifts with excitement. Her eyes go wide. She takes up space joyfully.

**Cosmo faces a problem by processing it completely before acting.**
- Design expression: his body is UPRIGHT. Vertical. He contains his energy. His eyes move; his body stills. He processes before committing. He takes up exactly as much space as he needs, precisely.

**Byte faces a problem by being rude about it and then solving it before anyone noticed he was going to.**
- Design expression: his body COMPRESSES, then EXTENDS. He closes off (arms in, body squared away), then opens sharply (arms out, floating forward, pixel display active). His problem-solving is an explosion preceded by visible reluctance.

**Miri faces a problem by having seen it before, and waiting for the right moment.**
- Design expression: her body is STILL. She is the still center around which others orbit. She does not lean, she does not compress. She watches. Her eyes do the moving — the information is in her eyes before it reaches her face. When she acts, it is decisive and final and looks effortless because she has been preparing since before you noticed the problem.

### Interaction Dynamics (Design Terms)

**In a calm three-shot (trio):**
Luma leans slightly toward whoever she's talking to. Cosmo is upright with one hand raised (note forthcoming). Byte faces partially away but the cracked eye is monitoring.

**In a calm scene with Miri present:**
Miri is centered and still. The trio orients toward her the way objects orient toward a gravitational center — not consciously, but consistently. Luma leans toward her. Cosmo is more relaxed in her presence (the rectangle rounds slightly). Byte floats closer than usual. She is the calmest person in any room she's in, and design composition should reflect this.

**In a high-stakes three-shot (trio):**
Luma is mid-motion — her line of action is a C-curve, arms extended, full commitment. Cosmo is behind and slightly to the side, his body finally in forward lean, notebook up or set aside, fully present. Byte is pointing ahead of all three of them — he saw the destination before either human did.

**In a den scene with Miri:**
Miri is seated or standing at an activity — always doing something, never idle. The trio arrives in her space, and the space has a calming effect on all of them. Luma's posture softens slightly. Cosmo's glasses are perhaps fractionally less crooked in this context. Byte is closer to Luma than usual. Miri looks up from what she's doing and asks a single question that instantly identifies the core of the problem they've been dancing around for the last ten minutes.

**In a quiet four-shot (rare, emotionally important):**
Luma is still, which is rare — her body has soft weight to it, sitting or leaning. Cosmo is beside her, body relaxed for once. Byte is between them, floating gap near-zero. Miri is the context for all of this — she is the seat, the room, the warmth. The trio at rest looks like home because she is home.

## 6. THE CAST AS A DESIGN SYSTEM

### Visual Rhymes Between Characters
Despite their differences, all four characters share construction DNA that makes them feel like they belong to the same show:

| Design Element | Luma | Cosmo | Byte | Grandma Miri |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Eye structure | Rounded rectangles, large | Circles (round glasses echo oval) | Rectangle (normal), display (cracked) | Rounded almond, medium |
| Eye highlight | One dot, upper-left | One dot, upper-right | One dot (normal eye), none (cracked) | One dot, upper-left |
| Silhouette complexity | High (hair) | Medium (glasses protrude) | High (angular forms, spike) | Medium (updo, wide torso) |
| Line color | Deep Cocoa #3B2820 | Deep Cocoa #3B2820 | Void Black #0A0A14 | Warm Dark Brown #4A2810 |
| Face imperfections | Hair never perfect | Glasses never level | Right side always damaged | Crow's feet, escaped hair strands |
| "Tell" item | Hoodie pixel pattern | Glasses crookedness | Cracked eye display | The mug (always present) |

**Miri and Luma's shared design DNA:** Eye highlight at upper-left (same). Iris in the amber family (same warmth, different depth). Escaping hair strands (same tendency, different scale). Warm garment in the orange-rust family (same color world, different life stage). This cross-character visual rhyming is the design's argument for their relationship before any dialogue.

### The "Tell" System
Each character has a single, readable "tell" item that animators can use for quick emotional shorthand:
- **Luma's hair** reacts to her emotions (lifts, droops, static)
- **Cosmo's glasses** slip, tilt, and crook with increasing agitation
- **Byte's cracked eye** displays the pixel symbol currently accurate to his interior state
- **Miri's mug** — its position, presence, or absence signals her engagement. Mug in hand = she is in mode (active, present, making something). Mug set down and pushed aside = she is fully focused, the warmth is being set aside for a moment. Mug absent = she is not in her element, or the scene is serious enough that even she needed both hands.

These four tells operate differently:
- Luma's tell is physical, involuntary, whole-body
- Cosmo's tell is mechanical, environmental (gravity and motion affect the glasses), slightly outside his control
- Byte's tell is technological, internal, but readable — the most honest display of any of them
- Miri's tell is domestic and habitual — it is a prop she has carried for decades. Its presence is ordinary; its absence is noticed.

Together they form a system. The audience learns to read these tells and will use them to track emotional subtext even when the dialogue says otherwise. This is exactly the right design investment for a comedy-adventure show that depends on character-read humor.

### Clothing Design as Character System
The three clothing identities create a visual shorthand for each character's relationship to control and freedom:

| Character | Clothing Type | Control Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Luma | Hoodie (oversized, broken-in, glitch-patterned) | Low — worn comfort, not precision | She dresses for movement, not appearance |
| Cosmo | Tucked shirt + pressed pants | High — deliberate, coordinated | He dresses as if the universe is watching |
| Byte | No clothes — his form IS his appearance | Total control (or total loss of it) | He cannot dress differently. He IS what he is. |

The contrast between Luma's chosen comfort and Cosmo's chosen precision is the central tension of their friendship. Byte, who has no choice about his appearance, is paradoxically the most "authentic" of the three.

## 7. THE ENSEMBLE FEELING

### What They Are Individually
- Luma alone: a protagonist with enormous energy and questionable judgment who makes you want to follow her anyway
- Cosmo alone: a supporting character who threatens to become the protagonist in the audience's estimation
- Byte alone: a mascot character who refuses to be a mascot
- Miri alone: a supporting character who makes every scene she's in feel larger and warmer, without seeming to try

### What They Are Together
As an ensemble, these four represent a complete approach to being alive:
- **Luma = Action.** The will to do.
- **Cosmo = Understanding.** The will to know.
- **Byte = Wisdom.** The will to survive.
- **Miri = Context.** The will to stay.

The show's premise — rounding up runaway Glitchkin before they scramble reality — maps perfectly onto this ensemble structure. Action (Luma) can locate and confront the problem. Understanding (Cosmo) can analyze and contextualize it. Wisdom (Byte) can navigate its digital nature and history. Context (Miri) can remember what it was like before the problem existed, and why it matters that it's solved.

None of them can solve the problem alone. Each one's weakness is exactly compensated by another's strength:
- Luma acts without understanding → Cosmo provides understanding
- Cosmo analyzes without acting → Luma provides action
- Byte knows but won't trust → Luma and Cosmo's combined sincerity wear him down
- The trio risks losing perspective → Miri provides the view from further back

The designs encode this interdependency:
- Luma's color reaches toward Byte (the cyan in her hoodie)
- Cosmo's color bridges them (blue to cyan on one side, sage green to Luma's warmth on the other)
- Byte's size necessitates Luma's shoulder (he needs her, literally)
- Miri's color (terracotta rust) echoes Luma — her design holds the origin story of the warm world the trio is trying to protect

### Visual Harmony Assessment
Do these four work as an ensemble? Yes. The evidence:

**Contrast:** High. Shape, color, proportion, and silhouette — all four are distinctly different while remaining in the same visual universe.

**Cohesion:** Strong. The show's visual style (flat color, warm-to-cool line work, large eyes, minimal clothing folds) is consistent across all four. They read as characters from the same show. The one designed variance — Miri's Warm Dark Brown line work vs. the trio's Deep Cocoa/Void Black — is subtle enough to read as the same system while providing a visual softness that communicates her age and warmth.

**Appeal distribution:** Complete. Luma appeals to viewers who want forward momentum and warmth. Cosmo appeals to viewers who identify with the "smart but overlooked" archetype. Byte appeals to viewers who are secretly much older and more tired than they admit. Miri appeals to everyone who misses someone who knew them completely. Together, the full cast covers the room.

**The ensemble test:** Can you tell the relationship between these four characters from a single image before anyone speaks?

Yes. The way Byte sits on Luma's shoulder says "familiar, chosen, slightly reluctant." The way Cosmo stands beside Luma says "beside, not behind — a partner, not a follower, even though he's taller." The way Miri is still at the center says "this is her world, and she has made room in it for all of you." The way Luma's colors echo Miri's says "she came from here."

This is the ensemble. This is "Luma & the Glitchkin."

## 8. DESIGN CONSISTENCY CHECKLIST — ALL CHARACTERS

Before submitting any multi-character composition, verify:

**Trio checklist (Luma, Cosmo, Byte):**
- [ ] All three characters have the correct line weight (Deep Cocoa for Luma + Cosmo, Void Black for Byte)
- [ ] Character saturation exceeds background saturation (always)
- [ ] Luma's hair is asymmetric and appropriately voluminous — exactly **5 spiral-curl indicators**
- [ ] Cosmo's glasses are crooked at **7 degrees** and magnifying his eyes; frames are thick (0.06x head width)
- [ ] Cosmo's notebook is tucked under his left arm in any shot wider than medium-close
- [ ] Byte's two eyes are clearly different (normal vs. cracked display)
- [ ] Byte is floating (small gap beneath lower limbs, pixel confetti present)
- [ ] No gradients on any character fills (flat color only)
- [ ] Mitten-hands default on all characters in wide/medium shots
- [ ] All three characters pass the silhouette test individually and as a group
- [ ] The warm/cool color temperature distribution is correct (Luma warm, Cosmo neutral, Byte cool)
- [ ] The cyan thread is maintained (present in Luma's accent colors and Byte's body)

**Full cast checklist (adding Miri):**
- [ ] Miri's line weight is Warm Dark Brown (#4A2810) — softer than Deep Cocoa, not Void Black
- [ ] Miri's silver-white updo reads clearly against her deep brown skin (high contrast)
- [ ] Miri's cardigan is terracotta rust, NOT the same orange as Luma's hoodie — warmer, darker, settled
- [ ] Miri has the permanent cheek blush present (unless in serious-concern expression — then removed)
- [ ] Miri's crow's feet and smile lines are present at correct weight (40-50% line weight)
- [ ] Miri's height is correctly shorter than Luma (3.2 heads vs. 3.5) — do not let them read as the same height
- [ ] Miri reads as the warmest, most grounded form in any shared composition
- [ ] The warm-family echo between Luma's orange and Miri's rust is visible and intentional in the composition

## 9. RECOMMENDED FIRST COMPOSITIONS (Production Notes)

### Cover/Title Card Composition (Trio)
**Framing:** Luma in center, mid-jump position. Cosmo on her left (viewer's right), in forward-lean running, notebook tucked. Byte on her right shoulder, pointing forward (his canonical position). All three facing the same direction — toward the right edge of frame (into the narrative). Behind them: a suggestion of the Glitch Layer portal opening (void black with cyan/magenta pixel confetti cascading).

**Why this works:** The composition shows their relationships (Byte on shoulder = trust/companionship; Cosmo beside not behind = partnership) and their direction (all forward = active series, not passive). The glitch elements behind them establish the show's visual world without overwhelming the characters.

### Three-Shot for Episodes (Medium/Reaction Shot)
**Framing:** Luma reacting with Reckless Excitement (Expression 1). Cosmo slightly behind her with Resigned Exasperation (Expression 5). Byte on her shoulder with Alarmed expression (Expression 6 — warning triangle on cracked eye). All looking at the same off-screen threat/opportunity.

**Why this works:** This is the show in one frame. Luma is excited. Cosmo is concerned. Byte is technically trying to warn everyone. They will proceed anyway. The audience knows all of this from the character designs, no dialogue required.

### Quiet Moment / Title Card Variant (Trio)
**Framing:** Low angle, small scene — all three sitting. Luma cross-legged on the ground, hoodie pixel pattern visible on crossed arms. Cosmo beside her, leaning against something (knee up, notebook closed in his lap). Byte between them or slightly above at floating height, pixel eye showing the dim star (Expression 8 — secretly satisfied). Warm lighting. Soft palette.

**Why this works:** Shows the audience that this is not only an action show. The warmth of the trio at rest is what earns emotional investment in the action. These characters can sit together without needing something to fight. That's rarer than it sounds.

### Den Scene with Miri (Home Base Establishing Shot)
**Framing:** Wide shot of Miri's den. Miri centered in the frame — seated in her chair or standing at her workbench, cardigan on, mug in hand. The den is warm and organized around her. The trio arrives in the background — they appear in the doorway or have just entered. Luma is mid-motion (partially paused by the warmth of the room). Cosmo is adjusting his glasses. Byte floats close to Luma.

**Why this works:** Establishes the den as a distinct visual world (warm, organized, layered), introduces Miri as someone who belongs in the scene in a way the trio doesn't — yet. The composition should communicate that Miri's stillness is the organizing center of the space, and the trio is orbiting it.

### Full Cast Character Lineup (Model Sheet Reference)
**Framing:** Characters in neutral front-view poses, left to right: Miri, Cosmo, Luma, Byte (on ground at Luma's feet, then Byte again on Luma's shoulder as second option). Height marks visible at each character's eye level and crown. Color chips beneath each character showing their primary palette.

**Why this works:** Establishes clear height, proportion, and color relationships for all production reference. The warm-cold-warm-digital temperature read of the lineup (Miri warm / Cosmo cool / Luma warm / Byte electric) communicates the show's color world in a single image.
