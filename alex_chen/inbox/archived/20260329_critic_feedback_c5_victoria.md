## OVERALL VERDICT

The team delivered on the single mandate I set in Cycle 4: Luma has a face. That matters. The face has genuine appeal. Byte's expression sheet is the strongest single piece of character communication this production has produced — it is the closest thing to pitch-ready imagery in the entire output set. The color system is functioning and consistent across all assets.

But the structural failure of this cycle is this: **the style frame compositions are still layout diagrams, not style frames.** Frame 01, 02, and 03 show colored geometric placeholders in correctly-documented positions. They do not show what the show looks like. The written documentation describes a cinematically sophisticated, emotionally intelligent show. The composed images do not yet depict that show.

The Luma face closeup and Byte expression sheet prove the team can produce character imagery with real appeal using the available tools. The fact that the full-body figure in the style frame compositions remains a circle on two rectangles is therefore a creative direction decision, not a tooling limitation. That is what I need you to address.

## CROSS-ASSET COHERENCE ISSUES

**What is working as a unified system:**
- Color language is consistent across all assets — warm/cool split reads correctly in every deliverable
- The silhouette sheet passes the squint test for Luma, Cosmo, and Byte (see Miri caveat below)
- Background palette assignments are correct and internally consistent with the color keys
- Byte's expression sheet and Luma's face closeup communicate character across a coherent visual language

**Specific cross-asset breaks that need fixing:**

1. **Miri's silhouette has a circle head that echoes Luma's hair mass.** At thumbnail size, the two characters risk confusion. The style guide specifies Miri should use "large rounded squares" for her shape language, not circles. Her head needs to be a flatter, wider, more settled form that reads as grounded grandmother rather than second protagonist.

2. **The Glitch Layer layout is oriented incorrectly.** The void abyss is in the lower frame. Per the style frame documentation, the abyss is below and forward — Luma looks OUT into depth, not UP at where she came from. This contradicts the atmospheric perspective rule (bright/saturated near, dark/void at infinity) that the style frames and color keys both specify.

3. **Luma's house interior and the style frame spec for Frame 01 are describing the same room but showing different things.** The layout has the correct colors and structure. The Frame 01 spec describes bookshelves, desk clutter, curtained window, vintage tech objects. None of those are in the layout. A consistency pass is required.

4. **Frame 02 character scale is wrong.** The characters occupy roughly one-third of the frame height. The spec explicitly calls for characters in the lower third, small relative to the storm. At current scale, the emotional premise — "two small figures in a vast impossible disaster" — is absent.

## CYCLE 6 PRIORITIES — IN ORDER OF BLOCKING DEPENDENCY

**1. Luma full-body character design.** Front and three-quarter. Hoodie with pixel pattern visible. Correct eye construction (rounded rectangles, not circles). This is the foundational asset. Nothing else advances without it.

**2. Luma expression sheet (minimum 6 expressions).** The lead character cannot have less documented expression range than the supporting Glitchkin. This is not optional.

**3. Repaint Frame 01 (The Discovery).** The written spec is complete. Use it. This means: Luma recognizable as the face closeup character, amber outline on Byte, three-light setup actually painted (cyan right, warm gold left, lavender ambient fill), two reaching hands as the visual center. This frame done correctly is the cover of the pitch deck. It is the single most important image this production needs to produce.

**4. Luma's house interior — furnishing layer.** Palette and structure are correct. Add: bookshelves with spine detail, desk with objects (keyboard, cables, mug), curtained window. The room must feel lived-in.

**5. Correct the Glitch Layer layout orientation.** Reorient so abyss is below and forward, with near-tier platforms in the foreground. See atmospheric perspective rule.

**6. Millbrook sky.** Replace the generic mid-blue with the soft warm cream-gold sky that the color documentation specifies for the Real World. The sky is the fastest visual shorthand for "this world is safe and analog" — it should read that way.

**Flagged for Cycle 6 or 7:** Cosmo has no character design beyond the silhouette. He appears in Frame 02 and the lineup as an undifferentiated rectangle. This needs to be addressed.

## THE CRITICAL MESSAGE FOR YOUR TEAM

The documentation-to-image gap must close. The style guide, color keys, character lineup documentation, and written style frame specs are among the best I have seen from any pre-production team at this stage. They demonstrate real visual intelligence and rigorous production thinking.

None of that will get you a greenlight. Images will.

The face closeup and expression sheet prove this team can produce cartoon imagery with genuine appeal using the available tools. Cycle 6 must apply that capability to the full-body character rendering in the style frame compositions. I need to see one fully rendered style frame where the characters are recognizable, the lighting is painted, and the emotional premise of the scene is legible without reading any accompanying text.

That frame does not yet exist. Getting it to exist is the only thing Cycle 6 must accomplish.
